# Guidance — DEL-007-03 Planting & Restoration Plans

## Purpose

DEL-007-03 exists to provide the botanical and ecological framework for landscape implementation of the WDMLRL Maintenance Shop Addition expansion area. The planting and restoration plans translate the approved site layout (DEL-007-02) and civil grading (PKG-005) into a plantable, restorable landscape that:

- Stabilizes disturbed soils in the expansion area following construction (ASSUMPTION: erosion/stabilization need inferred from site condition — active landfill compound with disturbed gravel/dirt yard, visible in R-07 Appendix C aerial).
- Integrates the new building addition within the broader landfill site context through appropriate vegetation.
- Establishes native vegetation where feasible, consistent with restoration best practice for central Alberta.
- Provides the plant callout and scheduling framework referenced by the Landscape Specification (DEL-007-04).

Source: _CONTEXT.md; DECOMP §7 PKG-007; R-01 §1.0, §3.3.2.

## Principles

### P-1: Site-Appropriate Plant Selection
Plant selection shall prioritize species that are adapted to central Alberta's climate (semi-arid continental), the disturbed nature of the site (active landfill compound), and the specific microclimatic and soil conditions of the expansion area. Native and naturalized species appropriate to the region are preferred for restoration areas.
Source: _CONTEXT.md ("native vegetation establishment"); _REFERENCES.md (native plant selection and restoration guidelines noted). Specific species guidance TBD pending Landscape Architect assessment and site ecological survey.

### P-2: Integration with Civil Grading
Planting zones must be designed in coordination with the civil grading plan (PKG-005). Planting locations shall not impede positive drainage features. Restoration areas shall respect the grading constraint that drainage conditions of neighboring properties must not be altered.
Source: R-01 §3.4 (SOW-0020, SOW-0021); DECOMP §6 PKG-007 exclusions (civil grading is PKG-005 scope).

### P-3: Phased Establishment
Given the construction timeline (all Work complete by December 31, 2026, per R-01 §3.1), planting and restoration shall be sequenced to account for construction staging, seasonal planting windows in Alberta, and the practical need for initial soil stabilization before full vegetation establishment.
Source: R-01 §3.1 (completion deadline); _CONTEXT.md ("phasing for native vegetation establishment"). Specific phasing details TBD.

### P-4: Downstream Specification Alignment
Plant codes, zone designations, and planting notations on the drawing set must be internally consistent and directly referenced by DEL-007-04 (Landscape Specification). The drawing set is the primary spatial document; the specification is the normative quality document.
Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md (downstream dependency: DEL-007-04).

### P-5: County Approval Gate
The preliminary landscape design (DEL-007-01) must be approved by the County project team before the planting plans proceed to IFC status. This ensures alignment with the County's landscape vision before detailed design effort is committed.
Source: R-01 §3.3.2 ("Deliver Preliminary Design for the County project team to approve"); DECOMP DEL-007-01.

### P-6: Design-Build Authority for Plant Selection
Under the CCDC 14-2013 Design-Build Stipulated Price Contract (R-01 §2.7), the proponent holds design responsibility. However, the County approval gate (P-5) for preliminary landscape design introduces a constraint on the proponent's latitude to finalize plant species and quantities. The Landscape Architect should clarify: (a) whether the proponent has full authority to set the plant palette once preliminary design is approved, or (b) whether County input continues to constrain species selection beyond the preliminary design gate. This affects the sequencing and finality of plant palette development (REQ-007-03-005).
Source: R-01 §2.7 (CCDC 14-2013); R-01 §3.3.2 (County approval of preliminary design); ASSUMPTION: design-build delivery model gives proponent design latitude subject to County approval at defined gates. (_SEMANTIC_LENSING.md D-001)

### Rationale for Assumed Requirements (REQ-007-03-006 and REQ-007-03-008)
REQ-007-03-006 (Plant Schedule) and REQ-007-03-008 (Planting Details) are sourced as "ASSUMPTION based on standard drawing set conventions" because the RFP does not explicitly require a plant schedule or planting detail sheets. These requirements are included because:
- A drawing set of type "Planting & Restoration Plans" conventionally includes a plant schedule and planting details as standard components needed for construction implementation.
- The downstream consumer (DEL-007-04 Landscape Specification) references planting callouts and plant details, implying these artifacts exist in the drawing set.
- Without a plant schedule and planting details, the drawing set would be incomplete for its stated purpose of guiding landscape installation.

These requirements form part of the governance basis for the deliverable but are self-declared assumptions. If the RFP or project team confirms or denies their necessity, the requirements should be updated accordingly. (Source: _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md C-001)

## Considerations

### Site Context — Active Landfill Compound
The site is an operating regional landfill (Camrose West Dried Meat Lake, SW 14-44-21-W4, Ferintosh, Alberta). The expansion area (visible in R-07 Appendix C aerial, page 2) is a disturbed yard immediately east of the existing maintenance shop buildings. Practical considerations include:
- Heavy equipment traffic — planting zones in vehicular areas will not be feasible; focus on non-trafficked margins.
- Soil quality — landfill compound soils may require amendment or importation for planting; ASSUMPTION pending site assessment.
- Dust and maintenance — low-maintenance, hardy species are likely most practical given the operational context.

### Alberta Seasonal Constraints
Central Alberta's growing season is approximately late May to mid-September (ASSUMPTION: typical for the region; specific USDA/Canadian hardiness zone data TBD). Planting windows need to align with construction sequencing. If construction completion is late 2026, some restoration seeding may need to be scheduled for spring 2027 establishment — this should be addressed in the phasing plan and discussed with the County.

### Native Tree Line Interface
Appendix C aerial imagery (R-07, page 2) shows a mature native tree line immediately north of the expansion area (likely mixed coniferous/deciduous). The planting and restoration design should consider:
- Buffer/interface treatment between the disturbed expansion area and the existing tree line.
- Risk of disturbance to the tree line root zone during construction — coordination with construction team recommended (ASSUMPTION: construction limits to be confirmed from DEL-007-02 site plans).
- Potential for seeding native understory species in the transition zone.

### Landfill Regulatory Context
The site is an active landfill. Environmental and regulatory constraints on vegetation within a landfill zone (e.g., restrictions on deep-rooted species near waste cells, leachate/gas considerations) should be assessed by the Landscape Architect in consultation with the project team. No specific landfill-specific planting restrictions are stated in the RFP; ASSUMPTION: expansion area is outside active fill zone; confirm with County.

## Trade-offs

### Native Species vs. Establishment Reliability
Native species are ecologically preferred for restoration but may have lower availability, higher cost, and slower establishment than introduced horticultural species. The Landscape Architect should advise on the appropriate balance given the site's operational context, budget, and the 2-year guarantee period obligations.

### Seeding vs. Sodding/Planting
Seeding is typically lower cost but slower to establish; sodding and containerized planting provide faster results but at higher cost. Given the December 31, 2026 completion deadline and Alberta's limited growing season, the phasing plan should clearly identify which restoration areas are suitable for direct seeding vs. those requiring immediate cover.

### Restoration Depth vs. Project Cost
A highly detailed restoration plan (multi-year phasing, monitoring protocols) is ecologically best practice but may be outside the scope implied by the RFP's brief reference to landscape design. The Landscape Architect should scope the restoration plan proportionately to the RFP requirements and the scale of disturbed area.

**Budget context:** No landscape budget allocation or cost target is identified in the accessible project documents. The RFP (R-01) does not specify a landscape budget envelope. Without a defined budget constraint, the trade-off between restoration depth and project cost cannot be fully assessed. TBD — project team to provide landscape budget allocation or cost guidance so the Landscape Architect can calibrate restoration scope appropriately. (Source: _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md D-003)
