# Guidance — DEL-007-04 Landscape Specification

---

## Purpose

This document provides directional guidance for the Landscape Architect developing DEL-007-04 (Landscape Specification). It explains the context and rationale behind the specification requirements, identifies key decision areas, and flags considerations and trade-offs that will require professional judgment or human ruling.

DEL-007-04 is the technical and performance framework for landscape construction at the West Dried Meat Lake Regional Landfill Maintenance Shop Addition (AB-2026-01424, Camrose County). Its primary role is to establish material standards, workmanship requirements, and quality criteria that ensure the landscape work complies with the RFP and supports the County's operational intent.
_Source: _CONTEXT.md; R-01 §1.0_

---

## Principles

**1. Specification content follows approved design.** This specification must be coordinated with and grounded in DEL-007-02 (Landscape Site Plans) and DEL-007-03 (Planting & Restoration Plans). Writing the specification before those upstream deliverables are approved introduces risk of misalignment. Where upstream deliverables are not yet available, this document uses TBD placeholders.
_Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md; _CONTEXT.md_

**2. Industrial function drives landscape decisions.** The site is an active regional landfill maintenance yard serving heavy equipment of motor scraper class. All landscape and hardscape specifications must prioritize durability, drainage performance, and compatibility with heavy vehicle operations.
_Source: R-01 §3.3.2; App B (Shop)_

**3. County scope boundaries are fixed.** Brushing and clearing, bulk cut and fill, and aggregate (gravel) supply are County responsibilities explicitly excluded from the Proponent's scope. The specification must not impose requirements on County-supplied items, but must define the interface conditions (e.g., subgrade preparation requirements for gravel surfaces that will receive aggregate from the County/Landfill).
_Source: R-01 §3.3.1_

**Terminology Note (aggregate vs. gravel):** The RFP uses both "gravel" and "aggregate" when referring to County-supplied driving surface material. To avoid procurement and construction communication confusion, this document set adopts the following convention: "aggregate" refers to the raw material supplied by County/Landfill; "gravel" refers to the finished driving surface. Where context requires specificity, both terms may appear together as "aggregate (gravel)." The Landscape Architect should confirm the preferred term and update all four documents accordingly.
_Lensing note [B-004]: Normalizing "aggregate" vs. "gravel" usage across Datasheet, Specification, and Guidance._

**Terminology Note (motor scraper class):** The RFP uses "large construction equipment" and "motor scraper class" variably (Datasheet uses parenthetical phrasing; Specification uses direct phrasing). The term "motor scraper class" is not explicitly defined in available sources. TBD: The Civil Engineer should define design vehicle parameters for "motor scraper class" (e.g., typical axle load, gross vehicle weight, minimum turning radius) to enable quantitative driving surface design. Until defined, "motor scraper class" should be understood as a placeholder for heavy earthmoving equipment capable of hauling and grading operations.
_Lensing note [E-002]: Normalizing equipment class terminology and identifying the need for explicit design vehicle definition._

**4. Civil grading is the primary interface.** The landscape specification's most significant coordination requirement is with PKG-005 Civil Design. Grading design and drainage intent established in DEL-005-02 and DEL-005-03 must be reflected as performance constraints in this specification.
_Source: R-01 §3.4; Decomp PKG-007_

**5. Source fidelity over assumption.** Where the RFP and conceptual drawings do not provide sufficient detail (e.g., planting species, soil depth, restoration methods), the specification must use TBD and not invent requirements. The Landscape Architect's professional judgment fills these gaps at the appropriate design stage.

## Considerations

### Site Context
The expansion area is located within an active landfill facility on exposed, disturbed soil with a gravel/earth character. The site map (Appendix C) shows forested vegetation to the north and open gravel/earth to the south and east. The landscape specification should account for:
- Cold Alberta climate (central region, approximately 30 km south of Camrose)
- Disturbed, potentially contaminated or compacted subsoils typical of landfill environs (ASSUMPTION -- confirmed site characterization depends on geotechnical report from DEL-008-01)
- Minimal ornamental landscape expectation -- functional restoration and erosion control are likely the primary objectives
_Source: R-01 §1.0; R-07 (Appendix C)_

### Geotechnical Dependency Rationale
The geotechnical investigation report (DEL-008-01) from PKG-008 is listed as Procedure prerequisite P5 because subsoil conditions directly influence multiple landscape specification decisions:
- **Pad design**: Bearing capacity data from DEL-008-01 determines cement pad thickness, reinforcement, and subbase requirements. Without geotechnical data, structural pad specifications cannot be finalized.
- **Planting soil**: Subsoil characterization (pH, contamination, compaction) determines whether imported topsoil or soil amendments are needed for planting areas, and what depth of soil preparation is appropriate.
- **Grading**: Subgrade conditions affect gravel driving surface compaction specifications and drainage design assumptions (permeability, frost heave potential).

TBD: Specific geotechnical parameters needed for landscape specification decisions will be defined when DEL-008-01 is available.

### Gravel Driving Surface
The RFP explicitly states the final driving surface shall be gravel, designed for motor scraper class equipment. The Landscape Architect should specify:
- Subgrade preparation requirements before aggregate placement
- Compaction standards for subgrade
- Depth and gradation of gravel surfacing (TBD — aggregate specifications to be coordinated with Civil Engineer and County/Landfill supply)
- Transition zones between paved/cement pads and gravel surfaces
_Source: R-01 §3.3.2_

### Cement and Gravel Pads
Appendix B shows cement pads adjacent to the building and a gravel pad to the northwest of the New North Shop. Specific dimensions are not stated in available sources beyond the overall site dimensions (235' total width, 130' new shop, 105' old shop). The Landscape Architect must define:
- Cement pad thickness, reinforcement, and finish (TBD — coordinate with structural/civil)
- Edge conditions and transitions
- Gravel pad depth and compaction
_Source: R-01 App B (Shop)_

### Drainage Integration
The civil grading requirements (positive drainage, no impact to neighboring properties) drive landscape grading. The Landscape Architect should confirm:
- Swale locations and specifications as generated by PKG-005 civil design
- Infiltration capacity assumptions for the site
- Any catchment or detention features that interact with landscape areas
_Source: R-01 §3.4_

### Drainage Interface Governance
The PKG-005 (Civil Design) / PKG-007 (Landscape Architectural Design) drainage interface requires clear governance to manage day-to-day design coordination. The following governance framing is recommended (TBD -- confirmation required from Project Manager):
- **Drainage design standard ownership**: PKG-005 Civil Engineer produces the drainage design standard and site grading plan; PKG-007 Landscape Architect integrates landscape treatment (swale seeding, erosion control) with the civil drainage design.
- **Conformance verification**: PKG-005 verifies that landscape grading conforms to drainage design intent; PKG-007 verifies that landscape treatments achieve the specified erosion control and vegetation performance.
- **Discrepancy resolution**: Where landscape grading requirements conflict with civil drainage requirements, the Project Manager shall convene a coordination meeting to resolve. Pending resolution, the drainage design standard (PKG-005) governs.

This governance framing supports resolution of CONF-007-04-02 (see Conflict Table below).

### Planting and Restoration
No planting schedule, species list, or restoration standard is specified in the RFP. This is typical for early-stage design-build RFPs where the County has a concept but not a detailed landscape program. The Landscape Architect should:
- Establish appropriate native or adapted species suited to central Alberta conditions
- Define restoration standards for disturbed areas not receiving hardscape
- Address establishment requirements (watering, maintenance period) appropriate to site conditions
- Consider the landfill context — species tolerant of disturbed soils and potential gas seepage at edges (ASSUMPTION — confirm with site geotechnical and environmental data)
_Source: _CONTEXT.md (description); RFP does not specify — Landscape Architect professional judgment required_

### Mud Sump
The exterior mud sump serves an operational function — it receives effluent from the wash bay and must be cleanable by excavator. The specification should address:
- Sump dimensions sufficient for excavator bucket access (TBD — coordinate with wash bay drainage designer in PKG-006 Plumbing)
- Material (concrete, liner, or other) — TBD
- Overflow and drainage provisions consistent with local environmental requirements (ASSUMPTION — Alberta environmental regulations apply; specific requirements TBD)
_Source: R-01 §3.4; App B (Shop)_

### Warranty
The 2-year post-CCC warranty is a hard contractual requirement. For landscape work, the Landscape Architect should specify:
- Plant establishment criteria at end of establishment period
- What constitutes a warrantable failure for planting, grading, and hardscape
- Owner inspection protocols triggering warranty rectification
_Source: R-01 §2.10_

### Defect Rectification Timeline Rationale
REQ-007-04-061 requires rectification of defects "within ten (10) days of written instruction" per RFP §2.10.3/§2.10.6. For landscape-specific defects, the 10-day timeline may present practical challenges:
- **Planting replacement**: Plant replacement is often constrained by seasonal planting windows in central Alberta (typically spring and early fall). A 10-day rectification period may be impractical if the defect notice is issued outside the planting season.
- **Gravel and hardscape defects**: 10 days is generally achievable for gravel surface repairs and hardscape defect rectification.

The Landscape Architect should consider whether the specification needs to distinguish between defect types with different rectification timelines, or whether a seasonal exception clause is warranted for planting defects. The contractual 10-day requirement (R-01 §2.10.3) is a hard obligation; any modification would require Owner agreement.
_Source: Specification.md > REQ-007-04-061; R-01 §2.10.3, §2.10.6_
_Lensing note [D-004]: Rationale gap -- seasonal planting constraints may make 10-day rectification impractical for certain landscape defects._

### County Review Sequencing Rationale
Procedure Step 7 states that County approval of DEL-007-01 (Preliminary Landscape Design) must be on file before the specification is issued for construction, and Step 7.2 requires incorporating "any County-directed changes." The following rationale and risk should be understood:
- **Why DEL-007-01 must be approved first**: The Preliminary Landscape Design establishes the design intent and concept that the County has endorsed. Issuing the specification before this approval risks producing a specification that does not reflect County expectations, leading to rework.
- **Conflict risk with upstream deliverables**: If the County directs changes during DEL-007-01 review that conflict with already-approved DEL-007-02 (Landscape Site Plans) or DEL-007-03 (Planting & Restoration Plans), those upstream deliverables may require revision. TBD: Define a protocol for managing conflicts between County-directed changes and approved upstream deliverables. Resolution source: Project Manager.
