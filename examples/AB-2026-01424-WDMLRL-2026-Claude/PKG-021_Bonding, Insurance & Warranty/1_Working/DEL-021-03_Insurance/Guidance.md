## Purpose

DEL-021-03 exists to satisfy the County's requirement that the Proponent carry comprehensive insurance coverage as a condition of the CCDC 14–2013 Design-Build Contract. Insurance is a pre-construction contractual obligation: without it, the contract cannot be executed and the Work cannot commence.

From the project perspective (Proponent as Design-Builder), insurance serves two related purposes:

1. **Risk transfer and protection** — Transfers financial exposure for third-party bodily injury, property damage, professional errors, and worker injuries from the Proponent to insurers, limiting the Proponent's direct liability exposure during design and construction.

2. **Owner protection** — Ensures Camrose County and its designated parties are covered as additional insureds, giving the County a direct claim against the Proponent's insurers in the event of a loss arising from the Work.

**Source:** RFP §4.2; Decomposition OBJ-008; _CONTEXT.md Scope Definition.

## Principles

### 1. Insurance is a Condition Precedent to Contract Execution

The RFP states insurance is a condition of the Contract (§4.2 preamble). This means insurance documentation must be confirmed by the County before the Contract is signed and before any Work begins. Do not defer insurance procurement to post-award. Engage the insurance broker immediately upon receiving award notification, in parallel with the bonding process.

**Source:** RFP §4.2 (preamble); _DEPENDENCIES.md (DEL-021-04 constraint).

### 2. Engage a Qualified Commercial Broker Early

Commercial construction insurance of this type — including CGL, automobile, professional liability, employer's liability, and WCB — requires a broker experienced with Alberta construction projects and CCDC contract requirements. Early engagement avoids last-minute compliance gaps. Broker selection criteria should consider experience with Alberta construction, CCDC contracts, and landfill-adjacent projects (see Procedure prerequisites for operational criteria — [X-001]).

**Source:** ASSUMPTION (best-effort — standard industry practice for CCDC design-build contracts; not explicitly stated in RFP §4.2).

### 3. Coordinate Insurance and Bonding as a Single Program

DEL-021-02 (Performance Bonds) and DEL-021-03 (Insurance) are complementary risk instruments that are often sourced from different providers (surety company vs. insurer) but must be coordinated. Coverage gaps or overlaps between bonding and insurance can create liability exposure or policy disputes. The Project Manager / Finance lead should review both programs together.

**Source:** _DEPENDENCIES.md (DEL-021-02 internal dependency note); Decomposition OBJ-008.

### 4. Name the County Correctly and Completely

RFP §4.2.6 requires Camrose County (and any other County-designated parties) to be named as additional insured on all policies except E&O. The exact legal name of the County must appear on all endorsements. Confirm the required name(s) with the County at contract negotiation.

**Source:** RFP §4.2.6.

### 5. Preserve the 30-Day Cancellation Clause

Policy cancellations without adequate notice expose the County to uninsured risk. The 30-day cancellation notice requirement (§4.2.6) must be confirmed as a policy condition at time of placement, not added retroactively. Verify this clause exists on each policy before submitting certificates to the County.

**Source:** RFP §4.2.6.

### 6. Maintain Coverage Throughout the Project Duration

Insurance is not a one-time procurement. Policies may have annual renewal dates that fall during the project. The Project Manager / Finance role is responsible for tracking policy renewal dates and ensuring continuous coverage. Expired certificates must be replaced before coverage lapses. Mid-project policy amendments, insurer changes, or coverage modifications should be documented through an audit log mechanism (see Procedure — [A-004]).

**Source:** _CONTEXT.md (Success Criteria — "Insurance maintained throughout project duration").

## Considerations

### Subcontractor Insurance

RFP §4.2.1.2 requires that the Proponent's automobile / CGL policy include coverage for independent subcontractors as a sub-coverage. However, the RFP does not explicitly state whether subcontractors must carry their own separate insurance policies.

ASSUMPTION: It is standard Alberta commercial construction practice to require subcontractors to carry their own CGL and WCB coverage and to provide certificates of insurance and additional insured endorsements to the general contractor / design-builder. The Proponent should confirm this requirement with the County and/or legal counsel at contract negotiation, and establish a subcontractor insurance compliance checklist.

**Source:** RFP §4.2.1.2 (sub-coverage); ASSUMPTION for subcontractor flow-down (location TBD in CCDC 14–2013).

### Professional Liability (E&O) Scope and Tail Coverage

The E&O policy covers design errors and omissions. In a Design-Build contract, the Proponent carries design responsibility, making E&O coverage particularly important. Unlike other policies, E&O coverage typically applies on a "claims-made" basis, meaning the policy in force at the time a claim is made (rather than when the error occurred) is the responding policy. This has implications for coverage continuity after project completion.

**[C-002] Tail Coverage / Extended Reporting Period:** The Proponent should discuss tail coverage / extended reporting period with their broker given the design-build scope and the 2-year guarantee period (RFP §2.10). If a claim for a design error is made after project completion but within the guarantee period, the E&O policy in force at that time must respond. If the Proponent's E&O policy is not renewed after project completion, a tail coverage endorsement (extended reporting period) would be needed to preserve coverage. **The consequence of not obtaining tail coverage is that a valid claim arising during the 2-year guarantee period could be uninsured if the base E&O policy has lapsed.** Whether tail coverage is recommended (best practice) or required (contractual obligation) is TBD pending review of CCDC 14-2013 provisions. At minimum, it is a prudent risk management measure given the design-build delivery model.

**Source:** RFP §4.2.3; RFP §2.10 (guarantee period); ASSUMPTION for tail coverage recommendation (CCDC 14-2013 may contain relevant provisions — not accessible).

### Environmental Liability Sub-Coverage

The Proponent's CGL policy must include environmental liability (§4.2.1.5). This is relevant given that the project site is at a regional landfill (West Dried Meat Lake Regional Landfill). Contractors working near or on a landfill site face elevated environmental liability exposure. Confirm with the broker that the environmental liability sub-coverage is adequate for landfill-adjacent construction.

**Source:** RFP §4.2.1.5; RFP §1.0 (project location — West Dried Meat Lake Regional Landfill).

### WCB and Employer's Liability Relationship

WCB coverage (§4.2.2) and Employer's Liability (§4.2.4) serve related but distinct purposes. WCB is a statutory scheme under Alberta law; employer's liability is a common-law coverage. In Alberta, WCB typically provides the exclusive remedy for worker injuries, but employer's liability coverage addresses claims outside the WCB system (e.g., claims by workers not covered by WCB, or by family members). Confirm with the broker that both coverages are correctly structured under Alberta law.

**Source:** RFP §4.2.2, §4.2.4; ASSUMPTION for Alberta-specific WCB / employer's liability relationship (location TBD in Workers' Compensation Act, RSA 2000).

### [D-002] Risk-Based Coverage Optimization Guidance

Meeting RFP §4.2 minimums achieves contractual compliance but does not necessarily achieve risk-optimized coverage. For this project, the following risk factors are relevant to coverage optimization:

- **Landfill-adjacent site** — Elevated environmental liability exposure (§4.2.1.5 sub-coverage). Standard CGL environmental liability endorsements may contain exclusions for pre-existing contamination or gradual pollution. The broker should confirm that the environmental liability sub-coverage is adequate for landfill-adjacent construction, or whether standalone environmental impairment liability (EIL) coverage should be considered.
- **Design-build delivery model** — The Proponent carries both design and construction liability. E&O coverage adequacy and tail coverage duration should be evaluated against the 2-year guarantee period.
- **$5M combined limit** — The RFP specifies $5M inclusive for primary and re-insurance. Whether this is adequate for a landfill-adjacent design-build project is a judgment call that depends on the Proponent's overall risk tolerance and portfolio.

**Framework for evaluation:** ASSUMPTION — The broker should be asked to provide a risk-adequacy opinion comparing the RFP-mandated coverages against standard market practice for landfill-adjacent Alberta construction projects. This opinion would support a human decision on whether to procure additional coverage beyond RFP minimums.

**Source:** RFP §4.2; RFP §1.0 (project location); RFP §2.10 (guarantee period); ASSUMPTION for optimization framework.
