# Guidance — DEL-021-04: CCDC 14-2013 Contract Execution

---

## Purpose

DEL-021-04 is the commercial gateway deliverable for the WDMLRL project. Executing the CCDC 14-2013 Design-Build Stipulated Price Contract is the formal act that transitions the project from the procurement phase into the construction phase. Until this contract is executed, no construction work may legally begin under the RFP framework.

This deliverable simultaneously:
- Establishes the legal and financial framework for the entire project (OBJ-006: Deliver the project within the negotiated stipulated contract price)
- Satisfies the final pre-construction commercial obligation within PKG-021 (OBJ-008: Satisfy all bonding, insurance, and warranty obligations)
- Unlocks downstream activities in PKG-019 (Construction Management & OH&S) and PKG-020 (Commissioning & Closeout)

Source: _CONTEXT.md (Scope Definition, Linked Objectives); Decomposition PKG-021 description; RFP §2.7

---

## Principles

### 1. Nothing starts until the contract is signed
The RFP is unambiguous: the successful Proponent must sign the CCDC 14-2013 contract prior to commencing work (RFP §2.7). Mobilization planning, procurement of long-lead items, and any site activity must all wait for an executed contract. The Project Manager must treat contract execution as the absolute predecessor to all field activity.

### 2. CCDC 14-2013 is a standard form — preserve its structure
CCDC 14-2013 is a widely recognized standard in Canadian construction. The Owner has specified this form because it provides a balanced, tested allocation of risk between Owner and Design-Builder. Modifications to standard CCDC forms require careful legal review. ASSUMPTION: Any proposed modifications to the standard form would require Owner and Proponent legal counsel review before execution. Specific negotiated deviations from standard conditions are TBD. Any such deviations must be documented as Supplementary Conditions (see Specification REQ-021-04-26).

### 3. Bonds and insurance are conditions of the contract, not post-execution formalities
The RFP language in §2.12.3 requires bonding "with the executed Contract Agreement" within 10 days of award notice. Insurance must be in place "as a condition of the Contract" per §4.2 preamble. The Project Manager should treat DEL-021-02 and DEL-021-03 as concurrent prerequisites that must be confirmed before or simultaneously with contract signing, not as items that follow.

### 4. The foundation price is deliberately open at execution
The RFP explicitly separates firm scope (stipulated price) from foundation scope (variable, negotiated post-geotech per §4.8.2). This is not a gap — it is a deliberate risk-sharing mechanism acknowledged in OBJ-006. The executed contract must accommodate this variable element from the outset, and the Project Manager should ensure the contract structure and price schedule reflect this two-tier pricing arrangement clearly.

### 5. The contract is the project control document
Once executed, the CCDC 14-2013 contract governs all project decisions including scope changes, payment claims, holdback release, and guarantee obligations. Contract administration procedures established at this stage will be the operational framework for the entire project duration through to CCC and the 2-year guarantee period.

Source: RFP §2.7, §2.10, §2.11, §2.12, §2.13, §4.2, §4.8; Decomposition OBJ-006, OBJ-008

### Lien Holdback Management
Ten percent (10%) of every payment claim will be withheld under the Prompt Payment and Construction Lien Act (Alberta). This has direct cash-flow implications for the Design-Builder. The fee payment schedule in the contract should be structured with this holdback in mind, and release conditions per RFP §2.13.2 should be clearly understood by the Project Manager and Finance team. Source: RFP §2.13.2

### Deficiency Holdback vs. Lien Holdback
These are distinct holdback mechanisms. The lien holdback (10%) is statutory and applies throughout construction per the Prompt Payment and Construction Lien Act (Alberta). The Deficiency Holdback (RFP §2.11) is a separate Owner contractual entitlement to retain the estimated value of outstanding deficiencies. Both may apply simultaneously near project completion and during the guarantee period. The Datasheet Attributes table records these as separate entries. Source: RFP §2.11, §2.13.2

### Handoff to DEL-021-05 (Warranty Obligations)
The guarantee period terms are established in this contract (REQ-021-04-21: construction period plus 2 years post-CCC; REQ-021-04-22: 10-day rectification). However, ongoing administration of guarantee period obligations — including defect identification, rectification tracking, and guarantee period closeout — transitions to DEL-021-05.

The handoff occurs at the point of Construction Completion Certificate (CCC) issuance. At CCC:
- The 2-year guarantee clock begins (RFP §2.10.2)
- Deficiency rectification obligations under the guarantee become active
- DEL-021-05 takes over tracking and administration

### CCDC 14-2013 General Conditions — Enforceability Context
The CCDC 14-2013 standard form establishes general conditions that create enforceable obligations for both Owner and Design-Builder. Key general conditions areas expected to be operative include:

- **Change order process** — governs how scope changes are initiated, priced, and approved
- **Dispute resolution** — establishes the mechanism for resolving disagreements (typically mediation, then arbitration or litigation)
- **Termination provisions** — governs conditions under which either party may terminate the contract
- **Design-Builder responsibilities** — defines the scope of design and construction obligations
- **Owner responsibilities** — defines payment obligations, site access, and information provision

**ASSUMPTION (C-001):** This list is based on general knowledge of CCDC standard forms. Until the standard form is obtained, downstream teams cannot fully assess the enforceability of specific contractual obligations beyond what is stated in the RFP.

## Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Description | Consideration |
|---|---|---|
| Speed to execute vs. completeness of package | Pressure to execute quickly after award to enable mobilization; risk of executing before all bonds/insurance are confirmed | Bonds and insurance are conditions of contract (RFP §2.12.3, §4.2); executing without them creates contractual risk. Sequence: confirm bonds and insurance first, then sign. |
| Firm price vs. flexibility | Firm price provides budget certainty for the County and risk for the Design-Builder | Foundation variable pricing is the explicit relief valve (RFP §4.8.2). All other scope must be absorbed at firm price once signed. |
| Standard form vs. negotiated terms | CCDC 14-2013 is a standard form; the County may have preferred modifications | ASSUMPTION: Any deviations require mutual agreement and legal review. Preference is to minimize modifications to preserve the standard allocation of risk. Document outcome in Supplementary Conditions (REQ-021-04-26). |
| Holdback timing | 10% statutory lien holdback plus potential deficiency holdback delays Design-Builder cash flow | Plan payment schedule to account for both holdback types and structure milestone claims accordingly. Ensure contract administration procedures address both holdback mechanisms separately. |

## Examples

CCDC 14-2013 is a published standard contract form issued by the Canadian Construction Documents Committee. It is widely used across Canadian design-build projects. The general structure includes:

- Agreement (parties, project description, contract price, contract documents)
- General Conditions (design-builder obligations, owner obligations, payment, changes, disputes, termination)
- Supplementary Conditions (project-specific modifications to general conditions)
- Design-Builder's Responsibilities schedule
- Appendices (contract documents list, design documents, pricing)
