## Terminology Note

The RFP uses the term "guarantee period" (RFP §2.10) while common industry usage and several sections of these production documents use "warranty period." These terms are synonymous in the context of this deliverable. The authoritative term from the RFP is "guarantee period"; "warranty period" is used interchangeably throughout these documents for readability. Where precision is required for audit or contractual purposes, prefer "guarantee period" as the governing term. (Source: RFP §2.10 heading; Semantic Lensing item E-002)

## Purpose

DEL-021-05 exists to ensure that Camrose County (Owner) receives the full benefit of the two-year guarantee period to which it is entitled under the RFP and the executed CCDC 14-2013 contract. From the Proponent/Contractor's perspective, this deliverable represents the active management of the guarantee obligations made at time of contracting — obligations that survive construction completion and cannot be discharged by the CCC or final payment.

The warranty period is structurally the final phase of the project's commercial lifecycle. All bonding, insurance, and contractual obligations converge here. The Project Manager's role is to ensure that defects are caught, documented, communicated, and remediated within the contractually-required timeframes, and that the Owner's financial protection mechanisms (Deficiency Holdback) are properly administered.

**Source:** OBJ-008 (Decomposition §5): "Satisfy all bonding, insurance, and warranty obligations through the 2-year guarantee period post-CCC."

## Principles

### P-1: The CCC Does Not Discharge the Guarantee

The Construction Completion Certificate and final progress payment do not release the Contractor from responsibility for faulty materials or defective workmanship. (RFP §2.10.1) The guarantee period persists for two years post-CCC regardless of payment status. The Project Manager must treat the CCC as the start of an active administrative period, not the end of obligations.

### P-2: All Guarantee Work Costs Are Borne by the Contractor

All costs of performing guarantee work — whether done by the Contractor, a representative, or the Owner itself — are the Contractor's financial responsibility. (RFP §2.10.7) This principle must be communicated clearly in the deficiency notification process so the Owner can confidently direct remediation without concern about cost exposure.

### P-3: Written Communication Is the Trigger

The 10-day rectification clock starts upon the Owner's **written instruction**. (RFP §2.10.6) Verbal observations and informal communications do not start the clock. The Project Manager must ensure all deficiency notifications are issued in writing and date-stamped to establish the compliance deadline. Initial notification may be by any available means, but must be confirmed in writing. (RFP §2.10.4)

### P-4: The Owner's Liability Exposure Is Protected Throughout

The guarantee article does not restrict the Contractor's liability in any way. (RFP §2.10.9) The Contractor is liable not only for the cost of remediation but for all expenses, losses, and damages incurred by the Owner as a result of faulty materials or workmanship. (RFP §2.10.8) The Project Manager should document any consequential damages in addition to direct remediation costs.

**Consequential Damages Documentation (Semantic Lensing item D-002):** RFP §2.10.8 establishes the Contractor's liability for "all expenses, losses, or damages" resulting from faulty materials or workmanship. Consequential damages may include but are not limited to: temporary facility costs, operational downtime losses, costs of alternative arrangements during remediation, professional fees for deficiency assessment, and administrative costs of managing the deficiency. The Project Manager should:
- Maintain a separate consequential damages record for each deficiency where damages extend beyond direct remediation cost.
- Document the causal link between the deficiency and each consequential expense.
- Retain supporting evidence (invoices, quotes, operational records) for cost recovery.
- Include consequential damages in the overall cost recovery from the Contractor alongside direct remediation costs.

ASSUMPTION: Specific consequential damage categories and documentation standards are TBD and should be informed by the executed CCDC 14-2013 contract terms (DEL-021-04) and, if needed, legal counsel. (Source: RFP §2.10.8; Guidance P-4; Semantic Lensing item D-002)

### P-5: The Deficiency Holdback Is a Financial Safety Net, Not a Penalty

The Deficiency Holdback exists to protect the Owner's ability to get deficiencies corrected. (RFP §2.11) It is not punitive — if the Contractor corrects all deficiencies, the full remaining holdback is returned to the Contractor. (RFP §2.11.4) The Project Manager should manage the holdback transparently, updating the ledger as deficiencies are resolved, to facilitate orderly release.

## Considerations

### C-1: Warranty Period Start Date Must Be Established Immediately at CCC

The warranty period start date is the CCC date. (RFP §2.10.2) The Project Manager must record this date formally and calculate the expiry date (CCC + 2 years) at the time the CCC is issued. This anchors all subsequent administration. Without a clear start date, the 10-day rectification obligation is difficult to enforce, and holdback release timing is unclear.

**Action implication:** Upon CCC issuance (DEL-020-02), immediately create the Warranty Period Register.

### C-2: Inspection Cadence During Warranty Period Is Not Prescribed

The RFP specifies that defects discovered through "use, tests, or inspection" are covered. (RFP §2.10.4) However, the RFP does not prescribe a specific inspection schedule during the warranty period. ASSUMPTION: A proactive inspection schedule (e.g., at 6 months, 12 months, and near the 24-month expiry) is advisable practice to identify latent defects before they become larger problems and to manage the warranty period end efficiently. The specific schedule is TBD and should be established at the time of CCC.

### C-3: 10-Day Window Is Short — Contractor Must Be Reachable

The 10-day rectification window is tight for construction deficiencies, particularly for a facility in a rural location (SW-14-44-21-W4, near Ferintosh). The Project Manager should ensure Contractor contact information (key personnel, emergency contacts) is captured and kept current from contract execution through the full warranty period. ASSUMPTION: Contact information will be established as part of warranty period initiation procedures.

### C-4: Emergency Work Authorization Requires Notification and Judgment Criteria

Even in emergencies where the Owner acts immediately, the Contractor must be notified. (RFP §2.10.5) This preserves the Owner's right to recover costs and the Contractor's right to participate in or observe remediation. The Project Manager should have a clear emergency notification protocol.

**Emergency vs. Standard Deficiency Judgment (Semantic Lensing item B-003):** RFP §2.10.5 states the threshold for emergency action as work that "must be done immediately to prevent serious damage, injury, or loss of life." However, determining whether a specific deficiency meets this threshold requires judgment. The following guidance is provided (ASSUMPTION: these are directional criteria, not normative thresholds, and should be confirmed with the Owner):

- **Emergency indicators:** Active water intrusion causing structural damage, electrical hazards presenting shock/fire risk, structural failure or imminent collapse, gas leaks, situations requiring immediate evacuation, conditions preventing safe facility use.
- **Standard deficiency indicators:** Cosmetic defects, performance degradation that does not present immediate safety risk, equipment malfunction with available workaround, issues that can be safely isolated or contained pending Contractor response.
- **Ambiguous situations:** When in doubt, the Project Manager should err on the side of safety and classify as emergency, documenting the reasoning for the classification decision.

(Source: RFP §2.10.5; Guidance C-4; Semantic Lensing item B-003)

### C-5: Bond and Insurance Status at Warranty Period Commencement

Performance bonds and contractor insurance should remain in effect during the warranty period per upstream dependencies (DEL-021-02, DEL-021-03; _DEPENDENCIES.md). The Project Manager should confirm this at the start of the warranty period. ASSUMPTION: The executed CCDC 14-2013 contract will specify the exact continuation terms; verify against the executed contract (DEL-021-04) at project closeout.

### C-6: Defect Classification Framework Is Not Defined in the RFP

The RFP treats all defects uniformly under the 10-day rectification requirement. However, practical administration may benefit from a classification framework (e.g., safety-critical / functional / cosmetic) that informs prioritization and the emergency provision trigger. ASSUMPTION: Such a classification, if adopted, should be agreed upon with the Owner at or before warranty period commencement to avoid disputes. As of this draft, classification criteria are TBD. See Specification REQ-021-05-02 for normative treatment.

### C-7: Record Retention After Warranty Expiry

Warranty documentation — deficiency logs, notification letters, rectification records, holdback ledger — should be retained post-project. (MEMORY.md) The specific retention period is TBD. ASSUMPTION: Retention should align with the project's overall record retention policy and applicable Alberta statutory limitation periods for construction claims.

### C-8: Mid-Period Administrative Review (Semantic Lensing item X-002)

The documents provide for physical inspections at scheduled intervals (C-2) but do not address an administrative/management review of the warranty administration process itself during the 2-year period. A formal mid-period status review (e.g., at 12 months) -- separate from physical inspections -- could assess:
- Overall warranty administration health (log currency, notification timeliness, holdback accuracy).
- Outstanding open items and their aging.
- Bond and insurance status confirmation.
- Adequacy of documentation practices and format compliance.
- Lessons learned from the first year of administration to improve the second year.

ASSUMPTION: Whether a formal mid-period administrative review is conducted is TBD. For a 2-year guarantee period on a facility of this type, such a review is directionally recommended to support audit readiness and continuous process improvement. (Source: Semantic Lensing item X-002; Guidance C-2)

## Trade-offs

### T-1: Proactive Inspection vs. Reactive Administration

**Trade-off:** Scheduling proactive inspections adds administrative effort and cost but reduces the risk that defects are discovered late or after the warranty period expires undetected. Purely reactive administration (waiting for the Owner to report issues) is lower effort but places the discovery burden on the Owner and may result in defects only being found after significant consequential damage.

**Proposed direction:** ASSUMPTION: Proactive inspection is preferred for a facility of this type (heavy industrial use, landfill equipment), where mechanical and structural defects may not be immediately visible but can escalate. This is directional guidance only; the human should confirm the inspection schedule approach.

### T-2: Granular Holdback Ledger vs. Lump-Sum Holdback

**Trade-off:** A granular ledger (per-deficiency holdback amounts) provides transparency and simplifies partial release, but is more administratively intensive. A lump-sum holdback is simpler but may create disputes at warranty end.

**Proposed direction:** ASSUMPTION: A granular ledger per deficiency is recommended to support orderly, item-by-item release and to reduce end-of-warranty disputes.
