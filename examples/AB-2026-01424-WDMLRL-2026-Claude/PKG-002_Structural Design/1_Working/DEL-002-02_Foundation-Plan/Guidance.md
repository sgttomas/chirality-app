# Guidance — DEL-002-02 Foundation Plan

---

## Purpose

The Foundation Plan drawing set (DEL-002-02) exists to provide the construction team with fully coordinated, P.Eng.-stamped Issued for Construction drawings that define the below-grade structural system for the New North Shop addition. It is the primary structural document governing foundation construction (DEL-010-01 — PKG-010) and establishes the physical datum — column locations, slab elevation, embedded elements — upon which all superstructure work depends.

From the project decomposition:
- It directly satisfies **SOW-0012** (complete final structural design) and **SOW-0019** (foundation design, variable-price scope).
- It supports **OBJ-001** (code-compliant, fully functional facility), **OBJ-003** (P.Eng.-stamped IFC documentation), and **OBJ-006** (foundation costs reconciled post-geotech within CCDC 14–2013 framework).

The variable-price nature of the foundation scope (RFP §4.8.2) makes this deliverable unusual within the package: the drawings must be design-complete and construction-ready, while also serving as the reference document for post-geotechnical cost negotiation with Camrose County.

Source: Decomposition WDMLRL_Decomposition_Claude.md §5, §7; RFP §4.8.2.

## Principles

### P-1: Geotech-First, then IFC
The foundation design cannot be finalized without the geotechnical investigation results (DEL-008-01). Preliminary design work and coordination can proceed using the normal-ground-conditions assumption stated in RFP §4.8.2, but **the foundation plan must not be issued for construction until geotechnical data has been received and the design confirmed**. Premature IFC issue carries significant risk of design rework and cost exposure to the Design-Builder.

Source: RFP §4.8.2.

### P-2: Variable-Price Scope Transparency
The RFP explicitly separates foundation pricing from the firm-price scope (RFP §4.8.1, §4.8.2). The Foundation Plan drawings must clearly document the assumptions underlying the preliminary cost estimate so that, when the geotechnical report is available, the basis of any cost change can be demonstrated objectively to Camrose County. This supports OBJ-006 and the CCDC 14--2013 contractual negotiation process.

**Assumption Tracking:** Design assumptions made during the preliminary phase (e.g., bearing capacity, frost depth, foundation type, soil conditions) form the baseline for the variable-price cost estimate. Changes to these assumptions between preliminary and final design should be tracked using a formal mechanism -- for example, a design assumption log or register maintained alongside the structural calculations (DEL-002-10). Each assumption change should document: (a) the original assumption and its source, (b) the revised value and the evidence triggering the change (e.g., geotechnical report finding), and (c) the cost impact classification (no impact / minor / significant). This tracking supports the post-geotechnical cost reconciliation process under CCDC 14--2013. If a project-level change management process exists, the assumption tracking should integrate with it. TBD -- specific format and ownership to be confirmed by the Design-Builder project manager. (Source: Lensing item F-003; RFP §4.8.2.)

Source: RFP §4.8.2; Decomposition OBJ-006, SOW-0304.

### P-3: Column Grid Governs Everything
The foundation plan establishes the column grid that all other structural deliverables (framing plans, crane support details, mezzanine details, stair details) and all MEP and architectural deliverables must reference. Changes to the column grid after interdisciplinary coordination has begun cascade across many deliverables. The column grid should be locked as early as possible and communicated to all disciplines.

Source: ASSUMPTION (standard structural design practice); aligned with RFP §3.3.2 (fully coordinated design across all disciplines).

### P-4: Embedments and Below-Slab Elements Are Hardest to Change
Steel plate embedments, service pit structure, sump drain rough-ins, and cistern provisions are cast into concrete. Errors or omissions discovered after concrete placement are extremely costly to remediate. These elements require early and thorough interdisciplinary coordination — particularly with mechanical/plumbing (drain locations) and the crane supplier (crane rail and hook loads).

Source: RFP §3.4 (steel plates, service pit, cistern); App B (Shop) — floor plan; ASSUMPTION (standard construction sequencing practice).

### P-5: Alberta Jurisdiction — P.Eng. Stamp Is Non-Negotiable
All IFC drawings must bear the stamp and signature of a P.Eng. registered in Alberta. This is a hard legal requirement under Alberta's Engineering and Geoscience Professions Act (ASSUMPTION: applicable; specific Act reference location TBD). Unstamped drawings cannot legally be submitted for building permit or used for construction in Alberta.

Source: RFP §3.3.2 ("All Issued For Construction Drawings must be signed/stamped by a professional engineer licensed to practice in the province of Alberta").

## Considerations

### C-1: Foundation Type Selection
The appropriate foundation type (e.g., spread footings, continuous footings/grade beams, pile-supported, or combination) depends entirely on the geotechnical investigation results. A concrete structure of this scale (35 ft ceiling, 5-tonne cranes) will impose significant column loads. In Alberta's climate, frost depth is a key design parameter. Until DEL-008-01 is available:
- The preliminary estimate should assume a conventional spread-footing / grade-beam / slab-on-grade system typical for normal ground conditions in central Alberta.
- If geotechnical results reveal poor soils or high groundwater, significant design and cost changes may be required.

Source: RFP §4.8.2 (normal ground conditions assumption); ASSUMPTION (design practice).

### C-2: Crane Column Load Demands
Two 5-tonne overhead cranes impose dynamic loads (vertical wheel loads, horizontal side thrust, longitudinal traction) on the crane columns. These are typically among the largest point loads the foundation must carry in an industrial maintenance facility of this type. The Structural Engineer should size crane column footings for both service and factored load combinations, including crane impact factors as required by applicable code.

Source: RFP §3.4 ("2 – 5-tonne overhead cranes on a trolley"); App B (Shop) — crane bay layout; ASSUMPTION (crane load design practice).

### C-3: Service Pit Coordination with Mechanical, Plumbing, and Electrical
The service pit (also referred to as "service trench" in some source documents; see Decomposition §2 Vocabulary Map for canonical terminology) requires coordination with: (a) the mechanical engineer for ventilation (RFP §3.4 — "ventilated and lighted service pit"), (b) the plumbing engineer for any sump drain within the pit, (c) the electrical engineer for lighting and power in the pit, and (d) the structural engineer for pit walls, floor slab, and cover/grating system. The pit structure must be designed before concrete is placed in that zone.

Source: RFP §3.4; App B (Shop) — service pit shown on floor plan.

### C-4: Slab Thickness and Reinforcement for Heavy Equipment
The conceptual drawings show steel plate embedments at multiple floor locations for track and packer-type heavy equipment — which can impose high point loads and dynamic loads on the slab. The slab design must account for concentrated loads from tracked equipment (e.g., a motor scraper as referenced in RFP §3.4) rather than treating the floor as a typical light-duty slab. This may require a thicker slab and/or additional reinforcement beneath crane rail areas and at steel plate locations.

Source: RFP §3.4 ("steel plating in concrete at strategic locations to accommodate track and packer type heavy equipment"); App B (Shop) — steel plate locations on floor plan.

### C-5: Cistern Below-Slab or In-Building Provisions
The 50,000 L cistern is a significant below-grade or at-grade element shown in the conceptual plan (App B — Shop). The foundation plan must coordinate the cistern location and any slab thickening, sump, or structural provision for the cistern and associated plumbing. The structural engineer should confirm with the plumbing engineer whether the cistern is buried, semi-buried, or slab-mounted, as this affects foundation design.

Source: RFP §3.4 ("Minimum 50,000 L Cistern complete with the necessary plumbing"); App B (Shop) — cistern shown in Utility Room area.

### C-6: Mezzanine Load-Bearing Provisions
The mezzanine over Parts Room, Utility Room, and Wash Bay must be load-bearing for heavy storage (oil totes, oil pumping equipment per RFP §3.4). The mezzanine columns or walls must have adequate foundation support. The foundation plan must show footings or thickened slab at mezzanine support points.

Source: RFP §3.4 ("Mezzanine storage above utility and parts room capable of handling heavy items such as several oil totes and oil pumping equipment"); App B (Shop) notes ("Load Bearing Over Parts Room + Utility For Storage").

### C-8: Drawing Sheet Organization and Count
The Specification Documentation section notes that the foundation plan may consist of "one or more sheets depending on building complexity" but provides no rationale for what determines the split. For a 130 ft x 100 ft building with multiple bay types, a service pit, embedded steel plates, a cistern provision, and mezzanine support points, the Structural Engineer should establish the drawing sheet organization at the preliminary design stage. Considerations include: (a) whether the foundation plan is a single sheet or split into zones, (b) whether foundation sections and details appear on the foundation plan sheet(s) or only in DEL-002-04 (Structural Sections & Details), and (c) the anticipated number of sheets for coordination review scope planning. TBD -- Structural Engineer to determine at preliminary design stage.

Source: Specification Documentation section; ASSUMPTION (standard drawing practice). (Lensing item X-002.)

### C-7: Wash Bay Drainage and Mud Sump Interface
The exterior mud sump (on the wash bay side, per App B) requires an interface between the building foundation/slab edge and the exterior civil/drainage work. The foundation plan should show the slab edge detail at the wash bay exit and the sump connection. This is a civil/structural interface that must be coordinated early.

Source: RFP §3.4 ("Mud sump from wash bay on exterior of building for clean out with excavator"); App B (Shop) — Wash Bay Mud Sump shown exterior.

## Trade-offs

### T-1: Timing of Geotech vs. Design Progress
There is an inherent tension between the project completion deadline (December 31, 2026) and the sequencing constraint that foundation IFC drawings cannot be finalized before the geotechnical report is available. The Design-Builder must balance advancing design (to maintain schedule) against the risk of designing to assumptions that the geotechnical report may invalidate. Recommended approach: advance preliminary design and interdisciplinary coordination as far as possible using the normal-ground-conditions assumption; plan for one design update cycle after geotech receipt.

Source: RFP §3.1 (deadline), §4.8.2 (geotech-first pricing); Decomposition OBJ-002.

### T-2: Foundation Cost Certainty vs. Schedule
Waiting for geotechnical results before issuing the foundation plan protects cost certainty but may delay construction start. Starting construction on other elements (site work, superstructure procurement) while foundation design is finalized can reduce schedule risk but requires careful sequencing.

Source: RFP §4.8.2; Decomposition OBJ-002, OBJ-006. ASSUMPTION (project scheduling practice).

### T-3: Slab Thickness — Cost vs. Capacity
A heavier, more robustly reinforced slab provides more capacity for future equipment loads and is more tolerant of subgrade variability, but increases concrete and reinforcing costs. The geotechnical report will clarify whether subgrade improvement (e.g., granular sub-base, soil treatment) can reduce slab requirements.

Source: RFP §3.4; ASSUMPTION (structural design trade-off).
