# DEL-018-04 | Guidance — Cement & Gravel Pads

---

## Purpose

DEL-018-04 delivers the cement (concrete) pads and gravel pad required to support the operational program of the WDMLRL Maintenance Shop Addition. These pads serve multiple functions:

1. **Crane base support** — The cement pad(s) within or adjacent to the New North Shop provide the structural foundation for the two 5-tonne overhead cranes on trolley (DEL-016-01). This is the highest-consequence sub-element of this deliverable: crane installation cannot proceed until the crane pads are complete and cured (Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md; App B).

2. **Equipment staging and operational areas** — The cement and gravel pads provide stable, all-weather surfaces for equipment placement, storage, and operational movement around the shop complex (Source: _CONTEXT.md; App B).

3. **Site organization** — The Gravel Pad (north of New North Shop) and Cement Pad zones demarcate distinct operational areas as laid out in the conceptual plan (Source: App B).

The dual SOW references (SOW-0078 for cement pads, SOW-0079 for gravel pad) reflect the two distinct material systems and likely the different supply responsibilities (contractor supplies cement; County/Landfill supplies gravel). These may also reflect a phased or sequenced construction approach, though the RFP does not explicitly describe phases for this work. (See Conflict Table below.)

## Principles

### P1 — Design-Build Responsibility
This is a design-build project (CCDC 14–2013). The Contractor is responsible for producing IFC civil drawings that define pad dimensions, concrete mix, reinforcement, slopes, and compaction specifications before construction begins (Source: RFP §3.3.2). The conceptual drawings (Appendix B) are the starting reference, not the construction documents. The IFC drawings govern construction.

### P2 — Crane Pad Coordination is Critical Path
The crane pad is the highest-stakes sub-element of this deliverable. Crane installation (DEL-016-01) depends on the crane pad being complete, cured, and accepted. Design of the crane pad must be coordinated with the crane supplier's anchor bolt pattern, base plate dimensions, and loading before IFC drawings are issued. Late coordination here becomes a schedule risk for the entire crane installation sequence. (Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md; App B; RFP §3.4)

### P3 — Material Supply Split Requires Clear Coordination
Appendix B states: "Contractor to supply cement and prep – Landfill to supply gravel." This creates a split-supply arrangement. The Contractor must plan for timely gravel delivery by the County/Landfill and not assume gravel is available on demand. The supply interface should be formalized in the project schedule and confirmed with the County/Landfill representative at project kick-off. (Source: App B; RFP §3.3.1, SOW-0203)

### P4 — Drainage is a Design Constraint, Not an Afterthought
The RFP requires that grading design account for positive drainage and not alter neighboring property drainage conditions (RFP §3.4). Pad surfaces — particularly the cement pads — must be sloped appropriately in the IFC design. This constraint should be considered early in civil design (PKG-005) and not treated as a finish-grade adjustment.

### P5 — Sequencing Governs Constructability
Pad construction depends on DEL-018-01 (topsoil strip) and DEL-018-02 (site grading) being sufficiently complete. Concrete placement on uncompacted or ungraded subgrade will compromise long-term pad performance. The concrete cure period (ASSUMPTION: minimum 28 days to design strength; shorter periods with accelerated mix admixtures possible) must be factored into the schedule before crane installation loads are applied. (Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md; ASSUMPTION)

## Considerations

### C1 — Two Pad Types, Different Performance Requirements
The cement pads and gravel pad serve different purposes and have different design and performance requirements:
- **Cement pads** must support defined structural loads (crane bases, heavy equipment), be durable in freeze-thaw conditions, resist surface degradation from fuel/oil spills (ASSUMPTION), and provide a consistent, smooth surface for operations.
- **Gravel pad** must provide a stable, load-bearing surface for vehicle and equipment movement/storage; it is lower-cost and more easily repaired but requires periodic maintenance (ASSUMPTION).

The civil engineer (PKG-005) should select appropriate design parameters for each pad type based on use case and loading.

### C2 — Cold Weather Concreting
The project completion deadline is December 31, 2026 (RFP §3.1). Depending on construction sequencing, concrete placement may occur in autumn or early winter in Alberta. Cold weather concreting provisions (heated enclosures, admixtures, extended cure monitoring) may be required. ASSUMPTION: the civil specification will address cold weather requirements; the Contractor should plan for this contingency in the project schedule and budget.

### C3 — Geotechnical Influence on Pad Design
The RFP requires a full geotechnical investigation (PKG-008) as a design input. Subgrade bearing capacity directly affects the required thickness and reinforcement of cement pads and the required depth/composition of the gravel pad subbase. Pad design should not be finalized until geotech results are available. (Source: RFP §3.3.2 — "A full complete geotechnical report will be required by the successful proponent to use during the design process.")

### C4 — Dual SOW Reference Ambiguity
SOW-0078 and SOW-0079 are assigned separately in the decomposition but are both mapped to DEL-018-04. This is consistent with two pad types (cement and gravel). However, some ambiguity exists as to whether SOW-0078/0079 also reflect phasing (e.g., crane pad first, then balance of pads), or simply the two materials. The conceptual drawing shows both pads adjacent to the building complex with no explicit phasing notation. ASSUMPTION: both pads are constructed in a single construction phase following site grading, unless the Contractor's schedule requires phased access. See Conflict Table below.

### C5 — Inspection Protocol
The County project representative must be present at all inspections, and copies of all inspection reports must be provided to the County (RFP §3.3.2). The Contractor should notify the County representative with adequate advance notice to avoid scheduling delays.

### C6 — Unit System Normalization (Enrichment: B-003)
The current document set uses mixed imperial and metric units: Datasheet.md records dimensions in feet (18', 60', 130') following the Appendix B conceptual drawing annotations, while Procedure.md uses millimetres for compaction lift thickness (200mm) and tolerances (+/-25mm). This creates a conversion error risk during construction. The IFC civil drawings will establish the governing unit system. Until IFC drawings are issued, interim documents should be internally consistent within each document. A unit conversion table or dual-unit notation should be considered once the IFC unit system is confirmed.

### C7 — Terminology: "Cement" vs. "Concrete" (Enrichment: E-003)
Throughout the document set, the terms "cement pad" and "concrete pad" are used interchangeably. Technically, "cement" refers only to the cite material (Portland cement), while "concrete" is the finished product (cement + aggregate + water). The RFP and Appendix B use "cement" in a colloquial sense to mean "concrete." Specification.md scope section clarifies "cement (concrete)" at first use. For consistency with the RFP language, the document set retains "cement pad" as the primary term but users should understand this means "concrete pad" in all instances. This note reduces confusion for readers with a technical civil engineering background.

### C8 — SOW Phasing Terminology (Enrichment: B-004)
_REFERENCES.md Cross-References section describes SOW-0078 as "Phase 1/Concrete Pads" and SOW-0079 as "Phase 2/Gravel Pads or additional pads," implying a phased construction approach. Guidance.md CONF-018-04-01 proposes treating these as two pad types, not phases. Until the human rules on CONF-018-04-01, the recommended approach is to treat SOW-0078 and SOW-0079 as scope identifiers for two pad types. The term "phases" in _REFERENCES.md should be understood as a labeling convention, not a scheduling directive. Once the human confirms, _REFERENCES.md terminology should be aligned with the confirmed interpretation. (Note: this agent does not modify _REFERENCES.md per its write-scope constraints.)

## Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Option A | Option B | Consideration |
|-----------|----------|----------|---------------|
| Concrete strength class | Higher MPa (e.g., 30–35 MPa) — more durable, higher cost | Lower MPa (e.g., 25 MPa) — adequate for standard slab-on-grade | Design loads, freeze-thaw exposure, and intended surface use should drive selection; P.Eng. to specify. TBD. |
| Reinforcement type | Rebar mesh — higher load capacity, crack control | Fibre reinforcement or no reinforcement — lower cost for lightly loaded areas | Crane pads will likely require rebar; other cement pads may be designed differently depending on loads. ASSUMPTION. |
| Gravel pad depth | Deeper sub-base — better long-term performance, better drainage capacity, more resilient under heavy vehicle loading | Shallower — lower material volume (though County supplies gravel, so material cost is partially offset); faster construction | Geotech report will inform minimum required depth; County supplies gravel so material cost is partially offset. The value trade-off depends on intended vehicle loading (e.g., heavy trucks vs. light equipment), seasonal moisture conditions, and long-term maintenance expectations. A deeper pad may reduce periodic regrading/replenishment frequency but requires more placement and compaction effort. TBD pending geotech report. (Enrichment: C-004) |
| Construction phasing | Construct crane pads first (critical path priority), then remaining pads | Construct all pads in one continuous operation | Critical path analysis may favour constructing crane pads first; this should be reflected in the project schedule. |
| Lifecycle maintenance cost | Concrete pads — lower long-term maintenance (joint sealant replacement, surface repairs at long intervals) | Gravel pad — periodic regrading, replenishment of material, pothole repair; lower initial cost but recurring maintenance | For a County asset with a multi-decade service life, lifecycle cost should be considered alongside initial construction cost. Gravel pad maintenance frequency depends on traffic intensity and seasonal conditions. County gravel supply may offset replenishment material cost but not labour. (Enrichment: F-004) |

## Examples

- **GRAVEL PAD** — labeled in the upper (north) zone of the Appendix B plan, north of the New North Shop building footprint. The 60' dimension annotation on the plan likely represents the depth (north–south) of this zone. (Source: App B)
- **CEMENT PAD** — labeled in the zone between the Overhead Pole Shed / Metal Racking area and the Gravel Pad area, with an 18' dimension annotation. A second "CEMENT PAD" notation with an arrow appears on the east side of the plan adjacent to the Wash Bay Mud Sump. (Source: App B)
- **Overhead Pole Shed / Metal Racking** — these elements are shown within or adjacent to the cement pad zone, suggesting the cement pad supports storage and equipment racking areas in addition to (or as part of) the crane bay zone. (Source: App B)

Do not use Appendix B dimensions as construction quantities without IFC confirmation.
