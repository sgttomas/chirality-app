## Purpose

DEL-018-06 exists to bring three critical utility services — natural gas, three-phase electrical power, and communications — to the new West Dried Meat Lake Regional Landfill Maintenance Shop Addition. These utility connections are the foundational enabling infrastructure for the facility: without them, the building cannot heat, operate equipment, run electrical systems, or communicate.

The deliverable directly supports:
- **OBJ-001**: Delivering a code-compliant, fully functional maintenance shop that satisfies the County's operational program and passes all applicable inspections.
- **OBJ-005**: Installing and commissioning all electrical and low-voltage systems to operational readiness (specifically via SOW-0081 and SOW-0082).

Sources: Decomposition SS5 (Objectives), SS7 DEL-018-06; RFP SS1.0.

The utility tie-ins are on the project's critical path. Delays in utility provider coordination, permitting, or installation directly delay the facility's ability to operate, commission other systems, and achieve Substantial Performance. Early engagement with providers is the single most important risk mitigation action for this deliverable.

Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md; ASSUMPTION — standard critical path analysis for rural Alberta utility projects where long provider lead times are common.

## Principles

### 1. Coordinate Early with Utility Providers

The RFP mandates that the Proponent "coordinate" each tie-in — natural gas, electrical, and communications — with the relevant utility providers (RFP SS3.3.2). In rural Alberta, utility provider lead times for new service connections can be significant (weeks to months). The General Contractor should initiate provider contact and service applications as early as possible in the project timeline.

### 2. Three Discrete Systems Require Parallel Coordination Tracks

Each of the three SOW items (SOW-0080, SOW-0081, SOW-0082) involves a different utility provider, different regulatory framework, and different Safety Code discipline. They should be tracked as separate coordination work streams even though they share a common construction phase (trenching, conduit installation). Combining the trench run where feasible is advisable for cost efficiency, but permit and inspection requirements must be tracked independently.

### 3. Design Must Precede Installation

Utility routing, service sizing, and connection point locations must be established by the civil and electrical design packages (PKG-005, DEL-004) before trenching begins. Do not commence physical installation without IFC drawings that are stamped by a P.Eng. licensed in Alberta (RFP SS3.3.2). This sequencing is a hard constraint from the RFP and Safety Code permit requirements.

### 4. Three-Phase Electrical is a Non-Negotiable Facility Requirement

The RFP explicitly states the building shall run on three-phase power (RFP SS3.4). The electrical service tie-in must be designed for and delivered as three-phase. This drives the service capacity requirements, the service entrance design, and the utility provider application. The General Contractor and electrical subcontractor should confirm the three-phase supply point location with the electrical utility at the earliest opportunity.

### 5. Inspections Require County Presence

The RFP is explicit: the County project representative must be present at all inspections, and copies of all completed inspection reports must be provided to the County (RFP SS3.3.2; SOW-0084, SOW-0085). Schedule inspections with adequate lead time to ensure the County representative can attend. Do not request inspections without coordinating with the County's project manager.

### 6. Coordinate Trenching with Other PKG-018 Work

The utility trenches will cross or run adjacent to areas being constructed under other DEL-018 deliverables (gravel driving surface, pads, drainage). Utility routing should be planned early and shared with the civil design team (PKG-005) to avoid conflicts and minimize re-excavation. ASSUMPTION: coordinating trench runs to occur before final surface construction is the most cost-effective sequencing approach.

## Considerations

### Regulatory and Permitting Environment (Alberta)

Utility connections in Alberta require Safety Code permits obtained by a licensed contractor/permit holder in the relevant discipline (gas, electrical). The General Contractor must confirm which subcontractors are licensed to pull the relevant permits before commencing work. This is now a formal requirement in the Specification (REQ-04.9). [Enrichment: X-003]

**Permit Fee vs. Permit Responsibility (Rationale):** The RFP establishes a split responsibility for permits: the County pays for permit fees (RFP SS3.3.1 — "Pay for development permit, building permit, and Safety Code Permit Fees"), but the Proponent is responsible for obtaining the permits (RFP SS3.3.2). This distinction matters for the regulatory audit trail because:

1. **Financial responsibility** (County) is separate from **administrative responsibility** (Proponent). The Proponent must apply for, manage, and track each permit through to closure.
2. The permit is issued in the name of the permit holder (the licensed contractor), not the fee payer. The County's payment of fees does not make the County the permit holder or responsible party for Safety Code compliance.
3. For audit purposes, the General Contractor must maintain a clear record showing: (a) which entity applied for each permit, (b) which licensed contractor/permit holder is named on each permit, (c) that the County was invoiced for or directly paid the permit fees, and (d) that all permit conditions were satisfied and closed out.

This interpretation is consistent with RFP SS3.3.1 and SS3.3.2 read together. ASSUMPTION: the County's payment of permit fees does not transfer regulatory compliance responsibility to the County. [Enrichment: A-004]

### Electrical Load and Service Sizing

The conceptual electrical drawing (App B — Electrical) identifies significant electrical loads: two 5-tonne overhead cranes, high bay lighting, heating systems, welding receptacles (high-voltage), a 40-amp compressor, 70-amp fire hose pump, 70-amp pressure washer, exhaust fans, ceiling fans, and low-voltage systems. The electrical service tie-in must be sized to support the full designed load with appropriate spare capacity. This sizing must be confirmed through the electrical design process (DEL-004).

A summary of anticipated electrical loads from App B has been added to the Datasheet (Electrical Service Attributes section) for reference. [Enrichment: B-002]

### Natural Gas Service and Heating System Interface

The natural gas tie-in (SOW-0080) is the supply for the building's heating systems. The RFP specifies a high-recovery heating system (RFP SS3.4; SOW-0035). Gas service capacity must be sized to meet the heating load as determined by the mechanical design (DEL-003). The gas service tie-in contractor (likely a licensed gas contractor/plumber as subcontractor) must coordinate sizing with the mechanical design team.

ASSUMPTION: Natural gas is the primary heating fuel. The RFP specifies SOW-0080 as a "natural gas tie-in" but does not explicitly confirm gas as the heating fuel source. If the site has no existing gas service, the cost and lead time for a new gas main extension could be significant and should be assessed early. See the Gas Service Extension Cost/Schedule Risk Assessment below. [Enrichment: A-001]
