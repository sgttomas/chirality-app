# Guidance — DEL-018-03 Gravel Driving Surface

---

## Purpose

DEL-018-03 exists to provide a stable, load-bearing gravel driving surface at the WDMLRL Maintenance Shop Addition site that supports safe and functional access for heavy equipment, material delivery vehicles, and operational staff.

The RFP specifically requires a gravel surface "designed to accommodate the weight and turning radius of large construction equipment, such as a motor scraper" (RFP §3.3.2). This framing makes clear that the driving surface is not a light-duty access road: it must function under the most demanding equipment the facility operates, including motor scrapers used in landfill cell construction and management.

The driving surface is directly tied to OBJ-001 — delivering a code-compliant, fully functional maintenance shop addition that satisfies the County's operational program. A deficient driving surface would impair the facility's ability to receive and service the heavy equipment that constitutes the County's core landfill operations.

Sources: RFP §3.3.2; _CONTEXT.md (OBJ-001 association); WDMLRL_Decomposition_Claude.md §5.

## Principles

### 1. Design Governs Construction
The gravel surface must be constructed in conformance with the Issued for Construction (IFC) civil drawings produced by PKG-005 (Civil Design). No construction work on the gravel surface should proceed without the approved IFC civil drawings (DEL-005-04 — Driving Surface & Pad Layout Plan, and DEL-005-07 — Civil Specification). The civil engineer bears the professional responsibility for the design; the General Contractor bears the construction responsibility.

Source: RFP §3.3.2; SOW-0018.

### 2. Owner Supplies the Aggregate
A critical scope boundary: the supply of gravel aggregate is the County/Landfill's responsibility (SOW-0203; RFP §3.3.1). The Contractor's scope is placement, spreading, and compaction — not procurement of aggregate. This split is explicitly noted in the Appendix B conceptual drawings: "Landfill to supply gravel."

This means the Contractor's schedule is dependent on the Owner's aggregate delivery. Coordination protocols should be established at the outset of the construction phase to prevent placement delays caused by aggregate unavailability.

Source: RFP §3.3.1 (SOW-0203); App B (Shop floor plan note).

### 3. Subgrade Quality Determines Surface Performance
The long-term performance of a gravel driving surface under heavy loads is primarily a function of subgrade preparation. Topsoil and organic material must be stripped (DEL-018-01), and the subgrade must be graded to proper elevation and drainage slope (DEL-018-02) before gravel placement begins.

A subgrade inspection should be completed and recorded before any aggregate is placed. Placing gravel over inadequate subgrade is a common cause of surface failure under heavy equipment.

Source: SOW-0075; SOW-0076; _DEPENDENCIES.md; ASSUMPTION: standard geotechnical practice for heavy-load gravel surfaces.

### 4. Load Class Drives All Thickness and Compaction Decisions
The motor scraper class design load is the governing performance criterion for this surface. All decisions about gravel depth, gradation, and compaction standard must be derived from this load class, not from lighter-duty assumptions. The civil engineer must confirm the design approach in the civil specification (DEL-005-07).

ASSUMPTION: A motor scraper (e.g., CAT 623 or similar) has an operating weight in the range of 30,000–50,000 kg depending on payload. Typical gravel road design for such loads may require 300–600 mm of compacted gravel over prepared subgrade, but the actual design thickness depends on subgrade CBR (California Bearing Ratio) or equivalent geotechnical assessment. The geotechnical report (DEL-008-01 — Geotechnical Investigation) should inform the civil design.

Source: RFP §3.3.2; SOW-0077; ASSUMPTION for typical load range.

### 5. Drainage Cannot Be an Afterthought
The RFP explicitly requires that the grading design provide positive drainage and not alter drainage conditions of neighbouring properties (RFP §3.4). For a gravel surface under heavy use, inadequate drainage leads to softening, rutting, and progressive failure.

The gravel surface must be integrated with the overall site drainage system designed under PKG-005 (DEL-005-02 — Site Grading Plan, DEL-005-03 — Drainage Plan). Cross-slopes and edge drainage should be designed to prevent water ponding on or under the driving surface.

Source: RFP §3.4; SOW-0015.

## Considerations

### Sequencing Within PKG-018
The gravel driving surface depends on completion of:
1. DEL-018-01 (Topsoil Stripping) — must precede placement on all surface areas.
2. DEL-018-02 (Site Grading & Landscaping) — must precede placement to ensure correct sub-base elevation and drainage slope.

Once those are complete, DEL-018-03 can proceed in parallel with DEL-018-04 (Pads), DEL-018-05 (Wash Bay Drainage), and DEL-018-06 (Utility Tie-Ins).

Early completion of the driving surface also benefits subsequent packages: it provides stable access for DEL-016 (crane delivery and installation) and general construction traffic for PKG-017 (building construction). The driving surface is therefore a schedule enabler, not just a standalone construction task.

Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md; WDMLRL_Decomposition_Claude.md PKG-016 and PKG-017 package descriptions.

### Maintenance During Construction
ASSUMPTION: Once placed, the gravel driving surface may require maintenance (top-dressing, grading, regrading) if subjected to heavy construction traffic before the site is substantially complete. The Contractor should consider a staged placement approach — initial lift during construction, final dress-out before CCC.

### County Inspection Involvement
The County requires its project representative to be present at all inspections (RFP §3.3.2; SOW-0084). For the driving surface, this includes at minimum:
- Subgrade acceptance prior to gravel placement.
- Post-compaction surface acceptance.

The Contractor should provide adequate advance notice to the County's project manager for inspection scheduling.

Source: RFP §3.3.2; SOW-0084, SOW-0085.

### Civil Design Dependency
DEL-018-03 cannot be executed in final form without approved IFC civil drawings. The civil specification (DEL-005-07) will define the binding technical requirements for aggregate type, gradation, depth, and compaction. Until those documents exist, the requirements in this document are based on the RFP intent and are subject to refinement by the civil engineer.

Source: RFP §3.3.2; SOW-0015, SOW-0018.

### Weather and Seasonal Constraints
ASSUMPTION: Gravel placement and compaction at the WDMLRL site (near Ferintosh, central Alberta) are subject to weather and seasonal constraints that should be addressed during construction planning. Relevant considerations include:

- **Frozen Ground:** Gravel placement and compaction on frozen subgrade may not achieve adequate compaction or bonding. The civil specification (DEL-005-07) should address frozen ground restrictions, or the Contractor should plan the work to avoid placement on frozen subgrade.
- **Spring Thaw:** During spring thaw, subgrade conditions may deteriorate. Placement should not proceed until the subgrade has dried to a condition suitable for compaction.
- **Wet Weather:** Excessive moisture in the aggregate or subgrade impairs compaction quality. The Contractor should have a wet weather shutdown protocol.
- **Moisture Content:** Step 3.3 of the Procedure references moisture content for compaction; the civil specification should specify the acceptable range.

These constraints are not explicitly addressed in the RFP but are standard considerations for earthworks in central Alberta. The civil engineer and site superintendent should confirm seasonal restrictions during project mobilization. (Lensing item F-003; also reflected in Procedure)
