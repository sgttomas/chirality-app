## Purpose

DEL-020-02 exists to formally close out construction of the West Dried Meat Lake Regional Landfill Maintenance Shop Addition by verifying that all Work has been completed to the satisfaction of the Owner (Camrose County), documenting and resolving any remaining deficiencies, and obtaining the Construction Completion Certificate (CCC) that signals substantial completion.

The CCC is contractually significant: it (a) marks the start of the 2-year guarantee period (RFP SS2.10.2), (b) triggers release of the lien holdback upon submission of the full documentation package (RFP SS2.13.2), and (c) establishes the completion date for contractual purposes under CCDC 14-2013 (RFP SS2.7).

Without a successfully executed CCC, the project cannot reach financial closeout (lien holdback retained), the guarantee period cannot be formally timed, and downstream closeout documentation (DEL-020-03) cannot be fully compiled.

**Supports:**
- OBJ-001: Code-compliant, fully functional delivery passing all applicable inspections (Decomposition SS5)
- OBJ-002: All work completed on or before December 31, 2026 (Decomposition SS5; RFP SS3.1)

## Principles

### 1. Self-Inspection is a Proponent Obligation, Not a Formality
The RFP requires the Proponent to carefully review all portions of the Work and satisfy themselves of completion before requesting Owner inspection (RFP SS2.14.1). The self-inspection should be treated as a genuine quality gate, not an administrative step. Deficiencies caught by the Proponent before Owner inspection are resolved faster and at lower cost than those identified during the Owner's formal inspection.

### 2. The Owner Controls CCC Issuance
The CCC is issued by the Owner (Camrose County), not the Proponent. The Proponent cannot self-certify completion. The Proponent's role is to achieve and demonstrate full compliance with the Contract documents; the Owner then confirms compliance and issues the CCC (RFP SS2.14.3). The Proponent should understand that any outstanding non-compliance — even minor — gives the Owner grounds to withhold the CCC.

### 3. Written Communication is Mandatory at Key Steps
The RFP explicitly requires written communication at two key steps: (a) the Proponent's request for final inspection (SS2.14.2) and (b) the Owner's notification of any defect or deficiency (SS2.10.4). All inspection-related communications should be documented in writing regardless of the medium first used, to protect both parties and maintain a clear record.

### 4. Deficiency Management is a Parallel Track, Not a Blocker in Isolation
The existence of deficiencies at the time of Owner inspection does not automatically prevent CCC issuance — the RFP's deficiency holdback mechanism (SS2.11) contemplates that some deficiencies may be documented and remediated post-CCC. However, the Owner will not issue a CCC until it confirms "full compliance with the Contract documents" (SS2.14.3). The Proponent should confirm with the Owner what threshold of outstanding deficiencies is acceptable before CCC issuance. This interpretation is an ASSUMPTION where the RFP is ambiguous — see Conflict Table below.

### 5. The December 31, 2026 Deadline is Non-Negotiable
SOW-0091 and RFP SS3.1 state the project completion deadline in absolute terms. The Proponent should plan the inspection sequence such that the self-inspection, written request, Owner inspection, and CCC issuance all occur with sufficient time buffer before December 31, 2026. Late discovery of major deficiencies during Owner inspection with insufficient time for remediation represents a material schedule risk.

### 6. Coordination Through the County Project Manager
All inspection scheduling and coordination is to flow through the County project manager (RFP SS3.1). The Proponent should establish the coordination channel early in the PKG-020 execution phase and confirm the County project manager's availability and any advance notice requirements for scheduling the final inspection. ## Considerations

### Timing and Sequencing
- DEL-020-01 (Commissioning) must be substantially complete before final inspection can occur, since the final inspection verifies all building systems are operational. (Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md; ASSUMPTION: implied by the logical sequencing of commissioning before final acceptance)
- All construction activities under PKG-019 must be substantially complete and QC-verified before requesting Owner inspection. (Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md)
- Allow adequate time between the Proponent's self-inspection and the Owner inspection to resolve deficiencies identified during self-inspection. The timeline for this buffer is TBD based on project schedule.

### Deficiency Identification and Tracking
- Establish a deficiency tracking log before the Owner inspection, so that deficiencies can be systematically recorded, assigned, tracked to resolution, and closed.
- Each deficiency entry should capture: description, location, responsible party, target remediation date, severity classification (see Deficiency Severity Classification below), and Owner approval status. (Enrichment: D-003)
- The deficiency log is also the basis for the Owner's Deficiency Holdback calculation (RFP SS2.11.1).

### Deficiency Severity Classification (Enrichment: D-003)
ASSUMPTION: The following severity classification framework is proposed for deficiency grading. The RFP does not define severity levels; this classification is inferred from standard construction closeout practice and is intended to support decision-making around CCC issuance threshold (see CONF-020-02-01).

| Severity | Criteria | CCC Implication |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Critical** | Affects life safety, structural integrity, or building code compliance; prevents occupancy or use | CCC should not be issued with unresolved critical deficiencies (ASSUMPTION) |
| **Major** | Affects functionality or contractual compliance but does not prevent safe occupancy; requires trade remediation | CCC issuance at Owner discretion; deficiency holdback applies |
| **Minor** | Cosmetic, aesthetic, or minor finish items that do not affect function or safety | CCC may be issued with minor deficiencies documented and held back financially |

This classification should be confirmed with the Owner before application. The severity level assigned to each deficiency influences whether the Proponent can pursue CCC issuance with outstanding items (per CONF-020-02-01).

### Deficiency Holdback Calculation (Enrichment: D-001)
RFP SS2.11.1 states the Owner may retain "an amount equal to the estimated value of outstanding deficiencies or defects" but does not specify:
- Who performs the cost estimation (Owner, Proponent, or agreed third party)
- What methodology is used (market rate, contract rate, or other basis)
- Whether the Proponent has a right to dispute the estimated value

ASSUMPTION: Standard practice in CCDC 14-2013 contracts typically allows the Owner to estimate holdback value based on reasonable cost to complete, with the Proponent able to provide their own estimate for negotiation. The Proponent should seek written confirmation of the holdback calculation methodology from the Owner before the inspection phase. (Source: RFP SS2.11.1; CCDC 14-2013 -- location TBD)

### Inspection Checklist Development
- The RFP does not prescribe a specific inspection checklist format. The Proponent should develop a checklist that covers all systems, spaces, finishes, and site conditions addressed in the Contract.
- ASSUMPTION: The checklist should be organized by building system/discipline (structural, architectural, mechanical, electrical, plumbing, civil/site) and should reference applicable Alberta Safety Code requirements and IFC drawing compliance. Minimum content categories are documented in Specification REQ-020-02-011. (Enrichment: A-001)
- The County project representative will be present at all inspections and will receive copies of all inspection reports (RFP SS3.3.2); the checklist format should be accessible and legible for non-specialist reviewers.

### Alberta Safety Code Inspections
- ASSUMPTION: Safety Code permit inspections (conducted by the applicable Safety Codes officer) during construction are a prerequisite to or concurrent with final inspection. These are primarily PKG-009 scope but their completion status will be verified as part of final inspection. The Proponent should confirm all mandatory Safety Code inspections have been completed and approved before requesting final Owner inspection. See Specification REQ-020-02-012 for the tracked requirement. (Enrichment: A-004)

### Guarantee Period Implications
- The CCC issuance date establishes the start of the 2-year guarantee period (RFP SS2.10.2). The Proponent should record this date accurately, as it governs the warranty/defect remediation obligations through PKG-021.
- Neither the CCC nor the final progress payment relieves the Proponent of responsibility for faulty materials or defective workmanship discovered during the guarantee period (RFP SS2.10.1).

### Owner Refusal of CCC / Additional Remediation (Enrichment: B-003)
ASSUMPTION: The Procedure covers the expected sequence (inspection, deficiencies, remediation, CCC). However, the scenario where the Owner declines to issue CCC after inspection — or identifies additional non-compliance beyond the initial deficiency list — is not procedurally addressed in the RFP. The Proponent should anticipate this possibility and have a process for:
- Requesting written explanation from the Owner for CCC refusal
- Responding to additional non-compliance items not on the original deficiency list
- Escalation path if the Proponent disagrees with the Owner's assessment

This is a material operational gap; the Proponent should clarify with the Owner before the inspection phase what the process is if CCC is not issued on first attempt.

## Trade-offs

### Trade-off 1: Thoroughness of Self-Inspection vs. Schedule Pressure
A more thorough self-inspection takes longer but reduces the likelihood of deficiencies being discovered during Owner inspection (which could delay CCC issuance). A rushed self-inspection preserves schedule in the short term but risks a longer Owner-inspection cycle if significant deficiencies are uncovered. Given the hard December 31, 2026 deadline, the Proponent must balance these competing pressures. Recommendation: prioritize thoroughness of self-inspection and plan inspection activities to complete by early-to-mid December 2026 at the latest to preserve remediation time.

### Trade-off 2: Pursuing CCC with Minor Outstanding Deficiencies vs. Waiting for Full Correction
The RFP's deficiency holdback mechanism (SS2.11) implies a process where CCC may be issued with some deficiencies outstanding (held back financially until corrected). However, the CCC issuance trigger at SS2.14.3 requires "full compliance with the Contract documents." This creates a potential ambiguity — see Conflict Table below. The Proponent should seek Owner clarification on whether a "deficiency punch-list approach" (CCC issued with documented minor deficiencies held back) is acceptable or whether all deficiencies must be corrected first. The deficiency severity classification framework (see Considerations above) provides a basis for this discussion. (Enrichment: D-003)
