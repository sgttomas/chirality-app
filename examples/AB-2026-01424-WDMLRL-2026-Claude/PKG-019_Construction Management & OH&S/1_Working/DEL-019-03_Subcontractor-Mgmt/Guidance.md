# Guidance — DEL-019-03: Subcontractor Management

---

## Purpose

DEL-019-03 exists because the Design-Builder cannot deliver the West Dried Meat Lake Regional Landfill Maintenance Shop Addition using direct labour alone. The project requires specialist trades and subcontracted services across multiple disciplines (structural, mechanical, electrical, plumbing, civil, and others). The Design-Builder, holding Prime Contractor designation under Alberta OH&S, is legally and contractually responsible for all persons on site — including all Subcontractors.

Effective subcontractor management serves two linked objectives:

- **OBJ-007**: Maintain safe site operations under Prime Contractor designation with zero lost-time incidents, fulfilling all OH&S obligations for subcontracted work. (Source: Decomposition §5)
- **OBJ-002**: Keep the project on schedule to meet the December 31, 2026 hard deadline. Delays caused by Subcontractor underperformance, non-compliance, or poor coordination are a primary schedule risk. (Source: Decomposition §5; RFP §3.1)

Note: The decomposition's Objective-to-Deliverable mapping explicitly lists DEL-019-03 as supporting OBJ-002 and OBJ-007 (Decomposition §7). The full text of those objectives (Decomposition §5) confirms this deliverable is central to both.

## Principles

### P-01: Prime Contractor Accountability is Non-Delegable
The Design-Builder's Prime Contractor status under Alberta OH&S means that safety accountability for all Subcontractors on site cannot be transferred or delegated away. The Design-Builder remains the accountable party regardless of which Subcontractor performs a given activity. All subcontractor management processes must be designed with this in mind.

Source: RFP §2.15.

### P-02: Subcontractors Must Be Qualified Before Mobilisation
Subcontractors should be qualified and approved before they are permitted to mobilise and commence work. Onboarding after mobilisation creates compliance gaps and OH&S risk. The qualification criteria and approval process should be established early in the project lifecycle.

Source: _CONTEXT.md (Success Criteria); Specification REQ-012.

### P-03: Financial and Safety Performance Are Both Monitored
Performance monitoring must include financial and safety metrics. These two dimensions are interrelated: Subcontractors with payment problems may cut corners on safety or materials, and Subcontractors with poor safety records create liability for the Prime Contractor. Quality performance (coordinated with DEL-019-04) and schedule performance are also monitored.

Source: Specification REQ-013; _DEPENDENCIES.md.

### P-04: OH&S Compliance for Subcontractors Is a Proposal Evaluation Criterion
The RFP evaluates proposals on health and safety program quality specifically in relation to "the safety management of subcontractors and other third parties on jobsites" (Criterion C, 10% weighting). This means the subcontractor OH&S management approach is not only a legal requirement but also a competitive differentiator. The management framework developed for this deliverable should be substantive and documented.

Source: RFP §5.1.2, Item C.

### P-05: Timely Payment Is a Legal and Contractual Obligation
Alberta's Prompt Payment and Construction Lien Act imposes timing obligations on payments to Subcontractors. The RFP specifies that invoices must be submitted at least every 31 days, payment is due within 28 days, and a 10% lien holdback applies (RFP §2.13.2). Failure to pay Subcontractors is specifically listed as a ground for the Owner to withhold payment from the Design-Builder (RFP §2.13.1.4). Subcontractor payment tracking should be treated as an integral part of subcontractor management, not a separate financial function.

Source: RFP §2.13.1.4; §2.13.2 (Lien Holdback).

### P-06: Subcontractor Coordination Is Routed Through the County Project Manager
All coordination with the Owner regarding subcontractor activities is channelled through the County project manager. The project manager's identity is confirmed at project award (TBD per SOW-0303). The weekly coordination forum provided by DEL-019-02 is the primary venue for surfacing and resolving subcontractor issues requiring Owner awareness.

Source: RFP §3.1; Decomposition §4 Scope Boundaries (SOW-0303); _DEPENDENCIES.md.

## Considerations

### C-01: WCB and Statutory Declaration Requirements at Closeout
RFP §2.13.2.3 requires both a WCB Letter of Clearance and a Statutory Declaration confirming no outstanding claims from Subcontractors at project close. These are prerequisites for the lien holdback release. The Design-Builder should ensure that Subcontractor WCB accounts are monitored throughout the project so that clearance is obtainable when needed — obtaining clearance from a non-compliant Subcontractor at the last moment is a known risk on construction projects.

Source: RFP §2.13.2.3.

### C-02: Deficiency Holdback and Guarantee Period
The Owner may retain a Deficiency Holdback against amounts owed until deficiencies are corrected (RFP §2.11). The guarantee period includes the construction period and a two (2) year warranty period following the CCC date (RFP §2.10.2). Deficiencies in Subcontractor work become the Design-Builder's obligation to rectify within 10 days of written instruction during the Guarantee Period (RFP §2.10.6). Subcontractor contracts should include back-to-back deficiency rectification obligations to manage the Design-Builder's exposure.

Source: RFP §2.10.2, §2.10.6, §2.11.

### C-03: Subcontractor Insurance Must Be Verified
The Design-Builder's own insurance (min. $5,000,000 general liability) must explicitly cover independent Subcontractors (RFP §4.2.1.2). Employer's liability must also cover Subcontractors at min. $5,000,000 per employee (RFP §4.2.4). Additionally, individual Subcontractors will typically carry their own insurance. The Design-Builder should verify Subcontractor insurance certificates before mobilisation to ensure coverage is in place and should track expiry dates to prevent lapses during the project. Minimum coverage types and limits to require from individual Subcontractors should be established (see Specification REQ-015).

Source: RFP §4.2.1.2, §4.2.4, §4.2.6; Specification REQ-003, REQ-015.

### C-04: County Project History of Subcontractors Is an Evaluation Factor
RFP §5.1.2 Item D notes that Subcontractors' positive or negative performance records on previous County projects are factored into proposal scoring. This suggests that Camrose County maintains awareness of subcontractor performance across projects. The Design-Builder should be attentive to this when selecting Subcontractors.

Source: RFP §5.1.2, Item D.

### C-05: Coordination with QC Management (DEL-019-04)
DEL-019-04 (Construction Quality Control) provides quality control feedback that directly informs subcontractor performance evaluation (_DEPENDENCIES.md). Subcontractor management and QC management processes should be designed to share relevant data — for example, non-conformance records generated by QC should feed directly into subcontractor performance assessments.

Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md; _CONTEXT.md.

### C-06: December 31, 2026 Hard Deadline
The project has a hard completion deadline of December 31, 2026 (RFP §3.1). Subcontractor scheduling, sequencing, and performance management must be conducted in the context of this non-negotiable deadline. Schedule performance (Criterion E, 15% weighting) and milestone delivery assurance (Criterion F, 10% weighting) are significant evaluation criteria, both of which are affected by subcontractor reliability.

Source: RFP §3.1, §5.1.2 Items E and F.

### C-07: Alberta OH&S Regulatory Framework for Prime Contractors
The Alberta Occupational Health and Safety Act establishes the Prime Contractor concept and imposes specific duties on Prime Contractors for work sites where multiple employers are present. ASSUMPTION: Under the Alberta OHS Act, the Prime Contractor is responsible for ensuring compliance by all employers and workers at the work site, establishing and maintaining a system for managing health and safety, and ensuring that the OHS requirements are coordinated between employers. The specific applicable provisions (Part and section numbers) of the Alberta OHS Act, Regulations, and Code should be confirmed with legal counsel and explicitly mapped to subcontractor management processes. This mapping remains incomplete in accessible project sources.

Source: RFP §2.15 (reference to Prime Contractor status as defined by Health and Safety agencies); ASSUMPTION: standard Alberta OH&S Prime Contractor duties. Enriched per _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md C-002.

### C-08: CCDC 14-2013 Non-Compliance and Termination Framework
The governing contract is CCDC 14-2013 Design-Build Stipulated Price Contract (RFP §2.7). This contract form contains provisions relevant to subcontractor non-compliance, including: subcontractor engagement requirements, change order processes, default and termination provisions, and dispute resolution mechanisms. ASSUMPTION: The CCDC 14-2013 contract likely establishes a framework for cure periods, corrective action requirements, and circumstances under which a subcontract may be terminated. The specific contract provisions are not accessible in current project sources (contract text is TBD — available post-award). The Design-Builder should review CCDC 14-2013 provisions and ensure subcontractor contract terms are consistent with the prime contract framework. This interpretation should be confirmed with legal counsel post-award.

Source: RFP §2.7; CCDC 14-2013 (specific provisions location TBD). Enriched per _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md B-005.

### C-09: Timing of Framework Establishment
The subcontractor management framework (Procedure Step 1) is to be completed "before any Subcontractor is engaged or invited to bid." For this project, this means the framework should be established between contract award and the first subcontract solicitation. Given the December 31, 2026 hard deadline and the need to mobilise subcontractors promptly after award, the framework establishment should be treated as a critical-path early activity. ASSUMPTION: The framework should be targeted for completion within the first two weeks post-award, before any RFQ/RFP documents are issued to potential subcontractors. The exact timeline should be anchored to the project schedule (which is TBD post-award).

Source: Procedure Step 1 timing; RFP §3.1 (completion deadline). Enriched per _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md D-002.

### C-10: Resource Allocation for Subcontractor Management
The Project Manager is identified as the responsible party for this deliverable (_CONTEXT.md). On a project with multiple concurrent trade packages (PKG-010 through PKG-018), day-to-day subcontractor oversight may exceed one person's capacity. Trade-off T-03 discusses centralised versus distributed monitoring. ASSUMPTION: If the Design-Builder delegates day-to-day subcontractor oversight to a Site Superintendent or trade supervisors, this delegation should be formally documented, and the delegated personnel should be identified in the project organizational chart. The Project Manager retains accountability regardless of delegation. The organizational structure submission (RFP §4.6.1, Evaluation Criterion A at 5% weighting) should reflect the subcontractor management accountability chain.

Source: _CONTEXT.md (Key Roles); RFP §4.6.1, §5.1.2 Item A. Enriched per _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md D-003.

### C-11: Performance Rating Scale
Specification REQ-013 requires performance monitoring but does not define a rating scale. The Subcontractor Register (Datasheet, Examples section) references "Performance rating (period-by-period)" as a field. ASSUMPTION: A standardised performance rating scale should be established as part of the Performance Monitoring Framework. A typical approach for construction subcontractor management would be a numeric or categorical scale (e.g., 1-5 or Exceeds/Meets/Below/Unacceptable) applied across each performance dimension (financial, safety, schedule, quality). The specific scale is TBD — to be established by the Design-Builder as part of the Performance Monitoring Framework (Procedure Step 1.3).

Source: Specification REQ-013; Procedure Step 1.3. Enriched per _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md F-003.

### C-12: Lessons Learned and Continuous Improvement
For a management deliverable tied to OBJ-007 (zero lost-time incidents), post-project review of subcontractor management effectiveness is a meaningful oversight activity. After project closeout (Procedure Step 7), the Design-Builder should capture lessons learned regarding: which qualification criteria proved most predictive of performance; which monitoring metrics were most useful; what non-compliance patterns emerged and how effectively they were addressed; and what improvements should be carried to future projects. ASSUMPTION: A lessons-learned review is standard practice for construction management deliverables, though it is not explicitly required by the RFP or Decomposition.

Source: OBJ-007 (Decomposition §5); standard construction management practice. Enriched per _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md E-003.

## Trade-offs

### T-01: Depth of Qualification vs. Speed of Mobilisation
More rigorous subcontractor pre-qualification (extensive reference checks, safety record reviews, financial health assessments) reduces risk but takes time and may delay mobilisation. Given the December 31, 2026 deadline, the Design-Builder must balance qualification thoroughness against mobilisation speed. ASSUMPTION: A tiered approach may be appropriate:

- **Tier 1 (high-value or high-risk trades):** Full qualification assessment including financial capacity review, safety record audit, detailed reference checks, and County project history review. ASSUMPTION: "High-value" could be defined as subcontracts exceeding a threshold percentage of total contract value (TBD), and "high-risk" as trades involving significant OH&S hazards (e.g., structural steel erection, excavation, work at heights, confined spaces, electrical).
- **Tier 2 (lower-risk engagements):** Streamlined qualification focusing on insurance verification, WCB standing, and basic reference check.

The tier definitions and threshold criteria are TBD — to be established by the Design-Builder. The cost/benefit of additional qualification rigour should be weighed against the risk profile of each trade and the potential cost of subcontractor failure (replacement cost, schedule delay, safety incident).

Source: Decomposition §1 (Location); RFP §3.1 (deadline). Enriched per _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md C-004.

### T-02: Subcontractor Contract Rigour vs. Market Availability
On a project in a rural Alberta location (near Ferintosh, SW 14-44-21-W4), the pool of available local Subcontractors may be limited. Imposing highly restrictive contract terms (aggressive penalties, extensive bond requirements) may deter available Subcontractors or increase subcontract pricing. The Design-Builder must weigh contractual protection against market realities. The specifics of subcontractor contract terms are TBD.

Source: Decomposition §1 (Location: SW 14-44-21-W4, near Ferintosh, Alberta).

### T-03: Centralised vs. Distributed Performance Monitoring
The Project Manager is the responsible party for this deliverable, but on a project of this scale with multiple trade packages, monitoring every Subcontractor centrally may be impractical. ASSUMPTION: The Design-Builder may delegate day-to-day subcontractor oversight to a Site Superintendent or individual trade supervisors while retaining central reporting and escalation processes through the Project Manager. This delegation should be formally documented (see Consideration C-10). This is not specified in accessible sources.
