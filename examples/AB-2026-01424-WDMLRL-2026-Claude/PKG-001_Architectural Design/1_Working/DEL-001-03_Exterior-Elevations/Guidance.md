# Guidance — DEL-001-03 Exterior Elevations

---

## Purpose

The Exterior Elevations drawing set exists to communicate the vertical expression and exterior composition of the New North Shop Addition to all parties: Owner, building permit authority, structural engineer, mechanical/plumbing/electrical engineers, and the construction team.

Within the design-build workflow for this project, the Exterior Elevations serve three specific functions:

1. **Permit and regulatory compliance record**: The elevations are part of the IFC drawing set submitted to the Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) for building permit review. They must depict sufficient information for the AHJ to assess code compliance including building height, egress door placement, and exterior wall assembly.

2. **Contractor construction reference**: The elevations give the General Contractor the exterior envelope dimensions, door rough opening locations, and interface conditions needed to set out formwork, frame openings, and sequence cladding work.

3. **Cross-discipline coordination anchor**: Elevation drawings serve as the geometric reference against which structural (crane rail heights, mezzanine level), mechanical (louver/exhaust penetration locations), plumbing (exterior mud sump, bulk water fill), and civil (finished grade) design is confirmed.

Sources: R-01 §1.0, §3.3.2; Decomposition OBJ-001, OBJ-003; SOW-0011, SOW-0018.

## Principles

### P-01: Completeness before submission
The elevation set must be complete across all four faces of the addition before inclusion in the IFC package. Partial elevation sets create permitting risk and downstream coordination failures. Each face must show at minimum: all openings, grade line, finished floor line, wall top/parapet, materials, and dimensions.
Source: R-01 §3.3.2 (complete final design, all IFC drawings signed/stamped). SOW-0011, SOW-0018.

### P-02: Dimensional consistency across all architectural drawings
Elevations, floor plans, sections, and schedules must be dimensionally consistent. Opening widths on elevations must match floor plan callouts and Door & Window Schedule entries. Heights must match section cuts. Discrepancies between drawings are a common construction deficiency source.
Source: OBJ-003 (fully coordinated design across disciplines); SOW-0011.

### P-03: 35-foot ceiling height governs wall height
The 35' interior ceiling height is an explicit design requirement stated in both the RFP and the conceptual drawings. This height is driven by the operational requirement to accommodate two 5-tonne overhead cranes on trolley plus the crane structure above. The exterior wall height on the elevations must be consistent with structural drawings and the crane support structure. The architect must confirm minimum clear height under crane hook versus finished floor.
Source: R-01 §3.4; R-04 (Appendix B notes — "Concrete Structure (35' Ceiling)", "(2) 5 Ton Cranes").

### P-04: Wash bay and mud sump are exterior program elements
The wash bay mud sump is an exterior building element — it sits on the exterior face of the building for excavator cleanout access. Its location on the elevation is not cosmetic; it must be constructable and accessible post-construction. The elevation must show clearance for excavator approach and should not have conflicting site elements blocking access.
Source: R-01 §3.4; R-04 (mud sump shown on east/exterior side of wash bay).

### P-05: Interface with existing Old North Shop requires explicit resolution
The New North Shop Addition (130' × 100') is being added adjacent to the existing Old North Shop (105' × 40'). The face where these two buildings meet or connect is an architecturally complex interface. The elevation of this face must show whether there is a shared wall, an expansion joint, a weather seal, or an open connection. This decision drives both the structural design and the weatherproofing strategy. The interface must be resolved at design stage, not left to field interpretation.
Source: R-01 §3.1; R-04 (combined plan shows both buildings on same drawing, total 235' width).

### P-06: Separate entrance and exit
The RFP explicitly requires separate entrance and exit doors. This is likely an operational safety requirement for a heavy equipment maintenance facility (to prevent pedestrian/vehicle conflict at door openings). The elevations must show these as physically separate openings at clearly distinct locations. Their placement should be coordinated with the traffic flow and site layout.
Source: R-01 §3.1.

### P-07: Alberta Building Code compliance is non-negotiable
The building must comply with all applicable Alberta Building Codes and Safety Codes. For exterior elevations, this includes: accessible entrance provisions, egress door quantity and swing, exterior wall fire resistance where applicable (proximity to property lines or adjacent buildings), and exterior finish material restrictions (if any apply to the occupancy class and construction type).
Source: R-01 §3.3.2, §3.4; SOW-0008, SOW-0009.

## Considerations

### C-01: Crane height implications for wall height and roofline
The 5-tonne overhead cranes on trolley (2 units) require crane rails and end stops above the repair bay floor. The structural engineer will determine crane rail height based on crane geometry and hook coverage requirements. The architect must confirm that the wall height on the exterior elevations accommodates the structural crane support system with the required clearances, and that this is visible on the elevations where the crane columns or wall connections appear.
Source: R-01 §3.4; R-04; SOW-0067; DEL-002-07 (Crane Support Structure Details).

### C-02: Overhead door sizing for motor scraper
The wash bay must accommodate a motor scraper, which is a large piece of heavy construction equipment. The overhead door for the wash bay must be sized accordingly. Typical motor scrapers require door openings in the range of 20-24' wide and 18-22' tall, but the exact size must be confirmed by the architect based on equipment specifications provided by the County (ASSUMPTION — specific equipment model not stated in RFP; County to provide specs or confirm). This sizing directly affects what is shown on the exterior elevation.
Source: R-01 §3.1, §3.4 ("Single enclosed bay dedicated for washing large equipment such as a motor scraper").

### C-03: Three-phase power service entry location
The building runs on three-phase power (R-01 §3.4). The electrical service entry point (meter base, distribution panel) will likely appear as an exterior penetration or exterior-mounted equipment on one face of the building. The architect should coordinate with the electrical engineer (PKG-004) to ensure the service entry location is reflected on the elevation if it involves a wall penetration or exterior panel.
Source: R-01 §3.4; SOW-0051; DEL-004-02 (Single-Line Diagram).

### C-04: Mechanical louvers and exhaust penetrations
The building contains a high-recovery heating system, high-volume air exchanger, forced-air makeup air system, and welding/equipment exhaust systems (R-01 §3.4; R-04). These mechanical systems require exterior louvers, exhaust terminations, and fresh air intakes. Their locations on the exterior faces should be coordinated between the architect and mechanical engineer (PKG-003) and shown on the elevations as exterior penetrations or equipment.
Source: R-01 §3.4; SOW-0035–0039; DEL-003-04 (Exhaust System Plans).

### C-05: Exterior gravel and cement pad interface
The conceptual drawing (Appendix B) shows gravel and cement pads on the exterior of the building. The relationship between these pads and the building face (i.e., does the pad come flush to the wall, is there a curb, is there a transition?) should be shown on the elevation where relevant, coordinated with the civil drawings.
Source: R-04; SOW-0078, SOW-0079.

### C-06: Alberta climate exposure — design implications
The site is in Central Alberta (Camrose County), approximately 30 km south of Camrose. Alberta climate is characterized by significant temperature swings, snow loading, and wind. While specific structural loads are the structural engineer's responsibility, the architect should ensure that exterior material and detailing selections on the elevations are appropriate for this climate. This includes freeze-thaw durable cladding, roof drainage design for snow, and protection of large overhead door openings (heated vestibules are TBD — not specified in RFP).
Source: R-01 §1.0 (location); Alberta Building Code climate data (location TBD).

## Trade-offs

### T-01: Level of exterior finish detail versus industrial facility program
This is an industrial maintenance facility for heavy landfill equipment. Elaborate exterior finishes are not indicated by the RFP or conceptual drawings. The design should achieve code-compliant, weather-tight, durable construction appropriate for the program. Pursuing architectural cladding beyond what the program warrants may consume design fee and construction budget without Owner benefit. The architect should default to functional, durable, low-maintenance exterior materials unless the Owner specifically requests otherwise.
Source: R-01 §3.1 (program description is purely functional); no RFP requirement for architectural expression.

**Rationale gap (Ref: Lensing Item A-006):** The statement "functional, durable, low-maintenance" establishes design intent but does not provide criteria for what constitutes acceptable quality for this facility type. For traceability, the Architect and Owner should explicitly link the industrial program to specific durability and maintenance expectations, such as:
- Expected cladding service life relative to building design life
- Acceptable maintenance frequency (e.g., no exterior repainting within first 15 years)
- Resistance to operational environment (equipment exhaust, road splash, abrasion from equipment maneuvering)
These criteria should be established before exterior material selection (see Specification — Exterior Material Durability Acceptance Criteria section). **PROPOSAL:** Architect + Owner to determine.

### T-02: Shared wall with Old North Shop — new wall vs. penetration of existing
At the interface between the new addition and the existing Old North Shop, there is a design decision to be made: is the connection a new full-height wall (with or without openings), or is there an opening cut into the existing building wall? This decision has structural, fire, weatherproofing, and cost implications. The conceptual floor plan suggests connectivity between the two buildings (the plan shows a continuous interior space) but does not detail the structural/architectural interface. This must be resolved in the design and shown explicitly on the elevation.
Source: R-04 (conceptual plan); SOW-0011, SOW-0070–SOW-0074 (renovation scope).

### T-02A: Preliminary vs. IFC elevation drawing quality expectations (Ref: Lensing Item D-003)
The Procedure includes both a County preliminary review stage (Step 9) and an IFC finalization stage (Step 10). There is currently no guidance on what differentiates a preliminary elevation from an IFC elevation in terms of drawing quality and content completeness. For this deliverable:

- **Preliminary elevation (County review):** Should show overall building geometry, all openings in approximate locations, grade line, and building height. Material keynotes may reference "TBD — see Finish Schedule." Line weights and annotation density may be at design development level. The purpose is to confirm the building form and massing with the Owner before committing to final detailing.
- **IFC elevation (for construction and permit):** Must show all content required by the Specification requirements (REQ-001 through REQ-012 and REQ-NEW-01 through REQ-NEW-04), with full dimensions, confirmed material keynotes, coordination notes resolved, and P.Eng. stamp. Line weights, annotation density, and keynote conventions must conform to the Architect's office drawing standard (currently TBD — see Procedure, Tools and Resources).

**PROPOSAL:** Architect to confirm quality gate criteria for preliminary vs. IFC elevation completeness.

### T-03: Early exterior envelope resolution reduces coordination risk
The longer the exterior envelope (wall height, cladding system, opening sizes) remains undefined, the higher the risk of downstream discipline rework. Structural, mechanical, and civil designs all depend on envelope geometry. Early resolution of the elevation design is recommended even if finish materials remain TBD temporarily.
Source: OBJ-003 (fully coordinated design); SOW-0018 (IFC drawings required).
