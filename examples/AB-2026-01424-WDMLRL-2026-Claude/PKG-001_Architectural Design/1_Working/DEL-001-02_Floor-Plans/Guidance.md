# Guidance — DEL-001-02 Architectural Floor Plans

---

## Purpose

DEL-001-02 exists to provide the definitive spatial layout of the New North Shop addition — establishing room locations, dimensions, traffic flow, equipment placement zones, and floor features that all other disciplines must coordinate against. In a design-build context (CCDC 14–2013), the architectural floor plan is the primary document that translates the Owner's operational program into a buildable spatial arrangement. All subsequent discipline drawings (structural, mechanical, electrical, plumbing) derive their layouts from this plan.

The Owner (Camrose County) has provided a concept drawing (Appendix B — Shop) that establishes the intent but does not constitute a design. The Architect's role is to transform that concept into a code-compliant, fully dimensioned, construction-ready floor plan set that passes building permit review and ultimately receives IFC (Issued for Construction) sign-off.

> Source context: RFP §3.4 ("The County only has a concept in mind; therefore, it will be a design/build project."); RFP §3.1 (program description); Decomp OBJ-001, OBJ-003.

---

## Principles

### P-1 — Operational program drives layout
The floor plan must satisfy the County's maintenance shop operational program as described in RFP §3.1 and §3.4. Large equipment (motor scraper-class) drives dimensional decisions: door widths (24'), bay lengths, turning radii, and the wash bay sizing. The service pit location must permit under-equipment access for the largest expected equipment type. The Architect should not reduce programmatic spaces below the RFP-stated minimums (e.g., 400 sq ft parts room, 6' roll-up door, 24' bay widths, 24' wash bay width) without Owner approval.
> Source: RFP §3.1, §3.4; R-04.

### P-2 — Conceptual drawing is intent, not specification
Appendix B (R-04) establishes programmatic intent and approximate dimensions (130' × 100', 24' bays, 24' wash bay, etc.) but is a conceptual sketch, not a design. The Architect should use it as a program guide and departure point. Spatial relationships shown (e.g., mezzanine over parts room, utility room, and wash bay) are Owner-preferred configuration and should be preserved unless a code or structural constraint requires deviation, which must be documented.
> Source: RFP §3.4 (County has a concept only).

### P-3 — IFC quality from the outset
Because this is a design-build project with a hard December 31, 2026 deadline, the floor plan should be developed to IFC quality efficiently, without multiple mid-stage rework cycles. The preliminary design (DEL-001-01) provides the County approval gate; once past that gate, the Architect should move directly to complete IFC drawings. Time spent on intermediate non-IFC quality documents that require full rework is not warranted given the schedule.
> Source: RFP §3.1 (deadline); Decomp OBJ-002, OBJ-003.

### P-4 — Cross-discipline coordination is mandatory before IFC
The floor plan is the spatial anchor for all disciplines. Before IFC, the Architect must confirm that structural column grid, mechanical equipment zones, electrical room/panel locations, crane runway positions, and plumbing routing are not in conflict with the architectural layout. The service pit, mezzanine, and overhead crane runway are areas of highest multi-discipline interface risk.
> Source: RFP §3.3.2 (fully coordinated IFC drawings required); Decomp OBJ-003.

### P-5 — Code compliance governs minimum spatial decisions
The Alberta Building Code governs occupancy classification, means of egress widths and travel distances, accessible design (where triggered), and fire separations. The Architect must perform a code analysis before finalizing the floor plan layout, particularly for the mixed-use nature of the building (industrial shop, office, washroom, mezzanine storage). Any code-required separations (fire walls, rated assemblies) must be shown on the floor plan.
> Source: RFP §3.3.2, §3.4; Decomp SOW-0008, SOW-0009; Alberta Building Code (ASSUMPTION: likely applicable — edition TBD).

## Considerations

### C-1 — Heavy equipment clearances in repair bays and wash bay
The two repair bays (24' wide each) and wash bay (24' wide) are sized for large construction equipment including motor scrapers. The floor plan must ensure sufficient longitudinal depth in the bays for the equipment to fully enter and, in the case of drive-through bays, fully exit. The conceptual drawing shows overhead doors at both ends of the repair bays, confirming drive-through intent.
> Source: RFP §3.4 ("single enclosed bay dedicated for washing large equipment such as a motor scraper"); R-04; Decomp SOW-0025, SOW-0027a.

### C-2 — Mezzanine extent and structural interface
The mezzanine spans over the parts room, utility room, and wash bay (per Appendix B and RFP §3.4). Because the mezzanine must be load-bearing for heavy items (oil totes, oil pumping equipment), the structural design (DEL-002-05) will impose column or beam locations that the floor plan must accommodate. The Architect should establish the mezzanine boundary on the floor plan early and share with the Structural Engineer before structural framing is resolved.
> Source: RFP §3.4; R-04; Decomp SOW-0032, SOW-0033.

### C-3 — Service pit location relative to crane runway
The service pit/trench (below-grade) and the overhead crane runways (above) are both located in the main shop area. Their positions must be coordinated: the crane hook radius must be able to reach the service pit for lifting purposes, while the pit walls and cover structure must not conflict with the crane runway beam supports. This coordination requires early alignment between DEL-001-02 (floor plan), DEL-002-06 (service pit structural details), and DEL-002-07 (crane support structure details).
> Source: R-04; Decomp SOW-0028, SOW-0067.

### C-4 — Steel plate locations in floor
Steel plates embedded in the concrete floor are shown in the conceptual drawing at multiple locations throughout the main shop and wash bay. The Architect should establish plate zones on the floor plan in coordination with the Structural Engineer (DEL-002-08 — Steel Plate Embedment Plan). The location criteria are: where tracked and packer-type heavy equipment will operate.
> Source: RFP §3.4 ("steel plating in concrete at strategic locations to accommodate track and packer type heavy equipment"); R-04; Decomp SOW-0024.

### C-5 — Exterior mud sump and building envelope penetration
The wash bay drains connect to an exterior mud sump that must be accessible by an excavator for cleanout. The floor plan must show the wash bay drain outlet and coordinate its penetration through the building envelope with the structural and plumbing drawings. The exterior mud sump location should be shown on the floor plan or referenced to the civil/site plan.
> Source: RFP §3.4; R-04; Decomp SOW-0027b.

### C-6 — 50,000 L cistern placement
The 50,000 L cistern is a large in-building water storage element (shown adjacent to the utility room in Appendix B). Its placement affects structural floor loading, plumbing routing, and access clearances. The Architect should confirm cistern dimensions with the Plumbing Engineer (DEL-006-04) before finalizing its location on the floor plan.
> Source: RFP §3.4; R-04; Decomp SOW-0041.

### C-7 — Old North Shop interface and renovation coordination
The New North Shop addition abuts the Old North Shop (105' × 40'). The floor plan must show the interface accurately — the new building's west wall alignment with the existing building — and must not conflict with any existing structure to remain. The renovation drawings (DEL-001-09, DEL-001-10) will address the Old North Shop interior; the floor plan for the addition should show the junction condition and any new openings between buildings, if applicable.
> Source: R-04; Decomp SOW-0070–SOW-0074.

### C-9 — Building envelope thermal performance and wall thickness
The floor plan must accommodate wall assembly thicknesses driven by insulation requirements for Alberta's climate. Wall assemblies in central Alberta (Climate Zone 7A or 7B) typically require substantial insulation depth, which affects interior clear dimensions and spatial clearances. The Architect should establish wall assembly thickness assumptions early in the floor plan layout process, as the difference between a minimal and a high-performance wall assembly can consume several inches of interior space on each perimeter wall. This consideration is particularly relevant for the 130' x 100' footprint, where interior useable area is measured against the "approximately 13,000 sq ft" threshold (REQ-001).
> Source: Datasheet.md Attributes (Structure type); Lensing item F-003.
> **ASSUMPTION:** Wall assembly thickness for Alberta climate will be significant but specific insulation standard / R-value requirement is TBD pending confirmation of applicable ABC edition and any Owner energy performance targets.

### C-10 — Discipline coordination sequencing
The Architect should consider the critical-path sequencing of discipline coordination requests for the floor plan. Not all discipline inputs carry equal urgency for the floor plan layout:
- **Critical-path inputs (needed before layout can be finalized):** Structural column grid and crane support beam positions (determines bay spacing and spatial boundaries); mechanical MUA unit footprint (determines equipment zone size).
- **Second-priority inputs (needed before IFC but after initial layout):** Plumbing cistern dimensions and drain routing; electrical panel/switchgear space requirements; ceiling fan locations.
- **Concurrent inputs (can proceed in parallel with floor plan production):** Mechanical exhaust routing details; electrical lighting zones; plumbing fixture connections.

Sequencing the coordination requests in this order reduces rework and accelerates the path to IFC. See Procedure Steps 2, 3, and 6 for the procedural execution of this coordination.
> Source: Guidance.md P-4; Procedure.md Steps 2, 3, 6; Lensing item C-003.
> **ASSUMPTION:** The sequencing above reflects standard architectural coordination practice. The Architect of Record may adjust based on project-specific circumstances (e.g., long-lead-time items, consultant availability).

### C-8 — Separate entrance and exit doors
The RFP explicitly requires separate entrance/exit doors for the addition. The floor plan must show these locations and confirm code-compliant egress paths from all spaces, including the mezzanine (via the stairs shown in Appendix B).
> Source: RFP §3.1; Decomp SOW-0026.

### C-11 — Lifecycle maintenance access and future equipment changeout
The floor plan establishes spatial relationships that affect the building's long-term operational value. Layout decisions — bay access clearances, service pit positioning, mezzanine access width, overhead door sizing, and circulation paths — determine whether future equipment can be replaced, upgraded, or serviced without structural modifications. The Architect should consider:
- Whether the drive-through bay depth and door heights accommodate foreseeable equipment size increases over the building lifecycle.
- Whether the mezzanine access (stairs) and loading areas allow for future oil tote and pumping equipment replacement without crane-only access.
- Whether the service pit dimensions accommodate future equipment wheelbase changes.
- Whether electrical and mechanical rough-in zones have adequate clearance for future capacity additions.

The December 31, 2026 deadline (RFP ss3.1) creates schedule pressure, but the floor plan is the one-time opportunity to establish spatial relationships that will govern the building's usefulness for decades.
> Source: Lensing item D-002; RFP ss3.1 (deadline); Decomp OBJ-001.
> **ASSUMPTION:** No specific lifecycle design criteria or future expansion requirements are stated in the RFP or Appendix B. This is a best-practice consideration.

## Vocabulary Notes

### V-1 — "Service pit" vs. "Service Pit"
The below-grade service element in the main shop area is referred to variably across documents as "service pit," "Service Pit," and "service pit/trench." The RFP ss3.4 and Appendix B (R-04) use both terms. For cross-document consistency and traceability, the primary term adopted across this deliverable document set is **"service pit"** with "Service Pit" as a parenthetical alternative on first use. References: Specification.md REQ-012; Datasheet.md Spatial Program; Procedure.md Step 3.5.
> Source: Lensing item E-002.

### V-2 — "Concrete structure" (structural system type)
Datasheet.md Attributes states "Structure type: Concrete structure" (from RFP ss3.4, R-04). However, Specification and Guidance discuss steel plates embedded in concrete floor, overhead crane runways (typically supported by steel framing), and mezzanine elements (which commonly use steel framing). The term "concrete structure" in the Datasheet likely refers to the floor slab and foundation system, not the overall structural framing system. The Architect and Structural Engineer should confirm and clarify the structural system type designation. Until confirmed, "concrete structure" should be understood as referring to the floor/foundation system, with the overall structural framing system TBD.
> Source: Datasheet.md Attributes (Structure type); Specification.md REQ-015; Guidance.md C-4; Lensing item B-003.

## Trade-offs

### T-1 — Mezzanine over wash bay: operational access vs. structural depth
The mezzanine extends over the wash bay. Heavy equipment entering the wash bay is 35' ceiling-constrained at the main shop level, but the mezzanine underside will reduce clearance over the wash bay. The Architect and Structural Engineer must determine a mezzanine slab/floor elevation that provides adequate wash bay operational clearance for the intended equipment while maximizing mezzanine storage height. This is a design optimization decision with no pre-determined answer from the available sources.
> ASSUMPTION: No specific minimum underside clearance for the wash bay mezzanine is stated in the RFP or Appendix B. The Architect and Owner should confirm an acceptable minimum.

### T-2 — Bay depth vs. overall building footprint
The 130' × 100' footprint is drawn from the conceptual drawing (R-04) and may be treated as a target rather than a hard constraint, depending on engineering and code requirements. However, deviating from it (especially increasing footprint) has site, permit, and cost implications. The Architect should confirm with the Owner whether the 130' × 100' is a firm limit or a guideline.
> ASSUMPTION: The 130' × 100' conceptual dimension is the Owner's intent. Treating it as a hard constraint is the conservative approach unless the Owner instructs otherwise.

### T-3 — Parts room security vs. circulation flow
The parts room requires a secure 400 sq ft area with a 6' roll-up door. Its location within the circulation flow of the main shop affects how parts are accessed during maintenance operations. The Appendix B layout shows the parts room adjacent to the office and utility room, separated from the main bay area — this is a reasonable arrangement that the Architect should evaluate against typical maintenance shop workflow.
> Source: R-04; RFP §3.4.

## Examples

### Conceptual floor plan (Appendix B — R-04)
The conceptual drawing provides the following spatial arrangement (Architect to verify and finalize):
- Two repair bays with overhead crane runways running north-south through the main shop
- Wash bay at the east end with a 24' width, overhead door, and steel plate floor; mezzanine above
- Parts room and utility room in the southeast zone; mezzanine above
- Office in the central-south zone
- Washroom adjacent to the parts room / office zone
- Service Pit running east-west in the main shop area
- 50,000 L cistern located at the east end, adjacent to utility room
- Stairs to mezzanine at the utility room / cistern zone
- Welding station at the north end of the main shop
- Work benches along north and east walls
- Steel plate locations distributed throughout main shop and wash bay
- Exterior mud sump shown to the east of the wash bay (outside building envelope)
- Old North Shop shown to the west (105' × 40')
- Cement pad to the east of the addition; gravel pad to the north/west

> This spatial arrangement is derived from R-04. It is the conceptual baseline, not a confirmed design. The Architect's final floor plan is the controlling document.
