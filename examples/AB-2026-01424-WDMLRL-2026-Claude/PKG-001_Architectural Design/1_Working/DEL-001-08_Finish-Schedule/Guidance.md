## Purpose

The Finish Schedule (DEL-001-08) serves three interrelated purposes within the WDMLRL Maintenance Shop Addition project:

1. **Design communication:** It translates the Architect's finish intent into a structured tabular format that can be read by contractors, subtrades, and the Owner without ambiguity.
2. **Coordination anchor:** It provides a single reference point against which the Floor Plans (DEL-001-02), Interior Elevations (DEL-001-05), Reflected Ceiling Plans (DEL-001-06), and Architectural Specification (DEL-001-11) are all cross-checked during IFC document production.
3. **Owner approval scaffold:** In a design-build context (CCDC 14-2013), finish selections are part of the design-builder's scope. The Finish Schedule surfaces these selections for explicit County review — particularly for finishes in staff-occupied spaces (office, washroom / locker-change room, kitchenette) where aesthetic and functional expectations may be more subjective.

Source: RFP S1.0 (design-build scope), S3.1 (program), S3.3.2 (IFC requirements), CCDC 14-2013 (R-01 S2.7).

## Principles

### P-01: Industrial-First Specification Strategy
The dominant use of this building is heavy equipment maintenance. The Finish Schedule should be developed with industrial durability as the primary selection criterion for the main shop areas (Main Shop repair bays, Wash Bay, Service Pit). Aesthetic considerations are secondary in these areas.
- Source: RFP S3.1 ("washroom, office space, two drive-through bays, one wash-bay, a parts storage room, utility room, mezzanine storage, and separate industrial wash sink"), S3.4.
- Practical implication: For the Main Shop and Wash Bay, specify finishes from categories specifically tested for industrial use (sealed concrete, epoxy broadcast systems, epoxy paint) before considering architectural finish-grade products.

### P-02: Design-Build Finish Selection Accountability
In a CCDC 14-2013 design-build, the Architect has latitude to select finish products within the program requirements. This latitude comes with accountability: finish selections must be defensible against the County's operational needs as described in the RFP. The Finish Schedule is part of the Architect's design-build deliverable and is subject to County approval at Preliminary Design.
- Source: RFP S3.3.2 ("Deliver Preliminary Design for the County project team to approve"); S3.3.2 ("Complete final design").
- Practical implication: Document rationale for non-obvious finish choices (e.g., why a particular epoxy system over a hardener, or why a suspended ceiling in the office vs. open).

### P-03: Minimize Finish Types — Simplicity and Maintainability
A landfill maintenance shop is operated by County staff, not a facilities management company. Finishes should be specifiable in few distinct types, easy to reorder locally (Edmonton/Camrose area markets), and easy to patch or replace in kind over a 20+ year building life.
- Source: ASSUMPTION — consistent with County institutional practice for rural facilities; no direct RFP clause, but supported by design-build value engineering principle.
- **Rationale for 20+ year lifecycle assumption (per lensing item F-003):** The 20+ year figure is an ASSUMPTION based on typical institutional building lifecycle expectations for permanent municipal facilities in Alberta. The RFP does not state an expected building lifecycle. If the County's institutional asset management policy specifies a different lifecycle (e.g., 30-40 years for a permanent public facility), the durability calculus for finish selection may change — particularly for epoxy coating lifecycle, tile grout maintenance intervals, and FRP panel replacement timing. **Action required:** Confirm building lifecycle expectation with County or reference Camrose County institutional asset management policy, if available.
- Practical implication: Avoid exotic or proprietary products without local distribution. Prefer generic or semi-generic specifications (e.g., "two-part epoxy floor coating, minimum 20 mil DFT") over single-manufacturer proprietary specs.

### P-04: Wet Area Non-Negotiability
Wet area finishes (Wash Bay, Washroom / Locker-Change Room / Laundry, Kitchenette) must be appropriate for moisture without compromise. Under-specification of wet area finishes leads to early substrate deterioration and costly remediation. These areas should be specified conservatively.
- Source: RFP S3.4 ("Expand washroom facilities to include urinal and locker change room including laundry services"); App B (Wash Bay, Washroom); design principle.
- This principle is reinforced by the addition of REQ-DS-006a (waterproofing membrane) in the Specification, which elevates the membrane requirement from procedural guidance to a normative requirement.

### P-05: Code Compliance Is Non-Negotiable
The Alberta Building Code (NBC 2019 Alberta Edition, or as amended at time of permit) and Alberta Safety Codes are mandatory. The Finish Schedule must not specify materials that would fail code in any of the occupied spaces. This principle now requires an explicit occupancy classification determination (REQ-DS-007a) as the foundation for all code-driven finish requirements.
- Source: RFP S3.3.2 ("The building design must adhere to all applicable building codes and regulations"; "The proposed building must adhere to all Alberta Safety Codes").

## Considerations

### C-01: Room-to-Room ID Coordination
The single most common cause of finish schedule disputes during construction is inconsistent room numbering between the floor plan and the finish schedule. The Architect should establish room IDs on DEL-001-02 first and then use those same IDs verbatim in the Finish Schedule. Any subsequent floor plan revision that changes room IDs must trigger a Finish Schedule revision.

### C-02: Concrete Slab Specification Complexity — Main Shop
The Main Shop slab is not a simple polished concrete situation. It has embedded steel plates (per RFP S3.4 and App B), a Service Pit, and sump drains (RFP S3.4). The Finish Schedule must clearly delineate:
- Slab areas with sealed/epoxy finish
- Steel plate zones (annotate as "steel plate — no finish coating" or "steel plate — specify weld pattern/edge treatment per structural," per REQ-DS-004 clarification)
- Service Pit cover (specify cover type, finish, and load rating per REQ-DS-003a — this is a walking/driving surface in a heavy industrial shop and must be addressed as a distinct schedule element)
- Sump drain locations (typically coordinated with mechanical/plumbing — Finish Schedule only notes floor finish around drains)
This level of detail prevents conflicts during construction.

### C-03: 35-Foot Ceiling in Main Shop
At 35 ft clear height, the Main Shop has no concealed ceiling — the underside of the roof structure will be the "ceiling." The Finish Schedule should explicitly state "Exposed to Structure — See Structural" (or painted steel/concrete as applicable) for A-01 ceiling, and cross-reference DEL-001-06 Reflected Ceiling Plans. This avoids interpretation as a suspended ceiling.
- Source: RFP S3.4 ("Concrete structure 35' ceiling"); App B.

### C-04: Mezzanine Floor Finish and Structural Compatibility
The mezzanine must support heavy items (oil totes, pumping equipment per RFP S3.4). If the Architect specifies a floor topping (e.g., epoxy, thin concrete topping), this adds dead load to the mezzanine structure. The floor finish specification must be coordinated with the structural design (DEL-002) to confirm the structure accounts for the finish dead load. Per REQ-DS-010 and lensing item X-003, structural engineer written confirmation is required before finish selection is finalized.

### C-05: Old North Shop Renovation — Unknown Substrate Conditions
The Old North Shop renovation areas (B-01 through B-04) involve working within an existing building. The substrate conditions (existing concrete slab condition, existing wall/ceiling construction) are not fully documented in the available sources. Finish selections for these areas carry a substrate risk — the Architect should note this in the schedule and anticipate a site-investigation verification step after project award.
- Source: RFP S3.2 (Mandatory Site Meeting — provides opportunity to observe existing building); App B.
- Per lensing item X-002, the site investigation evidence standard (visual inspection, destructive testing, moisture testing) must be defined before the investigation. See REQ-DS-011 acceptance criteria.

### C-06: Laundry Room in Washroom / Locker-Change Room
The Washroom / Locker-Change Room includes laundry services (RFP S3.4). A laundry area has specific requirements: floor drain, moisture-resistant finishes from floor to ceiling, and wall finishes that tolerate cleaning agent splash. These requirements are more demanding than a standard locker room and should drive finish selection for area A-07/B-04 to the higher wet-area standard.
- **Terminology note:** Per the project terminology convention (lensing item F-002), this area is referred to by the canonical name "Washroom / Locker-Change Room" throughout the document set. The laundry function is a sub-function within this area. If the Architect determines during design development that the laundry warrants a separate room with a distinct area ID, the Finish Schedule and Datasheet shall be updated accordingly.

### C-07: Wash Bay Mud Sump Interface
The Wash Bay exterior mud sump (App B) is an exterior element cleaned by excavator. The floor drain connection within the Wash Bay to the exterior sump means the Wash Bay floor finish must be sloped and terminated correctly at the drain. The Finish Schedule should note slope direction (toward drain, minimum 2% per REQ-DS-005a) and the finish termination condition at the exterior wall opening. Coordination with plumbing (PKG-006) is required.

### C-08: Welding Station Wall and Ceiling Finishes
The Welding Station is located within the Main Shop but requires specific consideration for spatter protection and fire safety. The wall and ceiling finish in the immediate welding zone should be non-combustible (per Alberta Building Code requirements for welding areas) and easy to maintain/clean. This may mean a localized departure from the Main Shop wall finish if the Main Shop walls are painted metal panel or similar combustible-rated assembly.
- Source: RFP S3.4 ("Ventilation at welding station"); ASSUMPTION: ABC non-combustible finish requirements apply in welding zone.

### C-09: Occupancy Classification as Foundation for Code Requirements
The occupancy classification under the Alberta Building Code is the single most important determinant of which flame spread, smoke development, and material restrictions apply to each area. Until the occupancy classification is determined (REQ-DS-007a), several Specification requirements (REQ-DS-008, REQ-DS-009) cannot be populated with specific numeric thresholds. The Architect should prioritize occupancy classification determination early in design development.
- Source: Lensing item A-001; general ABC practice.

## Trade-offs

### T-01: Epoxy Floor Coating vs. Concrete Hardener — Main Shop
**Option A — Epoxy coating:** Higher initial cost; provides excellent chemical resistance and a cleanable, non-porous surface; can be re-coated. Vulnerable to delamination under heavy impact or if applied to green concrete.
**Option B — Penetrating hardener/densifier:** Lower cost; integral with slab; not a separate layer so no delamination risk; less chemical resistance than epoxy; appearance more variable.
**Option C — No finish (raw concrete):** Lowest cost; practical precedent in many rural shop facilities; least durable for contaminant resistance.
- **Recommendation (ASSUMPTION):** For a 35 ft clear span main shop in a design-build context with a fixed completion date, a penetrating densifier + sealer may offer the best risk-adjusted value: no delamination concern, faster schedule, local availability. An epoxy broadcast system is appropriate for the Wash Bay where chemical resistance is most critical. Final selection is the Architect's design-build judgment call.

### T-02: Suspended Ceiling vs. Open Ceiling — Office and Parts Room
A suspended T-bar ceiling in the office provides acoustic separation, conceals mechanical/electrical runs, and provides a finished appearance. It adds cost and requires access for maintenance. An open-to-structure painted ceiling eliminates the access issue and reduces cost.
- **Recommendation (ASSUMPTION):** Given the industrial context and County's likely preference for low-maintenance solutions, open painted ceiling is reasonable for utility/parts areas. A modest suspended ceiling in the office only is reasonable for staff comfort. Confirm with County at Preliminary Design.

### T-03: Ceramic Tile vs. Fibre-Reinforced Plastic (FRP) Panels — Washroom / Locker-Change Room Walls
Ceramic tile is the conventional wet-area wall finish; durable but requires grout maintenance. FRP panels (e.g., Glasliner or equivalent) are common in industrial wet areas: faster to install, fewer grout joints, easier to clean, lower cost. In an industrial shop context, FRP may outperform ceramic tile for long-term maintainability.
- **Recommendation (ASSUMPTION):** FRP panels for the washroom / locker-change room and laundry walls are worth specifying consideration for this project type. Ceramic tile is a reasonable alternative for the floor where abrasion resistance is needed.
