# Guidance — DEL-014-04 Floor Drains & Sump Drains

---

## Purpose

Floor drains and sump drains are required to manage water from heavy equipment washing, vehicle maintenance, incidental spills, and cleaning operations throughout the New North Shop addition. They protect the facility from water damage, support worker safety (no standing water on floors), and enable compliance with environmental and plumbing codes.

The drain system has two functional zones:
1. **Shop floor / repair bay drains:** collect maintenance water, wash-down water, and incidental fluids and route them through the building drain/vent system to the septic holding tank (DEL-014-02). Source: R-06 (App B — Plumbing), R-01 S3.4.
2. **Wash bay floor drain:** collects heavy equipment wash water and routes it to the exterior mud sump (DEL-018-05), which is designed to be cleaned out by excavator. Source: R-01 S3.4 ("Mud sump from wash bay on exterior of building for clean out with excavator"); App B — Plumbing.

**Rationale for drainage path separation:** The separation of these two drainage paths serves both regulatory and operational purposes. The septic holding tank (DEL-014-02) is designed for domestic-grade wastewater and general floor wash water. Wash bay effluent — which carries heavy sediment loads, oil, grease, and contaminants from large equipment washing — must be directed to the mud sump (DEL-018-05) where solids can settle and be periodically removed by excavator. If the drainage paths are inadvertently cross-connected, the consequences include: (a) sediment and oil loading could overwhelm the septic system, leading to costly remediation and potential environmental violations under Alberta regulations; (b) the mud sump would fail to capture the high-sediment effluent it is designed for; and (c) the septic holding tank's capacity (2,000 US Gallons per R-01 S3.4) could be exceeded by combined flows. The Plumbing Engineer should confirm that the IFC design maintains clear separation between these paths. Source: R-01 S3.4; App B — Plumbing; ASSUMPTION regarding regulatory and operational consequences. [Enrichment: A-005 — drainage path separation rationale added.]

This deliverable (DEL-014-04) covers the plumbing contractor's installation of drain bodies, grates, traps, and drain/vent connections. The exterior mud sump civil construction sits in a separate deliverable (DEL-018-05, PKG-018 — General Contractor) and must be coordinated with this scope.

## Principles

### 1. Drains must be positioned before the concrete floor is poured.
Floor drain installation is a sequencing-critical activity. Drain bodies must be set to the correct finished-floor elevation and held in place before the concrete slab is placed. Any drain that is missed or mislocated at this stage requires core-drilling after the fact, which is costly and structurally disruptive. Source: ASSUMPTION (standard construction practice). **Coordination with PKG-011 (General Contractor / concrete) is mandatory before the slab pour.**

### 2. The conceptual drawing defines minimum drain locations; IFC drawings govern final installation.
The plumbing conceptual drawing (App B — Plumbing, R-06) shows approximate floor drain locations. These are design intent only. The IFC plumbing drawings (DEL-006-03, produced by PKG-006 Plumbing Engineer) will govern precise locations, invert elevations, pipe slopes, drain sizing, and vent configuration. Installation shall not proceed based solely on the conceptual drawing. Source: R-01 S3.3.2 ("All Issued for Construction Drawings must be signed/stamped by a professional engineer").

### 3. Heavy equipment loads govern grate and frame selection.
The repair bays and wash bay are designed to accommodate track-type and packer-type heavy equipment (e.g., motor scrapers). Drain grates and frames in these areas must be load-rated accordingly. Standard residential or light commercial drain grates are not appropriate. Source: R-01 S3.4 ("Steel plating in concrete at strategic locations to accommodate track and packer type heavy equipment").

### 4. Trap seal integrity is a code requirement and an operational concern.
Infrequently used floor drains are at risk of trap seal evaporation, which allows sewer gases into the building. In a heated industrial shop this is both a health/safety concern and a code violation. Trap primers or other trap-seal protection devices should be specified for drains in areas subject to low usage frequency. The Plumbing Engineer shall identify which specific drain locations require trap primers during IFC design (see Specification REQ-014-04-07). Source: ASSUMPTION (standard plumbing practice); National Plumbing Code of Canada (location TBD).

### 5. Scope boundary with DEL-018-05 must be explicitly agreed before installation.
The wash bay floor drain connects to the exterior mud sump. Two contractors are involved: the Plumbing Contractor (DEL-014-04) and the General Contractor for civil/site work (DEL-018-05). The connection point (building penetration, exterior clean-out stub, or other interface) must be formally agreed and coordinated to avoid gaps or overlaps. This scope boundary has been elevated to a normative requirement in Specification (see Scope section "Scope Boundary — Wash Bay Drain"). Source: Decomposition SOW-0027b, DEL-018-05; ASSUMPTION (interface risk identified from scope split).

### 6. A single coordination steward should be identified for drainage interfaces.
This deliverable interfaces with multiple packages and deliverables: DEL-014-02 (septic), DEL-014-05 (emergency shower), DEL-014-06 (plumbing fixtures), DEL-018-05 (mud sump/civil), PKG-011 (concrete), and PKG-006 (plumbing design). No single stewardship role is currently identified for managing coordination across all drain-related interfaces. **ASSUMPTION:** The Project Manager or Design-Builder should designate a coordination lead for drainage integration to prevent interface gaps. This is particularly important in a design-build context where multiple contractors operate on related scopes simultaneously. [Enrichment: D-002 — coordination stewardship rationale.]

## Considerations

### Design-Build Context
This is a design-build project (CCDC 14–2013). The IFC plumbing drawings do not yet exist; they will be produced during the design phase (PKG-006). As a result, many drain specifications (size, model, load rating, trap type) are TBD at this stage and will be resolved during design. The Plumbing Contractor should engage with the Plumbing Engineer (PKG-006) early to ensure drain selection is coordinated with the structural slab pour schedule.

### Drainage to Septic vs. Drainage to Mud Sump
The plumbing drawing (App B) shows shop floor drains connecting to the septic tank, and the wash bay mud sump receiving wash bay drainage separately. The separation of these two drainage paths is important for regulatory and operational reasons: wash bay effluent (oil, grease, heavy sediment from large equipment) may require different treatment or disposal than general floor wash water. See Purpose section above for detailed rationale on consequences of cross-connection. Source: App B — Plumbing; ASSUMPTION regarding regulatory intent. **This distinction should be confirmed with the Plumbing Engineer during design.**

### Oil/Grease Separation
**ASSUMPTION:** In a heavy equipment maintenance shop, floor drains receiving oil/grease-laden water may be subject to provincial environmental regulations governing discharge to septic systems. An oil/grease interceptor or separator may be required upstream of the septic connection. This is a design-phase determination (PKG-006). The Plumbing Contractor should flag this to the Plumbing Engineer if not already addressed. A prescriptive requirement (REQ-014-04-11) has been added to Specification to mandate this determination.

### Inspection Sequencing
Alberta Safety Code plumbing inspections (rough-in and final) are mandatory. Rough-in inspection must occur before the concrete slab is poured (or at minimum before walls are closed in). The Plumbing Contractor must schedule inspections early enough to avoid delays to the concrete pour sequence.

For an installation deliverable, the applicable workmanship standard is typically a combination of: (a) the National Plumbing Code of Canada (installation provisions), (b) Alberta Safety Codes, (c) manufacturer installation instructions for specific products, and (d) any CCDC 14-2013 contract provisions regarding quality of work. ### Common Installation Failure Modes
**ASSUMPTION (general industry practice):** Common failure modes for floor drain installations in heavy equipment maintenance shops include:
- **Drain body movement during concrete pour:** Inadequately braced drain bodies can shift during pour, resulting in misalignment with finished floor elevation or pipe connections. Mitigation: rigid bracing and monitoring during pour (see Procedure Step 3.1).
- **Trap seal loss:** In drains that are infrequently used, trap water evaporates, allowing sewer gas to enter the building. Mitigation: trap primers or trap seal protection devices (see Specification REQ-014-04-07).
- **Grate failure under heavy equipment loads:** Standard-duty grates installed in heavy equipment areas can crack or collapse. Mitigation: load-rated grates per REQ-014-04-06.
- **Pipe slope deficiency:** Insufficient pipe slope results in standing water, debris accumulation, and eventual blockage. Mitigation: slope verification during installation (see Procedure verification checks).
These are not project-specific findings but are provided as foresight context for field decision-making. [Enrichment: E-001 — common failure modes for prudent stewardship.]

## Examples

Typical drain locations and types for heavy equipment maintenance shops (ASSUMPTION — general industry practice; not derived from project-specific source):
- Sump drains in repair bays: often cast iron sump-type drain with deep body to allow oil/water sediment to settle before entry to drain line.
- Floor drains in wash areas: large-body cast iron or stainless floor drain with heavy-duty slotted or perforated grate, load-rated for equipment.
- Emergency shower drain: typically a standard floor drain immediately below or adjacent to the shower/eye wash head, sized to handle full shower flow rate (ASSUMPTION: approximately 75.7 L/min per ANSI Z358.1 for shower; see Specification REQ-014-04-13).
