# Guidance — DEL-013-04: Heavy Equipment Exhaust System

---

## Purpose

This deliverable exists to provide a dedicated, code-compliant exhaust system that safely removes combustion exhaust fumes and particulates generated by heavy equipment operated within the repair bays of the New North Shop. Without dedicated exhaust capture, heavy equipment running inside an enclosed shop would expose workers to hazardous diesel and gasoline exhaust concentrations.

The RFP explicitly calls for "exhaust hoses and fans for heavy equipment" (R-01 §3.4), making this a mandatory design requirement. The system is one of several coordinated mechanical elements (alongside the air exchanger, makeup air system, and heating system) required to achieve a safe and compliant indoor air environment in a 35-foot-ceiling industrial maintenance facility.

Source: R-01 §3.4; `_CONTEXT.md`; OBJ-001; OBJ-004.

---

## Principles

**1. Source capture is the preferred exhaust strategy.**
Capturing exhaust at or immediately adjacent to the tailpipe is more effective than dilution ventilation for controlling high-concentration fume sources. Flexible exhaust hose drops connected directly to vehicle tailpipes are the industry standard for heavy equipment in enclosed repair bays. This is the approach explicitly named in the RFP.
Source: R-01 §3.4 — "exhaust hoses and fans."

**2. System sizing must follow equipment exhaust volumes, not building volume alone.**
Fan and duct sizing must be based on the actual exhaust flow rates of the equipment being serviced. The mechanical engineer (PKG-003) must obtain or estimate equipment exhaust volumes before sizing fans. Undersizing creates a safety hazard; oversizing creates unnecessary energy load and may adversely affect makeup air balance. Capture velocity at the hose connection point must meet ACGIH Industrial Ventilation Manual guidelines for the applicable source type (see Specification REQ-013).
Source: `_DEPENDENCIES.md` — "Exhaust volume determines fan sizing." Enriched per _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md F-001.

**3. Air balance must be maintained across all exhaust systems.**
This system operates in the same building envelope as DEL-013-02 (air exchanger), DEL-013-03 (makeup air), and DEL-013-05 (welding exhaust). The exhaust volume removed by this system directly affects the makeup air requirement. Mechanical design must treat these systems as an integrated whole, not as independent items.
Source: `_DEPENDENCIES.md` Critical Integration Points — "Exhaust volume affects makeup air requirements."

**4. Outlet placement is a safety and environmental constraint, not just a design choice.**
The exhaust outlet and stack must be positioned and sized to prevent exhaust re-entrainment into building air intakes, to comply with setback and height requirements under Alberta environmental and safety codes, and to avoid hazardous zones for personnel near the building exterior. A field verification test for re-entrainment must be defined (see Specification REQ-005 acceptance criterion).
Source: `_CONTEXT.md` Key Requirements — "Proper outlet location and height"; R-01 §3.3.2. Enriched per _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md D-001.

**5. The system must be maintainable for ongoing operations.**
A regional landfill maintenance shop is an operational facility that will run continuously after project completion. Ductwork access panels, hose replacement provisions, and filter serviceability must be designed in from the start. Inaccessible or difficult-to-clean duct runs are a long-term maintenance liability.
Source: `_DEPENDENCIES.md` Constraint Notes — "System must be cleanable and maintainable."

**6. Worker exposure limits define the system's performance threshold.**
The system's effectiveness is ultimately measured against occupational exposure limits (OELs) for exhaust contaminants. Alberta OHS Code Part 25 establishes threshold limit values (TLVs) for CO, NO2, diesel particulate matter, and other combustion by-products. System design must ensure that residual exposure (after source capture) remains below these limits during normal equipment operation in the repair bays.
Source: _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md C-001; Alberta OHS Code Part 25 (specific TLV values TBD — location TBD).

## Considerations

### Mechanical Design Dependency
This installation deliverable cannot proceed without approved mechanical design drawings (DEL-003-04 Exhaust Plans) from PKG-003. The installation contractor is not responsible for system design; they are responsible for installing per the approved drawings. The critical path risk is the availability of finalized equipment specifications to inform fan and duct sizing in DEL-003-04.

Source: `_DEPENDENCIES.md` External Dependencies — "Equipment specifications and exhaust requirements."

### Equipment Type Uncertainty
At the time of writing, the specific heavy equipment types operated at the landfill are not fully documented in the available source material. The RFP references "track and packer type heavy equipment" (R-01 §3.4 steel plating note) and "motor scrapers" (R-01 §3.1), but a formal equipment exhaust specification has not been provided. This uncertainty affects filtration type, hose diameter, fan sizing, and exhaust hazard classification. A formal equipment list identifying all heavy equipment types serviced at the facility, with engine size and fuel type, must be obtained from the County before design can be finalized (see Datasheet TBD Register TBD-DS-03).

ASSUMPTION: Equipment is diesel-powered, generating diesel particulate matter (DPM) and combustion gases (CO, NOx, HC) as the primary exhaust hazards.

Source: R-01 §3.4; _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md B-003.

### Filtration vs. Direct Exhaust
Depending on regulatory requirements, the system may discharge collected exhaust directly to atmosphere (the most common approach for source-capture hose systems in Alberta) or may require in-line filtration before discharge. The choice depends on:
- Alberta Environment / AEP regulations applicable to the site classification (landfill industrial site).
- Local air quality permitting requirements.
- Equipment exhaust classification.

TBD — to be confirmed during environmental compliance review (`_DEPENDENCIES.md` External Dependencies). See Conflict Table CQ-002.

### Heat Recovery Opportunity
The `_DEPENDENCIES.md` notes a potential interface between this exhaust system and DEL-013-01 (High-Recovery Heating System). In principle, sensible heat in equipment exhaust could be recovered via a heat exchanger before discharge. However, diesel exhaust heat recovery is technically complex (corrosion, particulate fouling) and may not be economically viable at this scale.

ASSUMPTION: Heat recovery from equipment exhaust is a design-phase evaluation item, not a confirmed requirement. The mechanical engineer should assess feasibility and document the decision. See Conflict Table CQ-001.

### Ductwork Routing in a 35-Foot Ceiling Space
The New North Shop has a concrete structure with a 35-foot ceiling (R-01 §3.4; Appendix B). This provides generous vertical space for overhead ductwork routing without conflict with crane travel (two 5-tonne overhead cranes). Ductwork must be coordinated with crane runway beam heights and structural framing to ensure clearance.

ASSUMPTION: Ductwork will be routed overhead; floor-level or trench routing is unlikely for an exhaust system in this configuration.

### Coordination with Welding Exhaust (DEL-013-05)
The welding station exhaust system (DEL-013-05) is a separate system explicitly called out in both the RFP (Appendix B notes) and the decomposition. The two systems must be kept separate in design and installation because their exhaust classification, filtration requirements, and code treatment differ (welding fumes vs. combustion exhaust). Ductwork routing must not cross-contaminate the two systems.

Source: `_DEPENDENCIES.md` — "DEL-013-05 (Welding Exhaust): Separate exhaust system coordination."

### Electrical Coordination
Exhaust fans require electrical power supply. Coordination with the electrical contractor (PKG-015, DEL-015-04 Equipment Power Circuits) is required for fan circuit sizing, disconnect location, and controls wiring. This coordination must be captured in the overall mechanical-electrical interface review.

### Condensate Management Decision
Procedure Step 5.2 references installing ductwork with "positive slope toward drain points (if condensate management is specified by the mechanical engineer — TBD)." The Mechanical Engineer must determine during design whether condensate management is required for this exhaust system. If required, slope direction and drain point locations must be specified in the IFC drawings. If not required, the determination should be documented. Deferring this decision to the installation phase risks rework if drainage is needed after ductwork is installed without slope.

Source: _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md C-003; Procedure Step 5.2.

### Future System Modification and Expansion Capacity
The current design documents do not address whether the exhaust system should be sized for current equipment load only or should include capacity for future expansion (e.g., additional equipment bays, changes in equipment fleet composition). If the County anticipates fleet changes or facility expansion, the mechanical design should consider oversizing ductwork trunk lines or providing capped branch connections for future exhaust hose drops. This consideration should be raised with the Project Manager and County during design review.

Source: _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md X-001; directional coverage gap identified in semantic lensing.

### Lifecycle Operating Cost
Design trade-off decisions (e.g., filtration vs. direct exhaust, retractable hose reels vs. fixed drops, centralized vs. distributed fans) should consider lifecycle operating costs in addition to capital cost. Relevant lifecycle factors include:
- Energy consumption (fan motor operating hours and kW)
- Filter media replacement frequency and cost (if filtration is included)
- Exhaust hose wear and replacement cycle
- Maintenance labor for access panel inspections and duct cleaning

These factors may influence the cost-benefit analysis for design options in the Trade-offs table below. ASSUMPTION: lifecycle cost analysis is appropriate for a design-build value evaluation but specific cost data is not available in current source material.

Source: _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md C-004.

### Warranty Scope Clarification
The Datasheet states a 2-year warranty period post CCC, and Procedure Records reference warranty documentation per RFP §2.10. However, no document clarifies whether the warranty covers only fixed equipment (fans, ductwork, controls) or also includes consumable components (flexible exhaust hoses, filter media). For a continuously operating maintenance facility, the distinction has significant cost and operational implications. This should be clarified through contract review of CCDC 14-2013 warranty provisions and recorded in the project record.

Source: _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md F-005; Datasheet Attributes "Warranty Period"; R-01 §2.10.

## Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Option A | Option B | Recommended Direction | Lifecycle Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Exhaust discharge method | Direct discharge to atmosphere (no filtration) — simpler, lower cost | In-line filtration before discharge — added cost and maintenance | Determine by regulatory review; direct discharge is standard for source-capture hose systems if codes permit | Option B has recurring filter replacement cost; Option A has lower operating cost but may require regulatory approval |
| Hose type | Fixed pendant exhaust hose drops per bay position | Retractable hose reel assemblies on overhead track | ASSUMPTION: Retractable reel systems offer flexibility for different equipment lengths but are higher cost; fixed drops are simpler. Mechanical designer to specify per equipment layout. | Retractable reels have higher maintenance and replacement cost; fixed drops have lower lifecycle cost but less flexibility |
| Fan configuration | Single centralized fan serving all collection points | Multiple smaller fans per bay | ASSUMPTION: Centralized fan is simpler to maintain; multiple fans offer redundancy. Decision to be made in mechanical design based on duct sizing economics. | Centralized: single motor, lower total energy consumption. Distributed: redundancy, but higher total maintenance. |
| Heat recovery | Include heat exchanger on exhaust stream | Discharge without heat recovery | ASSUMPTION: Evaluate in mechanical design; likely not cost-effective unless specific recovery case is made | Heat exchanger adds capital and maintenance cost; payback depends on heating load offset and exhaust temperature |
