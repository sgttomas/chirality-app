# Guidance — DEL-008-01 Geotechnical Investigation & Report

---

## Purpose

The Geotechnical Investigation & Report (DEL-008-01) exists to characterize the subsurface conditions at the proposed Maintenance Shop Addition site before any foundation design or construction pricing is committed. The RFP explicitly requires this investigation and makes its completion a contractual prerequisite for final foundation pricing: "Final pricing for the design/construction of the foundation will be negotiated with the successful Proponent when the complete geotechnical report is available" (RFP §4.8.2).

This deliverable is the foundational data-gathering step that unlocks the entire structural and civil design stream. Without it, the Structural Engineer (PKG-002) cannot complete the foundation design and the Owner cannot agree to a firm foundation price. Its position on the critical path means delays to this deliverable directly affect the project completion deadline of December 31, 2026 (RFP §3.1).

The geotechnical investigation also supports the broader site investigation objective (OBJ-003 in the decomposition) and directly enables the project's contract financial objective (OBJ-006 — reconciliation of foundation variable-price scope).

---

## Principles

**Start early.** This deliverable is a critical-path predecessor. Field mobilization, laboratory testing, and report preparation all take time. Early execution of the investigation preserves schedule float for downstream design packages.

**Full and complete report.** The RFP is explicit: "A full complete geotechnical report will be required by the successful proponent to use during the design process" (RFP §3.3.2). Partial or preliminary reports are not sufficient. The report must address all necessary aspects — stratigraphy, groundwater, bearing capacity, settlement, and foundation recommendations — for the foundation design to proceed without re-investigation.

**Normal ground conditions baseline.** The initial proposal cost estimate is priced on the assumption of normal ground conditions without deleterious subgrade material (RFP §4.8.2). The investigation and report must clearly characterize whether actual conditions differ from this baseline. This finding directly determines whether the foundation cost will be at, above, or below the baseline estimate and is therefore commercially significant.

**Owner receives the report.** The report is explicitly to be provided to the Owner (RFP §3.3.2). The Owner is a primary user of this document, in addition to the Design-Builder's design team.

**Professional responsibility.** The geotechnical investigation and report are to be conducted by a Geotechnical Engineer (_CONTEXT.md). This carries professional and regulatory obligations under Alberta engineering practice. The investigation methodology, testing program, and report conclusions are the professional responsibility of the geotechnical practitioner.

**Regulatory applicability rationale.** The Specification Standards table lists several regulatory instruments (EGPA, Safety Codes Act, NBC/ABC) as likely applicable to this investigation. The rationale for their inclusion is as follows:

- **Alberta EGPA**: The practice of geotechnical engineering in Alberta falls under provincial professional regulation. The geotechnical investigation and report are professional engineering activities and the responsible engineer is expected to practice within the regulatory framework. However, the specific clause-level requirements (e.g., seal/stamp requirements for geotechnical reports as distinct from IFC drawings) have not been confirmed from accessible sources. **ASSUMPTION**: EGPA governs the practice; specific report-sealing requirements are TBD pending confirmation from APEGA or the relevant legislation.
- **Alberta Safety Codes Act**: RFP §3.3.2 requires adherence to "all Alberta Safety Codes." This requirement is directed at the building design broadly. Whether the Safety Codes Act has direct applicability to the geotechnical investigation report itself (as distinct from the building design that uses it) is an open question. **ASSUMPTION**: included in the Standards table as a conservative measure; the Geotechnical Engineer or project legal counsel should confirm applicability.
- **NBC/ABC**: The geotechnical report provides foundation design parameters that must support compliance with building code foundation requirements. The code's applicability is primarily through the foundation design (PKG-002), but the investigation scope should be sufficient to address code-required design parameters.

Source: _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md item C-001; Specification — Standards table.

## Considerations

### Site context — active landfill

The investigation site is within an active landfill facility (West Dried Meat Lake Regional Landfill). Active landfill operations involve heavy equipment, haul roads, and ongoing earthworks. The investigation must be coordinated with site operations to ensure safety and access. Access coordination and client approval of methodology are identified dependencies (_DEPENDENCIES.md).

### County site preparation activities

The County is responsible for brushing, clearing, and bulk cut and fill of the project area (RFP §3.3.1). The timing of these activities relative to the geotechnical investigation requires coordination. If bulk cut and fill occurs before the investigation, the investigation may encounter disturbed conditions rather than in-situ soils. If investigation occurs first, the ground surface examined may differ from final subgrade conditions. **ASSUMPTION**: the investigation program should account for the planned extent of County site preparation; coordination with the County on the sequence of activities is recommended (TBD — specific sequence not defined in available sources).

### Foundation type and loading

The proposed building is a concrete structure approximately 13,000 sq ft (130' x 100') with a 35-foot ceiling height (RFP §3.1, §3.4). It is designed to accommodate heavy equipment including tracked equipment and packer-class machinery (steel floor plates specified in RFP §3.4) and two 5-tonne overhead cranes (RFP §3.4). The mezzanine is described as capable of handling heavy items such as oil totes and pumping equipment (RFP §3.4). These represent significant and variable structural loads that the foundation design must address. The geotechnical investigation should provide data sufficient to support bearing capacity and settlement analysis for these loading conditions.

**ASSUMPTION**: the Structural Engineer responsible for PKG-002 should provide anticipated load ranges to the Geotechnical Engineer as input to the investigation program design. This coordination step is not explicitly described in the RFP but is standard engineering practice. No mechanism currently ensures these essential data are available before investigation program design; a formal prerequisite or coordination step should be established (see Procedure Step 2.2 and _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md item B-001).

### Service pit

The RFP includes a ventilated and lighted service pit/trench for under-equipment servicing (RFP §3.4; SOW-0028). **ASSUMPTION**: the service pit introduces a below-grade structure requiring excavation; the geotechnical report should address excavation recommendations, lateral earth pressure, and potential for groundwater ingress in the service pit area. This topic is now reflected in Specification REQ-011 to provide normative backing for the operational coverage already present in Procedure Steps 5.8 and 6.1.

### Variable-price commercial mechanism

The use of a variable-price mechanism for foundation scope (RFP §4.8.2) creates a direct commercial link between the geotechnical findings and the contract value. If the investigation reveals deleterious subgrade materials or conditions materially different from normal ground, the foundation price negotiation will be affected. This document is therefore not only a technical input but a commercial input to the project.

### Deleterious subgrade material — definition and determination criteria

REQ-004 and Procedure Step 5.6 require assessment relative to "normal ground conditions without any deleterious subgrade material" (RFP §4.8.2). This is the critical verdict basis for the commercial pricing mechanism, yet no source document defines what constitutes "deleterious subgrade material" or establishes the threshold for departing from the normal-conditions baseline in the context of this project.

Considerations for the Geotechnical Engineer and Owner in establishing criteria:

- The determination of "deleterious" likely depends on the proposed foundation type and loading conditions — material that is acceptable for one foundation solution may be problematic for another.
- On an active landfill site, the potential for encountering fill, waste, contaminated soils, or other non-native materials is higher than for a greenfield site. The investigation program should be designed with this context in mind.
- The criteria should be established before report finalization so that the assessment in the report can be made against agreed benchmarks, rather than left to post-report interpretation.

**ASSUMPTION**: The Geotechnical Engineer should define criteria for "deleterious subgrade material" and "normal ground conditions" in consultation with the Owner before report finalization. These criteria should be documented in the investigation program or as a separate technical memorandum. (See _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md item X-003.)

### Report transmission format

The Datasheet identifies the Owner (Camrose County) as the report recipient (RFP §3.3.2), and Procedure Step 7.1 directs transmission of the complete report. However, no source document specifies the format (hard copy, electronic, or both), medium, or number of copies required by the Owner. Complete audit documentation requires the transmission format to be defined. **ASSUMPTION**: Confirm Owner requirements for report format and number of copies before report preparation is finalized. (See _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md item X-004.)
