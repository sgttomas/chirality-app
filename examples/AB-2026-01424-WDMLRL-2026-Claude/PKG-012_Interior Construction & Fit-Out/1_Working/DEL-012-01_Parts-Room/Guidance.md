## 1. Purpose

This document provides directional guidance for the design and construction of the Parts Storage Room (DEL-012-01). It explains why this deliverable exists, the principles that should guide design decisions, key considerations, trade-offs, and relevant contextual information to inform the General Contractor and design team.

The parts storage room is a primary operational space within the new maintenance shop addition. It provides secure, organized storage for parts, tools, and consumables used to maintain the landfill's fleet of heavy equipment. The room must support efficient inventory management while being physically secure, environmentally conditioned, and appropriately equipped for industrial-scale storage needs.

Source: R-01 §3.1; Decomposition OBJ-001 statement; Decomposition PKG-012 scope description.

### 1.1 Operational Value Rationale

The parts room directly supports the facility's operational efficiency objectives (OBJ-001) by providing organized, secure, and environmentally controlled storage for maintenance parts. A well-designed parts room contributes to reduced equipment downtime through ready availability of correctly stored parts, efficient inventory management (technicians can locate and retrieve parts quickly), and protection of parts quality through appropriate environmental conditions (climate control prevents degradation of seals, fluids, and electronic components in Alberta's extreme temperatures). Design quality decisions for this room -- shelving layout, door sizing, security provisions, and environmental controls -- have a direct bearing on maintenance workflow efficiency and, by extension, on the landfill's operational continuity. ASSUMPTION: operational value linkage inferred from OBJ-001 and facility purpose (R-01 §3.1). (Lensing item F-003)

## 2. Principles

### 2.1 Operational First

The parts room is primarily a functional operational space — not an administrative or display space. Design decisions should prioritize:
- Accessibility for technicians retrieving and returning parts during maintenance work.
- Logical organization of storage space (shelving layout that supports efficient inventory management).
- Adequate lighting throughout the room (6× 8' LED fixtures per SOW-0056) to support reading part labels and identification numbers.

Source: R-01 §3.1 (operational program); App B (Shop) — floor plan; Decomposition SOW-0056.

### 2.2 Security

The RFP explicitly designates this as a "secure area" (R-01 §3.4; SOW-0029). The roll-up door must be lockable and the enclosure must prevent unauthorized access to stored parts, tools, and consumables. This implies:
- The roll-up door should include a locking mechanism appropriate for industrial use (ASSUMPTION: padlock-compatible latch or motor-driven lock; specific hardware TBD in design).
- All partition walls should be full-height or near full-height to prevent over-partition access (ASSUMPTION: consistent with secure room convention).

Source: R-01 §3.4; Decomposition SOW-0029.

### 2.3 Integration with Adjacent Spaces

The parts room sits adjacent to the Utility Room (DEL-012-02) and opens toward the main shop work area. The roll-up door orientation shown on the conceptual floor plan (App B Shop) opens toward the main shop — consistent with technician workflow (tools and parts retrieved from storage and taken to the work bays).

The mezzanine above (DEL-011-07) is a load-bearing structure spanning the parts room, utility room, and wash bay (Decomposition SOW-0032). It is designed to support heavy items including oil totes and pumping equipment (Decomposition SOW-0033). The parts room fit-out must not conflict with the structural requirements of the mezzanine above. Note: The mezzanine's relationship to the parts room is structural overlay only -- the mezzanine structure and its construction are PKG-011 scope (DEL-011-07), not PKG-012 scope. The characterization "load-bearing mezzanine over parts room, utility room, and wash bay" is the standardized description used across all documents. (Lensing item B-004)

Source: App B (Shop) — floor plan; Decomposition SOW-0032, SOW-0033; _DEPENDENCIES.md; Datasheet §2.1 (Mezzanine Above).

### 2.4 Design-Build Context

This is a design-build project. The RFP states "the County only has a concept in mind" (R-01 §3.4). The conceptual floor plan (App B Shop) and the design requirements in §3.4 are starting points, not final specifications. The design team has latitude to optimize the room layout, shelving configuration, and finishes subject to Owner approval during the preliminary design phase.

Preliminary design must receive County approval before construction commences (R-01 §3.3.2; Decomposition SOW-0010a).

## 3. Considerations

### 3.1 Shelving Selection

The conceptual floor plan shows shelving symbols along the south and east walls of the parts room. The specific shelving type, capacity, and configuration are TBD pending final design. Key considerations:

- **Load capacity:** The room stores maintenance parts for heavy equipment (tractors, motor scrapers, packer trucks). Heavy items such as filters, hydraulic components, and drive components may need to be stored. Shelving should be rated for heavy industrial use. ASSUMPTION: minimum 200–500 lbs per shelf level (TBD in design).
- **Adjustability:** Adjustable-height shelving will provide flexibility as the parts inventory evolves over time.
- **Chemical resistance:** Parts may be stored with residual oils and fluids. Powder-coated or galvanized steel shelving is appropriate for this environment. ASSUMPTION.
- **Fire code clearances:** Shelving layout must maintain required clearances from HVAC registers, sprinkler heads (if any), and electrical panels. ASSUMPTION: fire code compliance will be addressed in final design by Architect.

Source: R-01 §3.1 (operational context); App B (Shop) floor plan; ASSUMPTION for specifications not in source.

### 3.2 Roll-Up Door Selection

A 6-foot wide roll-up door provides the primary access point. Considerations:
- **Manual vs. motorized:** A 6-foot roll-up door can be manual or motorized. SOW-0060 provides power for all overhead doors, suggesting motorized operation is possible. ASSUMPTION: motorized is likely intended given the industrial shop context, but final selection is TBD.
- **Insulation:** If the HVAC system is designed to maintain temperature in the parts room, an insulated roll-up door will reduce heat loss when the door is open. ASSUMPTION: insulated door advisable in an Alberta climate.
- **Clear height:** The required clear height of the roll-up door opening is TBD in final design. Should accommodate access by hand trucks, pallet jacks, or small forklifts as needed for parts replenishment. ASSUMPTION.

Source: R-01 §3.4; Decomposition SOW-0029, SOW-0060; ASSUMPTION for specifications not in source.

### 3.3 Coordination with HVAC (PKG-013)

The _DEPENDENCIES.md notes that HVAC system routing passes through the storage room from PKG-013. Key coordination considerations:
- HVAC supply and/or return registers must be positioned to avoid conflicts with shelving layout.
- Ductwork routing within the 35-foot ceiling height must account for the mezzanine structure above.
- HVAC commissioning must be complete before the parts room is placed in service. This creates a sequencing dependency: PKG-013 work must be substantially complete before the parts room can be occupied.

Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md; R-01 §3.4; Decomposition SOW-0035–SOW-0037.

### 3.4 Coordination with Electrical (PKG-015)

Electrical scope (PKG-015) is responsible for:
- 6× 8' LED lighting fixtures (SOW-0056) — rough-in, wiring, and fixture mounting.
- Power circuit for roll-up door motor (SOW-0060).
- Receptacles within the room per the electrical drawing (SOW-0058 — specific receptacle types TBD pending electrical design).

The General Contractor (PKG-012) must coordinate timing with the electrical subcontractor to ensure rough openings, blocking, and access are available when the electrician requires them.

Source: App B-Elec (R-05); Decomposition SOW-0056, SOW-0058, SOW-0060; _DEPENDENCIES.md.

### 3.5 Alberta Climate Context

The facility is located near Ferintosh, Alberta (SW 14–44–21–W4), which experiences a continental climate with cold winters (temperatures regularly below -20°C). ASSUMPTION: the parts room will require:
- Connection to the building-wide high-recovery heating system to protect temperature-sensitive parts (fluids, gaskets, seals, electronics).
- Adequate insulation in the partition walls and ceiling (if exposed to exterior or unheated spaces) — TBD in final design.

Source: R-01 §1.0 (location); ASSUMPTION for climate-specific detail.

### 4.1 Post-Occupancy Feedback

To confirm that design trade-off decisions actually delivered the intended value, the Owner should consider a post-occupancy feedback mechanism for the parts room. This could include a structured review (e.g., 6 months and 12 months after occupancy) with maintenance staff to assess whether shelving layout supports efficient inventory management, whether the roll-up door type (manual/motorized) meets operational needs, and whether finishes are performing adequately in the industrial environment. This feedback would inform future facility improvement decisions and provide evidence for warranty claims if applicable.
