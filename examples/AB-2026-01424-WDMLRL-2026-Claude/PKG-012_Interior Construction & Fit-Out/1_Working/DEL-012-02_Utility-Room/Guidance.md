## Purpose

The Utility Room is the central infrastructure host room of the New North Shop. Its purpose is to house the building's primary mechanical, HVAC, plumbing, and water storage systems in a dedicated, accessible, and code-compliant environment. The room directly enables the operational functionality of the entire facility: all PKG-013 HVAC and mechanical equipment installations depend on this room being complete and properly configured before they can proceed.

Source: RFP S3.1 (building program); _DEPENDENCIES.md (PKG-013 dependency statement); OBJ-001 (deliver a fully functional maintenance shop).

## Principles

### P1 — Infrastructure Host First
The utility room must be treated as the highest-priority sequencing deliverable within PKG-012. All six PKG-013 mechanical deliverables (heating, air exchanger, makeup air, equipment exhaust, welding exhaust, ceiling fans) are blocked until the utility room is complete. Planning should front-load utility room construction within the PKG-012 schedule.

Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md -- "Utility room must be complete before any PKG-013 equipment installation."

### P2 — Equipment Footprint Must Drive Layout
The utility room's internal layout -- partition placement, equipment platform locations, service clearances, door swing, plumbing rough-in points -- must be driven by the finalized equipment footprints and manufacturer clearance requirements for the PKG-013 systems to be installed. Construction must not begin until these footprints are confirmed.

Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md -- "Equipment footprint must be finalized before construction start"; _CONTEXT.md -- Key Requirements.

### P3 — Coordinate Rough-Ins Before Any Concrete Work
The utility room contains a floor drain (PKG-014) and must accommodate cistern plumbing connections, HVAC penetrations (PKG-013), and electrical conduit (PKG-015). All rough-in locations must be confirmed and inspected before concrete floor placement. Re-work in concrete is costly and may be structurally impractical.

Source: App B (Plumbing) -- floor drain shown; App B (Electrical) -- electrical circuits; _CONTEXT.md -- Scope.

### P4 — Mezzanine Load Interface is Structural
The mezzanine above the utility room is specified as load-bearing for heavy items (oil totes, pumping equipment). The utility room's wall and ceiling interface must be coordinated with the structural engineer and PKG-011 to ensure load paths are not compromised. Do not treat the mezzanine interface as an interior finish detail. The actual clear height of the utility room below the mezzanine must be confirmed and documented -- it is a critical parameter for equipment clearance and ventilation design. (Enriched per B-003)

Source: RFP S3.4; App B (Shop) notes -- "Load Bearing Over Parts Room + Utility For Storage."

### P5 — Accessibility is an Ongoing Operational Requirement
The utility room houses equipment that will require regular maintenance. Service clearances, door widths, and internal circulation paths must be sufficient not only for initial installation but for ongoing maintenance activities including equipment replacement. This principle applies throughout design and should be verified against equipment manufacturer specifications when available.

Design should also consider long-term serviceability provisions: knock-out panels in walls for future equipment access, rigging points or structural provisions for equipment handling, and equipment access paths sized for replacement scenarios (which may require larger openings than initial installation). These provisions are significantly cheaper to include during initial construction than to retrofit. (Enriched per E-001)

Source: _CONTEXT.md -- Key Requirements ("Accessibility for maintenance and service"); _DEPENDENCIES.md -- Constraint Notes ("Service access must meet manufacturer requirements").

### P6 — Fire Safety and Code Branch Assessment (NEW -- enriched per A-004, C-002)
The utility room, as a mechanical equipment host room, may be subject to fire separation requirements under the Alberta Building Code and National Fire Code. If any hosted equipment is gas-fired, the Alberta Gas Safety Code also applies. The design team must conduct a code branch assessment early in design to identify all applicable safety code requirements (fire rating, combustion air, flue provisions, gas line routing). This assessment should drive wall assembly specifications, door ratings, and penetration sealing requirements. ASSUMPTION: fire-rated separation is likely required; gas code applicability depends on PKG-013 equipment selection.

Source: RFP S3.3.2, S3.4; A-004, C-002 in _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md.

## Considerations

### C1 — Cistern Location
The conceptual floor plan (App B -- Shop) places the 50,000 L water storage cistern within the utility room zone. A cistern of this size has significant structural implications (weight, foundation loading, plumbing connections, fill access). The design team should confirm whether the cistern is below-grade, at-grade, or elevated, and whether any structural pad or pit is required in the foundation/slab. This is a design-stage decision with construction sequencing implications.

Source: App B (Shop) -- "50,000 L Water Storage" label in utility room; RFP S3.4; App B (Plumbing).

### C2 — Mezzanine Stair Position
The stair to the mezzanine is shown in the utility room zone on the conceptual plan. The stair occupies useable floor area and may conflict with equipment placement. The design team should evaluate whether the stair can be positioned to minimize conflict with mechanical equipment footprints while maintaining safe egress.

Source: App B (Shop); App B (Plumbing) -- "Stairs to Mezzanine" in utility room zone.

### C3 — Ventilation and Heat
Mechanical and HVAC equipment generates heat and may require exhaust ventilation or dedicated make-up air. The utility room must be designed to manage these conditions. This is particularly important given the Alberta climate and the range of equipment types to be housed. Specific ventilation design is a PKG-013 responsibility, but the physical provisions (penetrations, louvers, duct openings) must be incorporated into the PKG-012 construction. The mechanical engineer (PKG-013) must define the specific ventilation rate (air changes per hour or cfm) so that PKG-012 can size penetrations accordingly. (Enriched per C-001)

Source: _CONTEXT.md -- Key Requirements ("Adequate ventilation"); RFP S3.4 -- HVAC requirements; App B (Shop) -- "Forced Air Makeup."

### C4 — Scope Boundary with PKG-013, PKG-014, PKG-015
The utility room construction (PKG-012) provides the physical space and rough-in conditions. It does not include equipment installation, which is the responsibility of PKG-013, PKG-014, and PKG-015 respectively. Clear scope demarcation at the interface -- specifically: who installs penetration sleeves, who provides blocking for equipment hangers, who does conduit rough-in -- should be resolved in the design coordination phase. Additionally, responsibility for penetration sealing and fire-stopping after MEP installations must be clearly assigned (enriched per E-003).

Source: Decomposition S6 -- PKG-012 exclusions; _DEPENDENCIES.md.

### C5 — Water Tap Provision
The plumbing drawing shows a water tap in or adjacent to the utility room. This likely serves as a service connection for the cistern system or for maintenance use. Coordination with PKG-014 is required to confirm whether rough-in is a PKG-012 or PKG-014 responsibility.

Source: App B (Plumbing) -- water tap annotation near utility room/Parts room boundary.

### C6 — Alberta Safety Codes Compliance
The utility room, as an equipment host room, may be subject to specific requirements under the Alberta Safety Codes Act (Electrical, Gas, Plumbing, and Fire Safety Codes). The design team should confirm applicability of each code branch to the utility room configuration. This includes: (a) fire separation requirements under the Alberta Building Code; (b) National Fire Code requirements for mechanical equipment rooms; (c) Alberta Gas Safety Code if any equipment is gas-fired; (d) Alberta Building Code barrier-free access provisions for a public-authority-owned building. ASSUMPTION: all relevant Alberta Safety Codes apply; specific clause-level requirements TBD at design stage. Each code branch has been assigned a resolution owner in the Specification Standards table. (Enriched per A-001, A-003, A-004, C-002, F-001)

Source: RFP S3.3.2; RFP S3.4 ("The proposed building must adhere to all Alberta Safety Codes").

### C7 — Warranty Scope Particularization (NEW -- enriched per F-005)
The project warranty is 2 years post-CCC per RFP S2.10.2. However, no document clarifies which specific utility room elements are covered by the warranty. The following elements should be addressed in warranty documentation: equipment platforms, wall and floor finishes, access doors, access panels, penetration sealing/fire-stopping, and cistern structural pad. Elements that interface with PKG-013/014/015 equipment may have shared warranty responsibility. TBD: confirm warranty coverage scope and boundary per CCDC 14-2013 contract interpretation.

Source: RFP S2.10.2; Datasheet Conditions; Procedure Records.

### C8 — Submittal Management (NEW -- enriched per E-002)
Multiple submittals are required for this deliverable (finish schedule, equipment coordination records, clearance verification, platform design). These are referenced across Specification Documentation, Procedure Steps, and Records with varying terminology. A consolidated submittal register should be established early in the pre-construction phase to track all required submittals in one place, with status, due dates, and approval responsibilities.

Source: E-002 in _SEMANTIC_LENSING.md; Specification Documentation table; Procedure Step 1 outputs.

### C9 — County PM Inspection Role (NEW -- enriched per E-004)
RFP S3.3.2 requires a County project representative to be present at all inspections. Multiple documents reference "County PM" or "County project representative" as an inspection party, but no document establishes what authority, qualifications, or competence this person has for technical inspections. TBD: confirm County PM inspection authority and qualifications with Camrose County. ## Trade-offs

### T1 — Utility Room Size vs. Operational Functionality
The conceptual plan does not specify the exact dimensions of the utility room. A larger room provides more service clearance and flexibility for equipment changes; a smaller room maximizes useable shop floor area. Given that this room hosts six PKG-013 equipment items plus a 50,000 L cistern and mezzanine stairs, the design team should err toward adequate service clearance over floor area optimization. Long-term serviceability (future equipment replacement) should also factor into sizing decisions. (Enriched per E-001)

ASSUMPTION: the 16' bay-width column grid zone is the utility room span; actual confirmed dimensions TBD at design stage. Source: App B (Shop) -- plan dimensions.

### T2 — Finish Level vs. Cost
Interior finish in a utility/mechanical room is functional rather than aesthetic. The minimum acceptable finish (e.g., sealed or painted concrete block walls, polished or sealed concrete floor) is appropriate. Specify finishes that are durable, moisture-resistant, and cleanable, but avoid over-specification that adds cost without operational benefit. Finishes must meet measurable performance criteria (see REQ-012-02-003). (Enriched per A-002)

Source: _CONTEXT.md -- Key Requirements ("Appropriate finishes for equipment environment").

### T3 — Sequencing Risk: Equipment Footprint Confirmation
If PKG-013 equipment selection is delayed, the utility room construction could be blocked or forced to proceed with incomplete information. Early coordination to lock equipment footprints is the primary risk mitigation. A contingency approach would be to build out the room with conservative clearances based on typical equipment dimensions (ASSUMPTION), with the understanding that final positions are confirmed at rough-in stage.

Source: _DEPENDENCIES.md -- "Equipment footprint must be finalized before construction start."
