# Guidance — DEL-006-03: Drain & Vent Plans

---

## Purpose

DEL-006-03 exists to provide the engineered, P.Eng.-stamped drawing set that defines the complete sanitary drainage and venting systems for the New North Shop Addition. Its downstream purpose is threefold:

1. **Regulatory compliance** — The drawings enable obtaining the Alberta Safety Code plumbing permit (SOW-0007, SOW-0009), which is a mandatory precondition for construction to proceed on the plumbing systems.
2. **Construction guidance** — The drawings are the primary instruction document for the plumbing installer (PKG-014), defining pipe routes, slopes, sizes, drain locations, vent stack locations, cleanout placement, and connection points.
3. **Coordination baseline** — The drain and vent layout directly constrains and is constrained by the structural slab design (sleeve locations, service pit drain provisions), the architectural layout (fixture positions), and the civil/site design (mud sump connection, septic tank location).

Sources: Decomposition OBJ-001, OBJ-003, OBJ-004; RFP §3.3.2 (IFC requirement); PKG-006 scope description.

## Principles

### 1. Conceptual drawing as the starting point, not the final design

The App B-Plumb conceptual plumbing drawing shows floor drain locations and plumbing fixture areas but is not an engineered design. The Plumbing Engineer must use it as the programmatic intent and produce a fully code-compliant engineered design. Floor drain positions may shift from the conceptual drawing during detailed coordination with architectural and structural disciplines.

Source: RFP §3.4 ("The County only has a concept in mind; therefore, it will be a design/build project."); App B-Plumb.

### 2. Wash bay drainage is separate from the sanitary system

The wash bay floor drain does not connect directly to the septic holding tank. Per SOW-0027b and the conceptual drawing, wash bay drainage connects to the exterior mud sump (shown on App B-Plumb at the east exterior of the building, labeled "Wash Bay Mud Sump"). The mud sump is designed to be cleaned by excavator. The drain and vent plan must clearly differentiate the wash bay/mud sump drainage path from the sanitary drain path to the septic holding tank.

Source: SOW-0027b; RFP §3.4 ("Mud sump from wash bay on exterior of building for clean out with excavator"); App B-Plumb.

### 3. On-site septic system — no municipal connection

The building is served by an on-site new 2,000 US gallon septic holding tank (SOW-0043; RFP §3.4). There is no municipal sewer connection. All sanitary fixtures and drains (except wash bay) must connect to this new septic holding tank. The existing septic tank (to be removed) is to be removed and replaced with the new septic holding tank (SOW-0043; see Conflict Table CONF-006-03-02 for RFP language clarification, Human Ruling TBD). The drain and vent plans must show the building drain connection to the new septic holding tank and coordinate with the septic details (DEL-006-05).

Source: RFP §3.4; SOW-0043; Decomposition PKG-014 description.

## Considerations

### Coordination with Structural for Slab Penetrations

Concrete slab construction requires drain sleeves and floor drain boxes to be placed before the slab is poured. This means drain locations must be fixed during design coordination — after slab pours, changes are very difficult. The Plumbing Engineer must issue coordinated drain locations to the structural engineer early in the design process.

ASSUMPTION: Standard practice for concrete slab construction with embedded drains; not explicitly stated in RFP but required by construction sequence.

### Service Pit Drain

The service pit/trench (SOW-0028; App B-Plumb shows "Service Trench") is below-grade. Provisions for draining the pit (potential oil/water accumulation from under-equipment servicing) need to be addressed. The connection method and whether it routes to septic or a separate collection requires engineering judgment and may involve regulatory requirements for oil-water separation.

ASSUMPTION: service pit drain provision is required; routing and treatment TBD — the Plumbing Engineer should assess applicable requirements (Alberta Environmental Protection requirements for shop drainage may apply; location TBD in regulations).

**Rationale for routing decision (Lensing item A-004):** The service pit drain routing involves three possible approaches, each with different regulatory and cost implications:

1. **Direct to septic holding tank** — Simplest routing but may not comply with Alberta Environmental Protection and Enhancement Act (EPEA) if oil-bearing wastewater is generated from under-vehicle servicing. The Plumbing Engineer must confirm with Alberta Environmental authority whether this is permitted.
2. **Through oil/water separator to septic** — Adds an oil/water separator upstream of the septic connection. This is the safer regulatory approach if oil contamination is possible, but adds equipment cost and maintenance requirements. The separator would need to be sized for the expected flow rate from the service pit.
3. **Separate collection system** — Routes service pit drainage to a separate holding tank or waste oil collection system, independent of the sanitary septic. This may be required if the environmental authority determines that even separated effluent cannot be discharged to the septic holding tank.

The Plumbing Engineer should determine the routing before proceeding past preliminary design (Step 2.2) by confirming the applicable environmental regulations with the Alberta Environmental authority. See Specification REQ-006-03-032 and Standards table (EPEA entry) for the TBD regulatory confirmation.

### Freeze Protection for Alberta Climate

Exterior drain piping, the mud sump connection, and any shallow drain runs are subject to freezing in Alberta's climate. Drain lines must be sloped to drain completely, and any exterior exposed piping must be insulated or heat-traced. The mud sump exterior location requires consideration for winter maintenance.

ASSUMPTION: Freeze protection design principles apply; specific design to be confirmed by Plumbing Engineer and referenced against applicable codes.

### Emergency Shower Drain and Trap

The emergency shower (SOW-0048; shown on App B-Plumb adjacent to the west bay entrance) requires a floor drain with a trap and connection to the sanitary drain. Emergency shower drains have specific flow rate and sizing considerations — the applicable standard (e.g., ANSI Z358.1 or Canadian equivalent) specifies minimum flow rates that govern drain and trap sizing (see Specification REQ-006-03-026; **specific flow rate TBD**).

**Trap seal maintenance for intermittent-use drains (Lensing item E-001):** The emergency shower drain and any other infrequently used floor drains (e.g., floor drains in seldom-occupied areas of the shop) are susceptible to trap seal evaporation. In an industrial maintenance shop environment, a dried trap seal creates a path for sewer gas intrusion, which is both a health concern and a code violation. The Plumbing Engineer should evaluate the following options and select the appropriate provision:

- **Trap primers** — Automatic devices that periodically add water to the trap to maintain the seal. Preferred for drains with very low use frequency. Requires a water supply connection (coordinate with DEL-006-02 Water Distribution).
- **Deep seal traps** — Traps with a deeper water seal (typically 4" vs. standard 2") that resist evaporation longer. Simpler but less reliable for very long idle periods.
- **Trap seal primer valves** — Installed on nearby water supply lines; prime the trap each time the supply line is used.
- **Administrative controls** — Periodic manual priming by maintenance staff (least reliable; not recommended as sole measure for code compliance).

The selected approach should be documented on the drawings and in the material schedule.

Source: SOW-0048; App B-Plumb. ASSUMPTION: Trap seal maintenance is a code requirement for infrequently used drains; specific code provisions TBD pending code edition confirmation.

### Venting in a High-Bay Industrial Building

The building has a 35' ceiling (RFP §3.4). Vent stacks will penetrate the roof at significant height. Coordination with the structural/architectural roof design for roof penetration locations, flashing, and weatherproofing is needed. The number and diameter of vent stacks will be determined by the plumbing code based on fixture counts and drain layouts.

ASSUMPTION: Vent stack roof penetrations require architectural/structural coordination; specific locations TBD.

### Drawing Set Quality Expectations

**(Lensing item D-003)**

The drain and vent drawing set must meet the quality expectations of two audiences: Camrose County (for preliminary design approval and permit) and the plumbing installer (for construction). The Plumbing Engineer should consider the following quality conventions:

- **Preliminary drawings** should be clearly marked "PRELIMINARY — NOT FOR CONSTRUCTION" and should contain enough information for the County to understand the design intent: floor drain locations, primary drain routing, pipe sizes, vent stack locations, and septic/mud sump connections. Drawing-level detail (e.g., inverts, detailed sections) is not expected at this stage unless County requirements specify otherwise.
- **IFC drawings** should follow standard plumbing engineering drawing conventions: plan views with all pipe sizes, slopes, and invert elevations annotated; isometric diagrams for complex routing; detail sheets for non-standard conditions; material and equipment schedules; general and specific construction notes.
- **Coordination drawings** issued to other disciplines during design should be marked with their purpose (e.g., "FOR COORDINATION — CONFIRM SLEEVE LOCATIONS") and revision-tracked.
- A **drawing content acceptance checklist** should be established before the IFC review (see Specification REQ-006-03-004, Lensing item F-004) to define the minimum content for completeness verification.

ASSUMPTION: Drawing quality conventions are based on standard engineering practice for industrial plumbing drawing sets; County-specific requirements TBD.

### As-Built Drawing Coordination

**(Lensing item E-002)**

The Procedure Records table requires an "As-built survey coordination record" (see Procedure Records table). The Plumbing Engineer should be aware that the following types of as-built differences are common for drain and vent systems in new construction:

- **Drain invert elevations** may differ from design due to field conditions (e.g., encountering rock, utility conflicts, or differing slab thickness).
- **Floor drain positions** may shift slightly from design coordinates due to slab forming and pouring tolerances.
- **Pipe routing** may change in the field to accommodate other trades (ductwork, electrical conduit) discovered during construction.
- **Vent termination locations** may shift due to roofing coordination.

The as-built coordination with the as-built survey (DEL-008-04) should record these differences so that the final record drawings accurately reflect the installed conditions. This is particularly important for drain lines embedded in the concrete slab, which cannot be visually inspected after slab pouring.

ASSUMPTION: As-built coordination is standard practice for construction projects; no specific as-built requirement is stated in the RFP or decomposition beyond DEL-008-04 (As-Built Survey).
