## Purpose

DEL-010-01 Foundation Construction is the physical construction of the building foundation for the New North Shop addition at the West Dried Meat Lake Regional Landfill. It is the enabling work that makes all superstructure construction (PKG-011 and subsequent packages) possible.

This deliverable is unique within the project because it is a **variable-price scope item**: unlike most of the work which carries a firm stipulated price, the foundation cost is negotiated post-geotechnical investigation. This reflects the inherent uncertainty of subsurface conditions at the site prior to investigation.

The purpose of this guidance document is to:
1. Explain why this deliverable is structured the way it is (variable-price, geotech-dependent);
2. Provide principles for managing the associated uncertainty;
3. Identify key trade-offs and coordination points;
4. Flag areas where the General Contractor should exercise judgment and bring issues forward.

Source: RFP §4.8.2; Decomposition §7 PKG-010; Decomposition OBJ-006

## Principles

### P-1: Geotechnical Investigation Gates Everything
The foundation system type, depth, reinforcement, and cost cannot be determined until the geotechnical report from PKG-008 is complete. No foundation design or construction commitments should be made that bypass or pre-empt the geotechnical findings.

**Basis:** RFP §4.8.2 — "Final pricing for the design/construction of the foundation will be negotiated with the successful Proponent when the complete geotechnical report is available."

### P-2: Preliminary Estimate is a Basis-of-Price, Not a Commitment
The cost estimate submitted with the Proposal for foundation work is based on assumed normal ground conditions. It is a pricing anchor for the negotiation, not a fixed obligation. Both the Owner (Camrose County) and the Proponent should treat the preliminary estimate as a reference point.

**Basis:** RFP §4.8.2 — "The Proponent shall provide a cost estimate based on normal ground conditions, without any deleterious subgrade material."

### P-3: Foundation is on the Critical Path
Any delay to geotechnical investigation (PKG-008), foundation design (PKG-002), or foundation construction (this deliverable) directly threatens the December 31, 2026 project completion deadline. The schedule must be planned with sufficient lead time for the geotech-to-design-to-construction sequence.

**Cost-benefit of early geotechnical mobilization:** ASSUMPTION — the foundation scope is variable-price and its final cost is unknown until the geotechnical report is received. Delayed geotechnical mobilization has two compounding cost effects: (1) direct schedule compression forcing overtime or winter construction premium, and (2) reduced negotiation time for the variable-price scope, potentially resulting in less favorable pricing. Early geotechnical mobilization reduces both risks and provides the GC with maximum flexibility for foundation pricing and scheduling. The financial exposure of delayed geotech relative to total project value should be quantified during scheduling; TBD pending project schedule and cost estimate details. (Source: Lensing item A-005)

**Basis:** RFP §3.1; RFP §4.8.2; Decomposition OBJ-002, OBJ-006

### P-4: Quality of Foundation Affects All Downstream Packages
The foundation must be constructed to accommodate the full structural program of the building: 35' concrete structure, two 5-tonne overhead cranes, heavy equipment floor loads, service pit, and wash bay. Deficiencies discovered after superstructure construction begins will be disproportionately costly to remediate.

**Basis:** RFP §3.4; R-04 (App B); Decomposition OBJ-001

### P-5: County Representative Must Be Present at Inspections
This is a contractual requirement, not a courtesy. The General Contractor must coordinate all inspection scheduling with the County project manager and must not proceed through inspection hold points without County confirmation.

**Basis:** RFP §3.3.2

## Considerations

### C-1: Unknown Subgrade Conditions
Alberta's central region can present variable subgrade conditions including weak or compressible soils, frost-susceptible materials, or high water table. Until PKG-008 is complete, the foundation type (e.g., spread footings, grade beams, piles) is unknown.

**Implication:** The General Contractor should anticipate potential scope change post-geotech and maintain contractual flexibility for cost adjustment. If deleterious material is encountered during excavation that was not identified in the geotechnical report, this should be escalated immediately to the Owner.

### C-2: Separation of Foundation from Superstructure Scope
PKG-010 covers foundation only; PKG-011 covers the concrete superstructure including the 35' concrete structure. The interface between foundation and superstructure (anchor bolt placement, holddowns, bearing surfaces) must be precisely coordinated between the foundation construction team and the structural design documents.

**Implication:** The structural drawings (PKG-002) must be reviewed carefully for embedded items before formwork is set. Missed embedments in concrete are extremely difficult to remediate.

**Basis:** Decomposition §6 PKG-010, PKG-011; RFP §3.4

### C-3: County Work vs. Proponent Work Interface
County forces perform bulk cut and fill and clearing. The General Contractor performs topsoil stripping (if not already done), excavation, and foundation construction. The handoff condition of the site from County forces determines the starting baseline for the Proponent's work.

**Implication:** The General Contractor should document the site condition at the time of mobilization (photos, survey) to establish the handoff baseline. Any discrepancy between the assumed start condition and the actual condition should be documented and raised with the County project manager.

**Basis:** RFP §3.3.1, §3.3.2

### C-4: Aggregate Supply by County
The County supplies aggregate for the project. The availability and timing of aggregate delivery may affect foundation construction sequencing.

**Implication:** Confirm aggregate delivery schedule with County project manager early. If aggregate is required under or around the foundation, coordinate delivery timing in the construction schedule.

**Basis:** RFP §3.3.1

### C-5: Service Pit Structural Complexity

**Terminology note:** The RFP and Appendix B use both "service pit" and "service trench" to refer to the same element. This document standardizes on **"service pit"** as the canonical term, with "service trench" noted as an alias. All production documents for DEL-010-01 should use "service pit" consistently. (Source: Lensing item B-003)

The building includes a ventilated and lighted service pit area for under-equipment servicing (Appendix B, RFP §3.4). The service pit is a significant structural element that affects excavation depth, drainage design, and concrete forming complexity.

**Implication:** The service pit structural details (DEL-002-06 in PKG-002) must be incorporated into the foundation construction scope. The General Contractor should confirm whether the service pit is within PKG-010 (foundation) or PKG-011 (superstructure) scope based on the structural drawings. This boundary is currently TBD — see Conflict Table CF-010-01 below and Lensing item X-005 for the full conflict analysis.

**Basis:** RFP §3.4; R-04 (App B); Decomposition DEL-002-06

### C-6: Inspection Requirements and Permit Coordination
All inspections must have the County representative present and copies of inspection reports must be provided. The General Contractor must coordinate inspection scheduling through the County project manager.

**Implication:** Build inspection coordination lead times into the construction schedule. Failure to have County representative present invalidates the inspection for the purposes of this contract.

**Basis:** RFP §3.3.2

### C-7: Seasonal and Weather Constraints on Foundation Construction (ASSUMPTION)

Alberta foundation construction is subject to significant seasonal constraints. The warm construction season (May through October) provides the most favorable conditions for earthwork, concrete placement, and curing. Proceeding with foundation work during winter months (November through March) requires cold-weather protection measures that increase cost and complexity, including heated enclosures, insulated blankets, and hot water for concrete mixing.

**Implication:** The December 31, 2026 project completion deadline means the geotech-to-design-to-construction sequence must be completed with awareness of the construction season window. If foundation construction is pushed into late fall or winter, the General Contractor should plan for cold-weather concrete measures and account for the associated cost premium in the variable-price negotiation. The interaction between seasonal constraints, schedule, and cost should be explicitly addressed during scheduling.

**Basis:** ASSUMPTION — Alberta climate and standard Canadian construction practice; RFP §3.1 (deadline); Lensing item D-002

### C-8: Schedule Contingency Between Foundation Completion and PKG-011

The handoff from PKG-010 (Foundation Construction) to PKG-011 (Building Structure & Envelope) is a critical interface. The schedule contingency (float) between foundation completion and PKG-011 mobilization is TBD. A zero-float handoff exposes the project to cascading delay if any foundation work overruns.

**Implication:** The project schedule should quantify the available float between foundation completion and PKG-011 start. If no float exists, the risk of any foundation delay directly propagates to the superstructure and ultimately threatens the December 31, 2026 deadline. The GC should plan for reasonable contingency and flag the float situation during scheduling.

**Basis:** ASSUMPTION — standard construction scheduling practice; _DEPENDENCIES.md (Downstream: PKG-011); Lensing item C-003

## Trade-offs

### T-1: Speed vs. Geotechnical Certainty
**Trade-off:** The project schedule is tight (completion December 31, 2026). There may be pressure to begin foundation work before the geotechnical report is fully received and incorporated into design.

**Guidance:** Do not commence foundation construction without an approved foundation design based on the geotechnical report. Proceeding without a geotechnical basis exposes the project to significant rework risk and potential structural liability. The schedule risk of waiting for the geotech is preferable to the cost/liability risk of constructing on an uninvestigated subgrade.

**Basis:** RFP §4.8.2; RFP §3.3.2

### T-2: Preliminary Estimate Conservatism vs. Price Competitiveness
**Trade-off:** Proponents must provide a preliminary foundation estimate for the proposal based on normal ground conditions. A conservative estimate may be less competitive; an aggressive estimate may become insufficient if actual conditions are worse than assumed.

**Guidance:** ASSUMPTION — the RFP explicitly provides for variable-price negotiation post-geotech, which limits the downside risk of an aggressive preliminary estimate. However, the preliminary estimate still serves as a negotiation anchor. The General Contractor should document clearly that the estimate is on a normal-conditions basis per §4.8.2.

### T-3: Foundation Scope Boundary at PKG-010/PKG-011 Interface
**Trade-off:** The boundary between foundation work (PKG-010) and superstructure work (PKG-011) is defined in the decomposition but the detailed construction boundary depends on structural drawings not yet available at this stage.

**Guidance:** The structural drawings will clarify the exact interface. Until those documents are available, treat the boundary as TBD and flag to the Owner/project manager if the construction schedule requires this boundary to be resolved before drawings are issued.

### T-4: Foundation Rework vs. Engineered Acceptance

**Trade-off:** When construction deviations are discovered (e.g., as-built dimensions outside tolerance, cylinder break results marginally below specified f'c, misplaced embedded items), the GC must decide whether rework or engineered acceptance is appropriate. Rework is costly and may delay the schedule; engineered acceptance requires structural engineer evaluation and may introduce long-term risk.

**Guidance:** ASSUMPTION — the structural engineer of record is the authority for acceptance of deviations. The GC should:
1. Document all deviations immediately and submit to the structural engineer of record (per Procedure.md Step 19).
2. Request written direction: rework, remediation, or acceptance as-is.
3. Consider the following factors in the decision (for escalation to the structural engineer):
   - Safety: any deviation affecting structural integrity requires rework or engineered remediation.
   - Schedule: if rework delays the critical path to PKG-011, quantify the schedule impact and present to the Owner.
   - Cost: compare rework cost to the cost of engineered acceptance (including any additional analysis, monitoring, or reduced service life).
4. Do not proceed with loading or covering the affected element until the engineer provides written direction.

This decision framework is TBD pending the foundation design; the structural engineer of record should establish deviation acceptance criteria as part of the Foundation Design Package (DEL-002-11).

**Basis:** ASSUMPTION — standard construction quality management practice; Procedure.md Step 19; Lensing item D-004
