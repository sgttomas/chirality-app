# Guidance — DEL-005-01: Preliminary Civil Design

---

## Purpose

DEL-005-01 exists to fulfill the County's requirement that the Design-Builder deliver a preliminary civil design for County project team approval before proceeding to final civil design (RFP §3.3.2; SOW-0010e). This deliverable is a gate: County approval of the preliminary design is a precondition for advancing civil design to IFC.

The preliminary civil design establishes the site grading strategy, drainage approach, driving surface design basis, pad layout, and topsoil stripping plan. It allows the County to review and validate the civil engineering approach early — before detailed engineering effort is committed — reducing the risk of costly late-stage redesign.

Within the broader project objectives, this deliverable directly supports:
- **OBJ-001** (code-compliant, fully functional facility satisfying the County's operational program)
- **OBJ-003** (complete, P.Eng.-stamped IFC design documentation across all disciplines, preceded by County-approved preliminary design)

ASSUMPTION (package-grouping heuristic): OBJ-001 and OBJ-003 are directionally relevant to all PKG-005 deliverables based on the decomposition's objective-to-deliverable mapping for PKG-005.

### What constitutes success from the County's perspective

The County's approval of the preliminary civil design is a binary gate (approve/request revisions), but the evaluation dimensions are TBD. Based on the RFP's emphasis areas (§3.3.2, §3.4), the following dimensions are **ASSUMPTION — inferred, not confirmed**:
- **Drainage confidence:** Demonstrated approach to positive site drainage and neighboring property protection
- **Consistency with Appendix B:** Preliminary layout aligns with the conceptual drawings the County prepared
- **Design basis clarity:** Assumptions, constraints, and TBD items are transparently stated
- **Completeness of scope coverage:** All required elements (grading, pads, driving surface, mud sump, topsoil) are addressed at an appropriate preliminary level

The Civil Engineer should confirm the County's actual evaluation priorities at the mandatory site meeting or project award. (D-004)

## Principles

### Civil design serves construction operability
The site at SW 14–44–21–W4 is an active landfill with heavy equipment operations. Civil design decisions — particularly driving surface strength, turning radii, and grade — must reflect the operational reality of motor scraper class equipment moving through the site daily. A gravel driving surface designed for this equipment class is explicitly required by the RFP (§3.3.2; SOW-0077).

### Drainage is a hard constraint in both directions
The RFP imposes two non-negotiable drainage requirements (§3.4):
1. The site must have positive drainage that can handle future storm events (water must leave the site without pooling).
2. The grading design must not alter drainage conditions for neighboring properties (water must not be redirected onto adjacent lands).

These two requirements must be satisfied simultaneously. Design approaches that achieve one at the expense of the other are not compliant.

### Preliminary design is an approval gate, not a sketch
The preliminary design must be substantive enough for the County to evaluate and approve the civil approach. It is not a concept sketch — it must convey design intent clearly, including grading strategy, drainage approach, and pad/surface layout. The County's project team makes an approval decision based on this submission.

### Scope boundary clarity matters
The civil design scope (PKG-005) is bounded:
- County performs: brushing/clearing, bulk cut and fill, and supplies aggregate/gravel (RFP §3.3.1; SOW-0200, SOW-0201, SOW-0203)
- Proponent performs: cement supply and prep, topsoil stripping from driving surfaces (if not already done by County), gravel driving surface construction, cement and gravel pad construction, grading and landscaping of the lot, mud sump

The preliminary design should clearly identify which site preparation work is County-responsibility so that the civil design does not inadvertently price or specify County-scope items.

## Considerations

### Geotechnical information availability
The preliminary civil design is intended to be developed with reference to available geotechnical and survey data (SOW-0001, SOW-0002). If the geotechnical investigation (PKG-008) is not complete at the time of preliminary civil design, the Civil Engineer must:
- State the geotechnical assumptions underpinning the grading and driving surface design (e.g., assumed subgrade bearing capacity, assumed frost depth for drainage design)
- Identify which design parameters are contingent on the geotech report
- Flag items for confirmation at final design

Geotechnical results may affect: subgrade preparation depth, driving surface section thickness, drainage swale depth and slope, and foundation proximity drainage requirements.

### Alignment with conceptual drawings (Appendix B)
The Appendix B conceptual floor plan (R-04) establishes the spatial context for the civil design:
- New North Shop addition: 130 ft x 100 ft
- Old North Shop (existing): 105 ft x 40 ft
- Gravel Pad North: shown north of the new addition
- Cement Pad West: shown west of the building complex
- Cement Pad East: shown east side (near wash bay)
- Wash Bay Mud Sump: exterior, east side of building
- Overall site complex width: approximately 235 ft

The preliminary civil design should be consistent with these spatial relationships. Where the civil design proposes departures from the conceptual layout, the rationale should be explained in the design narrative for County review.

### Driving surface turning radius for motor scraper class
Motor scrapers are large, articulated machines. The driving surface and turning area design must accommodate their turning radius. Specific turning radius values are TBD pending the Civil Engineer's confirmation of the equipment type(s) in use at the WDMLRL site. The RFP explicitly states this requirement (§3.3.2) but does not specify a minimum turning radius dimension. The Civil Engineer should confirm the design vehicle with the County at or before the mandatory site meeting (March 3, 2026, per RFP §3.2).

### Mud sump design and access
The exterior wash bay mud sump (east side) must be sized and positioned to allow an excavator to access and clean it out (R-01 §3.4; R-04 App B). Civil design implications include: clearance for excavator swing radius and approach, surface drainage toward the sump, and structural considerations for the sump structure (note: structural design may involve PKG-002 coordination for the sump walls/base).

### Mud sump structural coordination with PKG-002 (X-001)
Procedure Step 6.4 identifies coordination with the structural engineer (PKG-002) for the mud sump structure as a coordination point. The civil design must account for the interface between the sump's civil/site works (grading, drainage, excavator access) and its structural design (sump walls, base slab, load-bearing capacity for excavator access). The Civil Engineer should initiate PKG-002 coordination early in the preliminary design process to avoid spatial conflicts between the sump structure and the site grading/drainage design. This coordination is distinct from PKG-007 landscape and PKG-008 geotech coordination.

### Integration with landscape architectural design (PKG-007)
PKG-005 (Civil Design) and PKG-007 (Landscape Architectural Design) share a boundary at the grading/landscaping interface. The decomposition notes that PKG-005 includes civil grading while landscape planting and restoration are in PKG-007 (Decomposition §6, Packages table). The preliminary civil design should establish the graded topography that the landscape architect will work with, and the two packages should coordinate at the interface.

### Timing relative to County approval workflow
The RFP requires all work to be complete by December 31, 2026 (§3.1). Preliminary design approval by the County is a sequencing dependency for final civil design. Early submission and quick County turnaround is in the Design-Builder's interest. The preliminary design should be submitted as early as feasible in the project schedule.

### Frost penetration depth assumption basis (F-002)
The Procedure (Step 2.2) assumes a frost penetration depth of approximately 1.5–2.0 m for Alberta climate conditions. This is a conservative assumption based on general Alberta engineering practice for central Alberta locations. The range is approximate and is not sourced from a site-specific geotechnical investigation. The defensibility of this assumption rests on:
- Central Alberta climate zone frost depth data (general engineering knowledge)
- Conservative end of the range (2.0 m) used as the design basis until confirmed by site-specific geotech data from PKG-008

The Civil Engineer should confirm the local frost depth value when the geotechnical report becomes available and adjust the design basis at final design stage accordingly.

## Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Options | Considerations | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grading approach without full geotech data | (A) Proceed with conservative assumed parameters; (B) Wait for geotech report | Option A allows earlier County approval and schedule advancement; assumptions must be clearly stated and confirmed at final design. Option B is lower risk but delays the approval gate. **Cost/schedule impact (F-004):** The schedule impact of Option B (waiting for geotech) is TBD — the Project Manager or Civil Engineer should estimate the delay in weeks and the cost impact of deferred County approval on downstream deliverables (DEL-005-02 through DEL-005-07) and construction packages (PKG-018). This quantification is needed to make a defensible choice between Option A and Option B. | SOW-0001, SOW-0002; schedule constraint §3.1 |
| Drainage design — swales vs. piped | (A) Open drainage swales; (B) Catch basins and piped system | Rural landfill context and motor scraper operations suggest open swales may be more appropriate and maintainable; however, site-specific grading constraints may favor piped drainage in some areas. **Rationale gap (A-005):** The preference for open swales over piped drainage in a landfill context has not been fully evaluated. Factors that may support open swales include: lower maintenance complexity with heavy equipment operations, compatibility with gravel surface grading, reduced subsurface infrastructure in an active landfill area, and easier inspection. Factors favoring piped drainage in specific areas include: space constraints near the building, concentrated flows, and frost depth considerations. The Civil Engineer should determine the appropriate approach based on site survey data and confirm at final design. | R-01 §3.4; SOW-0020 |
| Mud sump siting — east side fixed vs. flexible | Fixed at east side per App B | App B conceptual drawing locates mud sump at east exterior of wash bay. Departures require County discussion. | R-04 App B |
| Topsoil stripping extent | Strip all driving surface areas vs. targeted stripping | RFP requires stripping from all driving surfaces. County may have already performed some stripping. Civil design should identify the extent requiring Proponent action and coordinate with County at project award. | R-01 §3.3.2; SOW-0075 |
