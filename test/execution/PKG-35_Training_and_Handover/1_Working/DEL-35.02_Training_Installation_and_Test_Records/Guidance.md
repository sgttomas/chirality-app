# Guidance: DEL-35.02 Training Installation & Test Records

## Purpose

This guidance document supports the development of **Training Installation & Test Records** for **PKG-35 Training & Handover**.

**Deliverable purpose:** Provides evidence of completion, inspection, and testing for training activities.

*Source: _CONTEXT.md, Decomposition Section 5 (PKG-35, DEL-35.02)*

**Deliverable classification:**
- **Type**: Record
- **Discipline**: Project Delivery
- **Responsible party**: D&B Contractor (with OEM/Supplier)

*Source: _CONTEXT.md*

**Project context:**
- **Project**: Canola Oil Transload Facility — Phase 1 (Design & Build)
- **Employer**: DP World Fraser Surrey Inc.
- **Location**: Fraser Surrey Terminal, 11060 Elevator Road, Surrey, BC
- **Facility capacity**: 1,000,000 MT/annum throughput, 45,000 MT storage

*Source: Decomposition Section 1 (Project Context)*

## Principles

### Core Rationale

**Principle 1: Evidence of Competent Workforce**

Training records serve as legal and contractual evidence that facility personnel have been trained and assessed as competent to operate and maintain the facility safely and effectively.

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — fundamental purpose of training records*

**Why training records are critical:**
- **Contractual obligation**: Design & Build contracts typically require delivery of trained operations team
- **Safety assurance**: Demonstrates compliance with occupational health and safety requirements for worker competence
- **Regulatory compliance**: Many jurisdictions require documented evidence of training for regulated activities
- **Risk mitigation**: Reduces Contractor liability by demonstrating due diligence in workforce preparation
- **Handover acceptance**: Training record completeness is often an acceptance criterion for facility handover (cross-reference DEL-35.03)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — typical rationale for training record requirements*

**Principle 2: Support for Lifecycle Cost Optimization (OBJ-9)**

Training records directly support **OBJ-9: Lifecycle Cost Optimization** by providing evidence that the Employer's operations team is competent, reducing future operational costs.

*Source: Decomposition Section 2 (OBJ-9), Section 6 (Objective-to-Deliverable Mapping showing DEL-35.01–DEL-35.05 support OBJ-9)*

**How training records contribute to lifecycle cost optimization:**
- **Reduces operational errors**: Competency-verified personnel make fewer costly mistakes
- **Reduces insurance costs**: Some insurers offer lower premiums for facilities with documented training programs
- **Reduces re-training costs**: Comprehensive initial training reduces need for remedial training post-handover
- **Supports future training**: Records provide baseline for future workforce planning and refresher training
- **Enables knowledge retention**: When personnel turnover occurs, training records help identify training gaps for new hires

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — rationale for how training records support OBJ-9*
*See Specification.md requirement PR-04 for specific performance criteria.*

**Principle 3: Safe and Reliable Operation (OBJ-1)**

Training records support **OBJ-1: Safe & Reliable Operation** by ensuring personnel competence is verified and documented.

*Source: Decomposition Section 2 (OBJ-1)*

**Safety and reliability implications:**
- Competency assessments demonstrate personnel can perform tasks safely
- Emergency response training records demonstrate readiness for incident response
- Training records support safety audits and regulatory inspections
- Training records identify any personnel with outstanding training requirements before operations commence

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — safety principles for training records*

**Principle 4: Traceability and Auditability**

Training records must enable complete traceability:
- Who was trained (personnel records)
- What training they received (training modules, cross-reference DEL-35.01)
- When training occurred (dates)
- Who delivered training (instructor qualifications)
- How competency was assessed (assessment method and results)
- Where training occurred (location, facility, or classroom)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — fundamental principle for training record management*

**Rationale for traceability:**
- Regulatory audits may require proof of specific training for specific personnel
- Incident investigations may examine whether involved personnel were properly trained
- Personnel disputes may require verification of training and competency
- Refresher training planning requires knowledge of when initial training occurred

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — typical use cases for training record traceability*

## Considerations

### Factors to Consider During Record Development

**Consideration 1: Record Format and Medium**

**Competing factors:**
- **Paper records**: Traditional, familiar, signed originals provide legal weight
- **Electronic records**: Easier to search, organize, and archive; but require electronic signature solution

**Decision factors:**
- Project document management system capabilities (electronic forms, workflows, e-signatures)
- Employer preferences and post-handover record management systems
- Regulatory requirements (some jurisdictions specify acceptable formats)
- Number of personnel to be trained (large training programs benefit from electronic records for manageability)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — typical format decision factors*

**Recommended approach:**
- Use paper forms during training (instructor and trainee sign physical form)
- Scan completed forms to searchable PDF for archival and handover
- Organize electronic copies in logical folder structure (by trainee, by module, by date)
- Provide both electronic and limited printed copies at handover (per contract requirements)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — practical approach balancing legal weight of signatures with electronic archival benefits*

**Consideration 2: Competency Assessment Rigor**

**Trade-off: Assessment rigor vs. training schedule constraints**

Competency assessments can range from simple knowledge checks to extensive practical evaluations:
- **Written tests**: Assess knowledge; quick to administer; but don't verify practical skill
- **Practical demonstrations**: Verify hands-on competence; but time-intensive and require facility/equipment access
- **Simulator exercises**: Good for emergency scenarios; but require simulator availability
- **On-the-job evaluations**: Most realistic; but occur post-handover (outside Contractor scope)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — typical assessment methods*

**Decision factors:**
- Criticality of competency (safety-critical tasks need rigorous assessment)
- Time available before commissioning (schedule constraints)
- Access to facility and equipment (for practical demonstrations)
- Employer expectations and regulatory requirements

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — factors affecting assessment rigor*

**Recommendation:**
- Safety-critical competencies: Practical demonstrations required (e.g., emergency shutdown, spill response, confined space entry)
- Operational procedures: Written tests acceptable for knowledge, practical demonstrations during commissioning for skill verification
- General awareness topics: Written tests or attendance-only (for orientation topics)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — risk-based approach to assessment rigor; see Specification.md FR-02 for assessment documentation requirements*

**Consideration 3: Timing of Training and Record Creation**

Training records are created during pre-commissioning and commissioning phases, which presents challenges:

**Timing considerations:**
- **Early training (pre-commissioning)**: Allows time for remedial training if competency gaps identified; but trainees may forget material before facility startup
- **Late training (during commissioning)**: More relevant and timely; but risks inadequate time for remedial training if issues arise
- **Progressive training**: Classroom training early, hands-on training during commissioning; spreads training over time

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — typical training timing challenges*

**Recommended approach:**
- Foundational classroom training during late construction phase
- Hands-on training during pre-commissioning and commissioning (when equipment available)
- Competency assessments immediately following training (while knowledge is fresh)
- Refresher sessions immediately before startup (if significant gap between training and operations)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — practical training schedule approach*

**Consideration 4: Integration with Other Deliverables**

Training records must integrate with several other deliverables:

**Upstream dependencies:**
- **DEL-35.01 (Training Materials)**: Training cannot occur without training materials; records must reference training material revisions used
  *Cross-reference DEL-35.01*

**Downstream dependencies:**
- **DEL-35.03 (Handover Documentation)**: Training records are key component of handover; completeness is often handover acceptance criterion
  *Cross-reference DEL-35.03*

**Related deliverables:**
- **PKG-32 (Health, Safety & Environmental Management)**: Training records demonstrate safety training compliance
- **PKG-33 (Emergency Response Planning)**: Training records demonstrate emergency response team readiness

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** based on package scope; see _DEPENDENCIES.md for coordination mode (NOT_TRACKED)*

**Coordination challenges:**
- Training materials may still be under revision during training delivery; manage version control carefully
- Handover may be delayed if training records incomplete; prioritize record completion for critical path

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — typical coordination challenges*

**Consideration 5: OEM-Provided Training**

When OEMs provide training (common for specialized equipment), coordination is required:

**OEM training considerations:**
- OEM training materials may differ in format from Contractor training materials (DEL-35.01)
- OEM training records may differ in format from Contractor training records
- OEM instructors may not be familiar with project record management procedures
- OEM training certificates may be provided instead of detailed attendance/competency records

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** based on _CONTEXT.md (Responsible: D&B Contractor with OEM/Supplier)*

**Recommended approach:**
- Provide OEM with template training record forms (attendance and competency) aligned with project requirements
- Request OEM training materials in advance for inclusion in DEL-35.01 package
- Contractor training coordinator attends OEM training sessions to verify record completion
- Incorporate OEM training certificates into overall training record package with supplementary attendance records if needed

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — practical approach to OEM training coordination*

**Consideration 6: Personnel Turnover During Project**

If operations personnel turnover occurs between training and facility startup, training records may become obsolete:

**Turnover risk:**
- Personnel trained during project may not be employed by Employer at startup
- New personnel require training, but training materials may no longer be in active use
- Training records may show "trained workforce" but actual startup team is different

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — common risk for long-duration projects*

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Train Employer's core operations management team early (less turnover at management level)
- Provide Employer with training materials (DEL-35.01) and competency assessment tools for post-handover training of new hires
- Include provision for refresher/makeup training in defects liability support (cross-reference DEL-35.05)
- **TBD** — Clarify Contractor's responsibility for training personnel hired post-handover but pre-startup

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — typical mitigation strategies; responsibility clarification TBD*

**Consideration 7: Confidentiality and Privacy**

Training records contain personal information (names, assessment results) and must be managed per privacy regulations:

**Privacy considerations:**
- Canadian privacy legislation (PIPEDA federally, PIPA in BC) governs personal information handling
- Trainees may have right to access their own training records
- Training records should not be disclosed without authorization (except where required by law)
- Assessment failures should be handled sensitively (confidential feedback to trainee)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — Canadian privacy law considerations; specific requirements TBD*

**Recommended approach:**
- Include privacy notice on training record forms
- Restrict access to training records (need-to-know basis)
- Provide confidentiality notice when transferring records to Employer at handover
- Ensure Employer is aware of privacy obligations when managing post-handover training records

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — practical privacy protection measures; see Specification.md FR-05 for confidentiality requirements*

## Trade-offs

### Competing Concerns to Evaluate

**Trade-off 1: Comprehensiveness vs. Practicality**

**Competing concerns:**
- **Comprehensiveness**: Detailed training records provide strong evidence of competency and support audit requirements
- **Practicality**: Overly detailed records are time-consuming to complete and manage, burdening trainers and administrators

**Evaluation factors:**
- Regulatory requirements (minimum information required)
- Employer expectations (level of detail desired)
- Project schedule (time available for record completion)
- Risk level (higher-risk operations justify more detailed records)

**Recommended approach:**
- Define minimum required information per Specification.md FR-01 and FR-02
- Use standard forms with checkboxes and fields to streamline completion
- Avoid free-form narrative records (difficult to verify completeness)
- Focus detail on competency assessment results (most critical information)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — typical trade-off in training record design; see Specification.md for minimum requirements*

**Trade-off 2: Real-Time Record Completion vs. Batch Processing**

**Competing concerns:**
- **Real-time completion**: Records completed immediately following training/assessment (more accurate, less risk of lost data)
- **Batch processing**: Records completed in batches at end of training program (more efficient, allows for quality review before finalization)

**Evaluation factors:**
- Accuracy risk (delay increases risk of errors or missing information)
- Efficiency (batch processing reduces administrative burden)
- Schedule pressure (real-time completion ensures no last-minute record backlog before handover)

**Recommended approach:**
- Attendance records completed real-time (signed at end of each training session)
- Competency assessment records completed real-time (signed immediately following assessment)
- Record compilation and indexing done in batches (weekly or milestone-based)
- Final quality review of complete record package before handover submittal

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — practical approach balancing accuracy and efficiency*

**Trade-off 3: Paper vs. Electronic Records**

**Competing concerns:**
- **Paper records**: Original signatures provide legal weight; familiar process; but difficult to search and archive
- **Electronic records**: Easy to search, organize, and share; but require electronic signature infrastructure

**Evaluation factors:**
- Legal acceptance of electronic signatures (jurisdiction-dependent)
- Project document management system capabilities
- Employer's post-handover record management system preferences
- Number of trainees (larger programs benefit more from electronic records)

**Recommended approach** (reiterated from Consideration 1):
- Capture signatures on paper forms during training
- Scan to searchable PDF for electronic archival and handover
- Provide electronic copies as primary handover medium (searchable, indexed)
- Retain paper originals for warranty/defects liability period if legal weight of original signatures is important

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — practical compromise*

**Trade-off 4: Pass/Fail vs. Graded Competency Assessment**

**Competing concerns:**
- **Pass/Fail**: Simple, clear, sufficient for demonstrating competency; but provides limited information for performance management
- **Graded assessment**: Provides more information (e.g., 90% score, "exceeds expectations"); useful for identifying high performers and areas for improvement; but more complex to administer and interpret

**Evaluation factors:**
- Purpose of assessment (handover evidence vs. ongoing performance management)
- Employer preferences (some organizations use competency grades for personnel development)
- Regulatory requirements (typically only require pass/fail evidence)

**Recommended approach:**
- Use pass/fail for safety-critical competencies (clear, unambiguous)
- Optionally provide scores/grades for complex assessments where additional detail is useful
- Ensure minimum pass criteria are clearly defined and documented
- Focus on demonstrating "competent" vs. "not yet competent" rather than relative ranking

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — typical competency assessment approach; see Specification.md FR-02 for assessment result documentation*

### Lessons Learned and Best Practices

**Best Practice 1: Standardize Record Forms Early**

Develop and approve training record forms (attendance, competency assessment) early in the project:
- Reduces inconsistency in record quality
- Streamlines record completion (trainers know what to fill out)
- Facilitates Employer review and approval of form templates before use
- Easier to compile and verify complete records at handover

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — lesson learned from training record management challenges*

**Best Practice 2: Assign Dedicated Training Coordinator**

Designate one person as training coordinator responsible for:
- Scheduling training sessions
- Coordinating with instructors/trainers
- Ensuring attendance records are completed after each session
- Tracking competency assessments and follow-ups
- Maintaining training record files
- Compiling final training record package for handover

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — best practice for training program management*

**Best Practice 3: Real-Time Quality Checks**

Implement quality checks during training delivery, not just at the end:
- Review attendance records immediately after each session (check for missing signatures, incomplete fields)
- Review competency assessment records immediately after assessments
- Address gaps immediately (easier to correct when trainer/trainee are still available)
- Avoids last-minute scramble before handover

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — lesson learned from record quality issues*

**Best Practice 4: Pilot Test Record Forms**

Before full training program rollout, pilot test record forms with first training session:
- Identify unclear fields or instructions
- Identify missing information
- Get feedback from trainers and trainees on usability
- Revise forms before wider use

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — quality assurance best practice*

**Risk to Avoid: Incomplete Records at Handover**

Incomplete training records can delay handover acceptance:
- Missing signatures are most common issue (trainee or instructor forgot to sign)
- Missing competency assessments (training delivered but assessment not documented)
- Missing training material cross-references (can't trace which material version was used)

**Mitigation**:
- Real-time completion and quality checks (per Best Practice 3 above)
- Checklist-driven record compilation (verify all required records present)
- Early Employer engagement (submit sample records for review before full program delivery)

*Source: **ASSUMPTION** — common risk for training records; aligns with Specification.md verification requirements*
